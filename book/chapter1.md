# Chapter 1: The Glitch in the 800

The tragedy of my existence isn’t that I am remarkably unlucky. I wish I was unlucky; at least bad luck has a degree of flair to it. No, my tragedy is that I am aggressively average.

My name is Rahul Sharma. I am thirty-four years old, and I am a Senior Software Engineer at a mid-tier IT firm in Noida. I’ve held this exact title for six agonizing years without so much as a sympathy promotion. If my life were a movie, I wouldn’t even be the supporting character; I’d be the guy in the background of the coffee shop scene, staring blankly at a laptop, out of focus.

I am a solid 6/10 on my best days. My height is an unthreatening 5'8", my hairline is staging a slow, highly organized tactical retreat, and my entire wardrobe consists of promotional tech-conference t-shirts from startups that went bankrupt during the pandemic. I live in a cramped, eternally damp 2BHK in Mayur Vihar. My diet is primarily instant noodles, tepid office coffee, and a distinct, lingering sense of regret.

But the absolute zenith of my daily suffering is my boss, Sumit.

Sumit is a man whose entire personality is built around aggressive micro-management wrapped in passive-aggressive corporate jargon. He doesn't just manage you; he emotionally waterboards you. He possesses this terrifying, supernatural ability to compliment me while simultaneously torpedoing my self-esteem.

Just this morning, the horror began at exactly 10:14 AM. 

I was staring at a block of legacy payment gateway code, contemplating whether it would be faster to rewrite it or just set my monitor on fire, when a heavy hand clapped down on my shoulder. I smelled him before I saw him—a noxious cloud of 'Mountain Frost' deodorant and aggressively chewed cardamom mouth freshener.

"Rahul, buddy, superstar, ninja," Sumit said, leaning over my cubicle partition so far that his tie dipped into my empty coffee mug. 

"Hi, Sumit," I sighed, not taking my eyes off the IDE. 

"I reviewed your code for the payment gateway module last night," Sumit continued, his voice dripping with synthetic enthusiasm. "Beautiful. Absolutely poetic. It compiles without screaming, which is a rare, rare treat on a Tuesday. The way you nested those loops? It’s like watching an artisan weave a basket. A very basic, functioning basket."

"Thanks, Sumit," I mumbled. I knew the trap was coming, but I stepped into it anyway. "So, actually, about the Lead Developer position that opened up last week—"

Sumit's smile widened into a grimace of profound, weaponized empathy. He pulled up a spare chair and sat down backward, straddling it like a cool youth pastor about to drop some hard truths.

"Ah, the Lead position. Look, Rahul. Let’s talk about ships. You are the engine room of this corporate ship. You’re the guy down in the dark, shoveling the coal, getting callouses on your hands. If I take my absolute best coal-shoveler and put him up on the bright, sunny captain's deck to look through the fancy binoculars… who is going to shovel the coal?"

I stared at him blankly. "Someone newly hired? Perhaps an intern?"

Sumit chuckled, shaking his head as if I had just suggested we invest in Beanie Babies. "No, Rahul. The ship sinks. You don't want the ship to sink, do you? Because if the ship sinks, the coal shoveler drowns first."

"Is that a threat?"

"It’s a metaphor, champ! You’re a builder, a craftsman, a solid… implementer. You lack the 'visionary synergy' for a Lead role. You don't think outside the box; you excel at sitting quietly inside the box and making sure the box’s structural integrity is sound. Stick to the code, buddy. Let the eagles soar while the badgers dig."

"Badgers?" I asked, my voice cracking slightly. "Did you just call me a badger?"

But the cool youth pastor act was over. Sumit stood up, tapped on my desk twice, and moonwalked away. Literally moonwalked. "We'll circle back on this in Q3! Keep digging, badger!"

***

"The man called me a badger, Kabir. A *badger*." 

It was 4:00 PM. I was standing by the designated tapri (tea stall) outside our office park, clutching a scalding paper cup of ginger tea like it was a lifeline. 

My best friend, Kabir, took a slow drag of his cigarette. Kabir was a UX designer whose entire aesthetic could be described as 'urban lumberjack who has never chopped wood.' He wore flannel in the Delhi summer and paid Rs. 3000 for haircuts that made him look like he hadn’t had a haircut in months. He was also my designated emotional support human.

"What does that even mean?" I demanded, blowing on the tea. "Is that a corporate acronym? B.A.D.G.E.R? Boring And Dumb Geek Easily Replaced?"

"It means you’re stuck, bro," Kabir said, exhaling a thin stream of smoke. "Sumit is gatekeeping you. If you get promoted, he actually has to do his own job, and we all know Sumit’s actual job is just forwarding emails and taking credit in PowerPoint presentations. But listen, forget Sumit. Let’s talk about the real crisis. You have a date tonight."

I groaned, leaning against the rusty metal shutter of a closed shop. "Don't remind me."

"Neha from Hinge, right? The marketing executive? The one who actually swiped right and didn't immediately unmatch when you made that terrible joke about SQL injection?" 

"Yes, Neha," I said, dread Pooling in my stomach. "We are going to that ridiculously expensive fusion pan-Asian place in Cyber Hub. The one where they serve sushi on actual rocks. But there is a massive problem, Kabir. My bike is dead."

Kabir raised an eyebrow. "Dead dead, or just Rahul-forgot-to-fill-petrol dead?"

"The battery completely flatlined this morning, and the kick-start is jammed. I tried pushing it, and I think I slipped a disc. How do I pick her up? If I show up outside her apartment in a noisy, vibrating auto-rickshaw smelling of CNG, she’s going to ghost me before the appetizers even arrive."

"Rent an Uber?" Kabir suggested lightly. 

"She literally lives in one of those fortress gated societies in Gurgaon. The ones that don't let cabs past the main gate without calling the residents, taking a retinal scan, an OTP, a blood sample, and a letter of recommendation from the local MLA. If I take a cab, I have to make her walk half a kilometer to the main gate. I need a car, Kabir. I need to look like a fully formed, independently mobile adult male."

Kabir smirked, crushing his cigarette under his boot. "Well. You don't have a car. But you do have access to a street-legal spaceship."

I shuddered. My blood ran cold. "No. Absolutely not."

"Think about it, Rahul—"

"I said no! I am not taking Uncle Jaggi’s Maruti."

Uncle Jaggi was my maternal uncle, a man of terrifying eccentricity who lived in the ground-floor flat beneath mine. Jaggi was a retired electrical engineer from the Railways who spent his pension, and every waking hour, locked inside his garage, tinkering. 

Specifically, he tinkered with a 1999 Maruti 800. 

To call that thing a 'car' was an insult to the entire history of the automobile industry. It was a rolling Frankenstein's monster of aftermarket parts, discarded 90s electronics, and highly questionable wiring. Uncle Jaggi was obsessed with it. He washed it twice a day, spoke to it in dulcet Punjabi tones, and had slowly replaced its internal organs with whatever he sourced at the Nehru Place electronic junk markets. 

There was a secret about Uncle Jaggi that nobody in the family talked about, mostly because they stopped listening to him after the first five minutes of any conversation. Jaggi firmly believed that the principles of theoretical physics were vastly overcomplicated by modern scientists and could be easily bypassed using adequate amounts of duct tape, copper coils, and high-speed Jio broadband. For ten years, he had been attempting to build something in that garage that he vaguely referred to as the 'continuum project.'

"I can't take the Maruti," I pleaded with Kabir. "It’s painted matte black with spray paint. It looks like a toaster mated with a Decepticon."

"Bro, it's retro," Kabir argued, thoroughly enjoying my misery. "Chicks dig retro. Plus, it’s highly modified. It looks... geeky. Tell her you're ironically reviving vintage Indian iconography to protest the homogenization of modern automotive design."

I blinked. "That is the most pretentious, meaningless sentence I have ever heard."

"Exactly. Marketing executives eat that stuff up. Go ask Uncle Jaggi for the keys."

***

At 7:00 PM, I swallowed my pride, walked down the stairs of my building, and stood in front of Uncle Jaggi’s open garage. 

The Maruti 800 sat under the flickering fluorescent lights, looking absolutely absurd. The front grille had been replaced with what looked like the cooling fan of an industrial air conditioner. The dashboard had been completely ripped out and rebuilt to resemble the control panel of a Soviet submarine. Two Amazon Alexa devices were physically hot-glued to the AC vents. A JioFiber router was bolted to the passenger side footwell, its green lights blinking menacingly in the shadows. 

"Uncle Jaggi?" I called out tentatively. 

A loud clatter echoed from beneath the chassis. A moment later, Jaggi emerged on a mechanic's creeper, covered head-to-toe in black engine grease and proudly holding a soldering iron. He was wearing heavy welding goggles over his regular spectacles. 

"Ah, Rahul beta!" he beamed, showing a row of tobacco-stained teeth. "You chose exactly the right moment to come down! Do you want to see the flux-capacitor matrix I just synced with the alternator? I finally managed to stabilize the tachyonic feedback loop using a spare motherboard from a Dell Inspiron!"

"That sounds... great, Uncle. But no, I actually came down to ask a favor. Can I borrow the car for tonight? Just for a few hours. I have a date."

Jaggi froze. He lowered the soldering iron slowly. He stared at me, then up at the car, then back at me. He pushed his welding goggles up to his forehead.

"A date?" Jaggi asked, as if I had just spoken to him in fluent Klingon. "A romantic engagement with a female woman?"

"Yes, Uncle. It’s an emergency. My bike is completely dead, and I really need to make a good impression."

Jaggi wiped his hands on a greasy rag, his eyes narrowing defensively as he stood up. "Rahul, this is not a regular vehicle anymore. It is a highly sensitive chronal-displacement prototype. I am currently in the middle of calibrating the quantum manifold. If the alignment is off by even a micro-millimeter, the consequences could be disastrous."

"Uncle, please," I begged, putting my hands together. "I just need to drive to Cyber Hub and back. It’s a 45-minute drive tops. I promise I won't go over 40 km/ph. I won't touch any of the weird buttons. I won't even use the AC."

Jaggi sighed heavily, the tremendous weight of a brilliant, misunderstood scientist slumping his shoulders. He reached deep into the pockets of his grease-stained overalls and pulled out a key. Except, it wasn't a normal car key. It was a bulky, glowing USB drive with a small metal key protruding from the end. 

"Fine," Jaggi whispered, looking at the key like it was his firstborn child. "But you must listen to me very carefully, Rahul. Do NOT, under any circumstances, touch the red toggle switch near the handbrake. That engages the particle accelerator."

"Got it. No red switches."

"And whatever you do, do not try to sync the dual-Alexa nodes while the car engine is operating above 3000 RPM. The local chronal field is intensely unstable right now. The routing algorithm is still buggy."

"Yes, yes, no red switches, no shouting at Alexa, keep RPM low. Got it. Thank you so much, Uncle!" I snatched the keys, practically running to the driver's side door before he could change his mind.

I yanked the heavy, lead-lined door open and squeezed into the driver's seat. It smelled intensely of soldering wire and old pine air fresheners. 

"Rahul!" Jaggi yelled as I put the key in the ignition. 

I turned the key. The engine didn't purr; it shrieked. It sounded less like a combustion engine and more like a jet turbine having a severe asthma attack. 

"What?!" I yelled back over the deafening noise.

"If the center console flashes neon blue, pull over immediately and ground yourself by touching soil!" Jaggi screamed over the roar of the exhaust. 

"Okay, Uncle! Love you!" I shouted, slamming the door shut. 

I put the car in gear. The clutch was absurdly stiff. I lurched out of the garage, hopping like a kangaroo down the driveway, much to the amusement of the neighborhood watch guard. 

The drive toward Gurugram was, frankly, terrifying. The steering wheel buzzed constantly with low-frequency electricity, leaving my palms numb. Every time I applied the brakes, one of the Alexas glued to the AC vent would loudly announce the current price of Bitcoin. But as I merged onto the Delhi-Gurgaon expressway, I relaxed a fraction. Kabir was somewhat right; inside the dark, neon-lit cabin of this mechanical beast, I felt less like an IT badger and more like a cyberpunk protagonist.

I hit the NH-8 highway. Traffic was surprisingly light for a Friday evening. 

My phone buzzed in my pocket. I managed to pull it out at a red light. It was a text from Neha: *Hey! Almost ready. What's your ETA?*

I realized I needed to call her. I needed to verbally explain that I was pulling up to her upscale apartment complex in a loudly vibrating, matte-black 1999 mechanical abomination, just so she wouldn't immediately call the police when I arrived. 

I looked at the dashboard. There, taped beneath the stereo system, was a crude, backlit button labeled 'BLUETOOTH SYNC / ORBITAL LINK' in Uncle Jaggi's messy handwriting. 

"Well, how complicated can it be?" I muttered to myself. I pressed the button.

Immediately, the JioFiber router in the passenger footwell began to scream. It wasn't a low hum; it was a high-pitched, 90s dial-up modem screech that rattled my teeth and made my ears pop. Both of the Alexas abruptly lit up, their rings glowing blindingly neon-blue. 

"Syncing device," Alexa 1 announced in its calming corporate voice.
"Warning. Chronal matrix destabilized. Temporal anchor detached," Alexa 2 announced simultaneously, sounding panicked.

"Wait, what?" I yelled, my heart leaping into my throat. I frantically mashed the Bluetooth button again. "Cancel! Hey Alexa, stop! Cancel sync!"

"Connecting to: NEHA_IPHONE," Alexa 1 said. 
"Setting destination coordinates to: 3200 BCE, Coordinates: 29.17° N, 78.02° E," Alexa 2 countered. "Location Designation: Hastinapur."

"What the hell is a Hastinapur?!" I screamed, slamming my foot onto the brake pedal. 

But the brakes didn't engage. Instead, the accelerator pedal depressed itself completely to the floorboards. The Maruti 800 surged forward with the force of a rocket launch. The speedometer needle snapped right past its maximum limit of 140, wrapping all the way around the dial, and just kept spinning. 

The world outside the windshield smeared. The neon lights of the NH-8 toll plaza stretched into infinite, blinding white beams. The asphalt dissolved into a violent spectrum of swirling colors. I was violently thrown back into my seat, pinned by a G-force that felt like an elephant sitting directly on my chest. The air inside the cabin instantly tasted like burnt copper, ozone, and sheer panic. 

I couldn't breathe. I tried to scream, but the air was sucked out of my lungs. 

Through watering eyes, I watched in absolute, paralyzed horror as the digital clock on the dashboard—which usually displayed the current time—started rolling backward like a slot machine. 

*2026... 1990... 1400... 0500... 0000... -0500... -2000...*

And then, amidst the terrifying roar of the space-time continuum tearing apart... my phone connected. The Bluetooth successfully paired. 

Through the groaning, unnatural physics of a wormhole, Neha’s voice echoed out of the vehicle's heavily modded surround-sound speakers. 

"Hey Rahul?" Neha said, sounding perfectly relaxed. "Are you nearby? I’m waiting down near the society gates."

"NEHA! MY CAR IS EATING TIME!" I shrieked, my voice cracking an octave higher. "DO NOT ORDER THE APPETIZERS! I THINK I AM IN A WORMHOLE!"

"Wait, you're in a what? Is that the radio—?" 

Her voice was swallowed whole by an earsplitting thunderclap. 

The Maruti 800 materialized out of thin air, dropping three feet with a bone-jarring *CRACK* before slamming nose-deep into something soft, dry, and incredibly dusty. 

The airbags did not deploy, mostly because, as I dimly recalled, Uncle Jaggi had replaced the airbag matrix with additional cooling fans for his servers. My head snapped forward, bouncing off the steering wheel. 

I blacked out for what must have been seconds. I was slowly lulled back to consciousness by the gentle, corporate voice of Alexa 1 declaring: "You have arrived at your destination. Turn left to avoid the incoming spear."

I groaned loudly, peeling my sweaty forehead off the steering wheel. I was intensely dizzy, thoroughly nauseous, and my cheap unbranded cologne had been completely overpowered by the smell of ionized radiation. 

I wiped my eyes and looked out the windshield. 

I was not on NH-8. I was nowhere near Gurugram's Cyber Hub. 

The Maruti was wedged nose-first into a colossal mound of golden hay. Beyond the hay, the landscape was deeply, terrifyingly wrong. There were no skyscrapers towering in the distance. There was no smog choking the horizon. There were no paved roads. The sky was an offensive, vivid, utterly unpolluted blue that I hadn't seen since a screensaver in 2004. 

I pushed the heavy car door open and stumbled out onto the soft earth, my legs feeling entirely like jelly. I was standing on the dirty outskirts of a massive, sprawling settlement. It didn't look like a city; it looked like an oversized medieval encampment built out of incredibly refined timber, carved stone, and an absurd amount of gold. Vibrant banners bearing an unrecognizable, ancient crest fluttered in the warm wind.

But it was the people that made my brain completely short-circuit. 

Hundreds of people were streaming past the haystack, all heading down a wide dirt avenue towards an impossibly large, circular amphitheater in the distance. They were dressed in what I could only describe as high-budget, historically inaccurate Bollywood-mythology cosplay. Men were wearing pristine silk dhotis, ornate armor plates strapped across their chests, and carrying actual, functional, pointy spears. Women were adorned in heavy gold jewelry, brightly colored draped garments, and intricate, towering hairstyles. 

Nobody was looking at a smartphone. Nobody was honking a horn. There was not a single Zomato delivery guy in sight. 

I looked down at myself. I was wearing faded blue Levi's jeans, a black graphic t-shirt that read "I AM HERE BECAUSE YOU BROKE SOMETHING", and bright white Puma sneakers. 

A group of sturdy-looking men, carrying what looked like massive bronze offering plates, paused in their march to stare at me. One of them, a man with shoulders as wide as a refrigerator, pointed a thick, calloused finger directly at my chest.

"By the gods," the man proclaimed loudly. And here was the weirdest part—he spoke in an ancient, localized dialect that my brain somehow instantly auto-translated into modern Hindi, likely another horrifying side effect of the ambient temporal radiation emitting from the car. "What sort of demon-cloth covers your legs, fool? It is tighter than a second skin! Do your calves not scream for air?"

"Uh. They’re jeans," I stammered, raising my hands harmlessly. "Slim fit. Listen, bro. I think my GPS really glitched out. Like, severely. Is this a film set? Are you guys shooting a movie? Where's the director?"

Another man in the group let out a booming, hearty laugh. "Look at him! He speaks the gibberish of the foreign lands, and wears the garb of a court jester! You are late, entertainer! The King has already taken his seat for the graduation tournament! Run along before you are whipped!"

"Entertainer? What tournament?" I asked, genuine panic clawing up my throat. "Wait. Hold on. Let's do a quick reality check. What year is it? Where am I?"

The men exchanged amused, pitying glances. "Have you consumed too much fermented Somras, clown? You are in Hastinapur! The jewel of Bharat! The year of our great King Dhritarashtra!" 

*Hastinapur.*

My mind immediately snapped back to the dusty eighth-grade history books, the Sunday morning TV serials my grandmother rigorously forced me to watch, and the horrifying truth of Uncle Jaggi’s dashboard clock. 

He wasn't crazy. He had built a time machine. And I had just used it to ruin my date. 

I was in 3200 BCE. In a Maruti 800. 

Before my psyche could fully shatter, two heavily armored guards with imposing, twirled mustaches marched over, alerted by the commotion. 

"You there! You hold up the procession!" the lead guard barked, his spear pointed lazily at my chest. "If you are the hired entertainment from the southern lands, get moving. The princes of the Kuru blood are about to display their martial prowess. You are expected in the VIP viewing stands by order of the royal steward!"

"I'm really not an entertainer—" I started, my voice pitching high.

The guard shoved me forward with the blunt butt of his spear. "Move, strange one! Or I will test the sharpness of my blade on your strange blue leg-coverings!"

Having absolutely no choice, and entirely unwilling to argue with a large man holding a sharp piece of metal, I began to walk. The crowd immediately swept me up, a physical river of silk, gold, sweat, and aggressive body odor carrying me helplessly toward the towering wooden gates of the grand arena. 

As we passed beneath the arches and entered the stadium, the sheer, staggering scale of the structure took my breath away. It was vast, circular, and packed with tens of thousands of screaming, cheering citizens. The roar of the crowd was deafening, a localized earthquake of human excitement. Banners snapped violently in the wind. The air smelled of roasted meat and incense. 

I was unceremoniously shoved up a decorated flight of wooden stairs by the guards, bypassing the general seating altogether. We emerged into a highly luxurious, elevated viewing gallery draped in fine silks and smelling heavily of expensive perfumes. 

As the guard shoved me toward the back rows, I had to squeeze past what initially appeared to be a small mountain draped in golden silk. It wasn't a mountain; it was a man. He must have been at least six-foot-seven, built like an industrial refrigerator, and was currently occupying two full-sized seating cushions. With his booming voice and wildly aggressive gestures, he looked and sounded exactly like a hyper-aggressive Punjabi uncle furious at a loud wedding buffet. 

"Oye, Arjuna! Main tennu keha si na subah parathe taal de makhkhan naaal! Eh ghas-phus kha ke thodee naa mace chalaggi?!" he boomed, slapping his massive thigh. 

My auto-translate temporal brain kindly supplied the English subtitle echoing in my head: *"Hey Arjuna! I told you to fry stuffed parathas this morning! How will anyone swing a mace eating this grass-and-twigs diet?!"*

Sitting next to this giant was a man so unreasonably handsome he looked like he belonged on a promotional billboard for ancient cologne. He had perfectly structured cheekbones and an aura of incredibly annoying, serene perfection. Arjuna. 

Arjuna sighed, pinching the bridge of his nose in deep, patient exhaustion. "Bhrata Bhima, please lower your voice. We are royals. We are about to be presented to the King. The royal kitchen's diet is optimized for martial agility. And frankly, your obsession with dairy is slowing down your footwork."

"Dairy? My footwork?!" Bhima scoffed, grabbing a handful of what looked like ancient dry fruits and tossing them into his mouth. "Oye tuc jaande ni meri footwork bare! Paratha is the fuel of champions. Without butter, life is just sadness and tragedy. Eh sukkhe badam mere daant tod denge (These dry almonds will break my teeth)!"

I ducked my head, hastily sidestepping Bhima's wildly gesticulating massive arm, and let the guard force me down onto a plush floor cushion just behind them. 

I stayed low, crossing my legs awkwardly in my jeans, trying to make myself as invisible as possible. The gallery was filled with aristocrats, royals, and minor nobles, all dripping in unimaginable wealth. Nobody paid much attention to me, likely assuming my bizarre Puma sneakers and graphic tee were just the eccentric uniform of a freshly imported foreign court jester.

Suddenly, a heavy, anticipatory hush fell over the entire stadium. A deep, resonant conch shell blasted through the air, signaling silence. 

At the very center of the royal pavilion directly above our gallery, a man was led to the edge of the balcony. He was blind, his eyes milky white, and he wore a heavy, blindingly radiant gold crown. It was undeniably him. King Dhritarashtra. Beside him stood a terrifyingly tall, broad-shouldered man with a flowing white beard that commanded instant respect—Bhishma, the grandfather of the entire nation. 

Dhritarashtra raised a hand, and tens of thousands of people fell dead silent. It was deeply, viscerally unnerving. 

"Citizens of Hastinapur!" Dhritarashtra’s voice boomed. It echoed perfectly around the arena without a microphone, magically amplified by what I assumed was some genius acoustic architectural design. "Before we begin, a question! Why did the chariot wheel break?"

A magnificent, horrific, deeply uncomfortable silence fell over the tens of thousands of people.

"Because it was *two-tired*!" Dhritarashtra wheezed, blindly slapping his thigh. 

Silence. Complete, absolute silence. Even the crickets refused to chirp out of embarrassment. 

Suddenly, the royal guards leveled their spears threateningly at the front row of the lower stadium. "LAUGH!" the captain roared, his veins bulging.

"HA HA HA! HA HA! OH, WHAT A MAJESTIC JOKE! LONG LIVE THE KING!" the crowd immediately erupted into sheer, terrified, artificial laughter. It sounded like a hyena enclosure on fire.

Dhritarashtra nodded, appearing profoundly satisfied. "I still have the magic. Anyway! Behold the princes of the Kuru house, as they demonstrate the martial skills taught to them by the venerable, slightly inebriated Guru Dronacharya!"

He gestured broadly to his right. Beside him, an older, heavily bearded man draped in spotless white silk swaying slightly on his feet. The great weapons master, Dronacharya. 

"Riiiight," Dronacharya slurred, pointing a wobbly finger at the stadium. "We are here. To... hit things. With sticks. And sharpness. Because... honor and... stuff. Ah, whatever, hit 'em with the pointy end, kids!"

Beside him, a younger, intense-looking warrior with a brilliant gem embedded in his forehead quickly stepped forward, desperately projecting his voice. "What my esteemed father means," Ashwatthama yelled, flashing a high-wattage, panicked smile, "is that today, the Kshatriya spirit shall be tested in the crucible of dharma!"

Dronacharya burped loudly. Ashwatthama winced. 

Bhishma, the towering grandfather of the entire nation, stepped forward next, his flowing white beard commanding instant respect. He radiated absolute authority. "We stand today to witness the pinnacle of human discipline," Bhishma thundered, his voice rumbling with gravitas. "Let no trickery soil this pure sand. Let strength meet strength. For the path of righteousness—"

"Is mostly just hitting people really hard," a voice drawled loudly from the VIP section right next to me. 

I cautiously looked around my immediate surroundings. Sitting directly to my right, lounging aggressively across three silk cushions and eating what looked like an ancient version of a grape, was a young, smirking prince. He had deeply oiled dark hair, expensive red silks, and the undeniable, punchable aura of a spoiled rich kid hanging out in a VIP section of a Delhi nightclub. He was casually taking a drag from a small, smoking ceremonial clay pipe. 

"Well said, little brother!" Bhima boomed enthusiastically from in front of me, turning around and offering a massive palm. 

"Thanks, big guy," the prince smirked, reaching over me to high-five the giant. The slapping sound echoed like a gunshot across the gallery.

"Is there no decorum left in this kingdom?!" Arjuna hissed, his perfect cheekbones flushing with irritation. "Bhishma Pitamah is speaking of dharma, and you two are behaving like common tavern drunks!"

"Oh, loosen your dhoti, Arjuna," the prince rolled his eyes. "You're just mad because you can't see your reflection in your bowstring." His gaze shifted to me, noticing me staring. He raised a judgmental eyebrow. "Nice threads, peasant. Very tight. Does the mud-blooded foreign blood lack circulation?"

I swallowed dryly. "Uh. Just... fashion." 

"I am Vikarna," the prince said lazily, offering me the pipe. I shook my head no. "Third son of Dhritarashtra, though honestly, nobody keeps count once you pass the fiftieth kid. My idiot brothers are down there sweating in the dirt for our blind father's approval. I prefer to stay up here. Much less chance of getting accidentally impaled by a rogue arrow. Are you the new juggling act?"

"Software engineer," I replied automatically, my corporate drone instincts kicking in even in the BCE era. "I build scalable payment solutions. Primarily backend integrations."

Vikarna stared at me for a long, quiet moment, chewing his grape thoughtfully. "That sounds like a terrible, deeply boring magic trick. Well, whatever. Watch the dirt. The third bout is starting. My eldest brother, the glorious meat-head Suyodhana, is fighting the scrawny psychic cousin, Sahadeva. This should be delightfully brutal."

The crowd finally went completely berserk. Trumpets blared. Massive drums pounded a war-rhythm that aggressively vibrated straight through my ribcage.

I turned my attention to the sandy floor of the vast arena below. Two figures stood facing each other, armed with massive, heavy wooden maces adorned with iron spikes. 

On one side was an absolute mountain of a man. He was wildly over-muscled and furious, exuding sheer, aggressive, arrogant power. Suyodhana. Duryodhana. The primary antagonist of one of the greatest epics ever written in human history. 

Standing opposite him was a remarkably leaner, smaller, intellectual-looking young man with intense, deeply tired eyes. Sahadeva. The youngest Pandava. The prophet who knew the future but was cursed so that no one would ever believe him. 

The conch blew again. The fight began. 

It wasn't a fight; it was a scheduled demolition. Or at least, it should have been. 

Suyodhana swung his mace with the terrifying power of a runaway semi-truck. But something incredible happened. Sahadeva didn't even look stressed. He casually stepped two inches to the left, exactly a fraction of a second before the massive iron mace crushed the empty space he previously occupied. 

Suyodhana roared in frustration, launching a chaotic flurry of crushing overhead strikes and wide sweeping blows. It didn't matter. Sahadeva was constantly moving just before the attacks even began, sidestepping, ducking, and parrying with a bare minimum of effort. He was reading the immediate future. He already knew exactly where the mace was going to fall before Suyodhana even fully formed the thought. 

"Look at the scrawny one," Vikarna commented lazily next to me, taking another hit from his pipe. "He's impossibly evasive today. It's almost like he knows what's coming before my brother even swings."

Down in the arena, Sahadeva dodged another massive swing, looking vaguely bored. But knowing the future, I quickly realized, didn't change the punishing laws of physics. Sahadeva was lean, frail, and dodging took raw energy. Suyodhana, on the other hand, was an absolute tank with unlimited stamina. 

Eventually, Suyodhana didn't even bother aiming. He just swung his mace in a massive, sweeping horizontal arc that covered half the fighting pit. Sahadeva knew it was coming. He tried to leap backward, but his exhausted legs simply weren't fast enough to clear the brutal blast radius. 

The solid iron shaft clipped Sahadeva's unprotected side. 

Sahadeva gasped loudly, a sound that carried across the sand. His knees buckled instantly. He fell hard into the dirt, dropping his weapon with a heavy thud.

The entire stadium erupted into deafening cheers for the victorious Kaurava prince. Suyodhana raised both his massive hands in arrogant triumph, turning to bask in the roaring glory, completely ignoring his fallen cousin. 

Sahadeva lay gasping in the dirt, clutching his presumably shattered ribs, struggling to breathe. Royal healers began to rush out from the sidelines. 

But before they could reach him, Sahadeva slowly, agonizingly, pushed himself up onto one elbow. 

Instead of looking at his victorious cousin, or looking up in shame at the disappointed faces of his own brothers in the competitor’s pit, Sahadeva’s intense, tired eyes tracked upward. 

He looked past the fighting pit. Past the lower seating tiers. Straight toward the elevated VIP box. 

Through the massive, dusty distance of the arena, his eyes moved past the lounging Prince Vikarna and locked dead onto my face. 

The air in my lungs turned to pure, freezing ice. 

Sahadeva, bleeding profusely from the mouth, trapped thousands of years in the past, looked directly at the time-traveling, underpaid software engineer in the Levi's jeans. 

And then, very clearly, despite the pain, he winked. 

A sharp, electric shock ran violently down my spine. It wasn't just a random facial spasm. It was a calculated look of profound, undeniable recognition. Deep in my mind, my temporal-radiation-addled brain swore I could faintly hear the upbeat melody of a Bollywood classic: *“Kya Hua Tera Wada.”* 

"What the actual..." I whispered out loud, my hands gripping the edge of my silk cushion so hard my knuckles turned white. 

The historical texts, I realized with a sinking, nauseating dread, had missed quite a lot of the critical details. 

And more importantly, Sumit was going to be absolutely furious when I didn't show up for the 10 AM stand-up meeting on Monday.
