# Chapter 5: The Deal, The Dakshina, and The Desi Chakravyuh

I stood outside Shakuni’s chambers, sweating profusely into my aggressively embroidered silk dhoti. The air was heavy with the scent of sandalwood, plotting, and impending doom.

"Sahadeva," I whispered, grabbing the hem of my companion’s robe like a terrified toddler on his first day of kindergarten. "We don't have to do this. I can just hide. I can hide in the royal kitchens with Bhima. I’ve started teaching him about the concept of a calzone. He’s very passionate about it. If I stay in the pantry, Shakuni will never find me."

Sahadeva sighed, the deep, soulful sigh of a man who knew the future and recognized that running away was just an inefficient way of arriving at your destiny anyway. 

"Rahul," he said, adjusting his perfectly pleated upper garment while looking at me with dead, knowing eyes. "If we do not address the non-compete clause in the MBHT Media contract, he will legally own your soul, your intellectual property, and your equity in Bhim Cola. We must negotiate. Do not show weakness. You are a tech executive from 2026. Remember your corporate training."

"My corporate training consisted of hiding in the bathroom stall during daily standups," I hissed, my heart hammering against my ribs. "I am not equipped for Bronze Age geopolitical venture capitalism!"

"Just stand there and look like you know what 'synergy' means," Sahadeva commanded, pushing the heavy mahogany doors open.

Shakuni was waiting for us. He sat upon his velvet cushion, the ambient light catching the sharp angles of his face. He rolled his lethal bone dice between his fingers—clack, clack, clack. 

"Ah," Shakuni purred, extending his arms in a gesture of fake, suffocating affection. "My favorite temporal anomaly and my most aggressively depressed nephew. Come in, my children. Come in. I assume you have brought the signed startup aggreement?"

"We are here to renegotiate, Uncle," Sahadeva stated flatly, stepping between Shakuni and me like a protective, deeply exhausted shield. "The 10,000-year lock-in non-compete clause is legally unenforceable, even by Hastinapur standards. It violates basic labor rights. Also, it's just very tacky."

Shakuni chuckled, the sound reminding me of dry leaves being crunched under a boot. "Tacky? My boy, I am trying to build an empire! MBHT Media needs security! I cannot have our Chief Visionary Officer here running off and giving away Hastinapur’s market secrets to the Panchala kingdom or, gods forbid, a rival tech conglomerate in Dwarka! Lord Krishna is already having all the good domain names!"

"We demand concessions," Sahadeva pressed on. "Rahul will not interfere with your aggressive monopoly, and you will not disturb his... existence. In exchange, what do you offer?"

"Offer?" Shakuni’s eyes gleamed with manic, entrepreneurial fire. "I offer unparalleled influence! Rahul beta, listen to me. I will grant you top-tier placement in the algorithm of our ancient networking circles. I am talking about priority YouTube listing."

I blinked. "What?"

Shakuni declared proudly. "I have hired an entire battalion of young orphans to stand in the central market square—which is a tube-like corridor—and scream your name and ideas at top volume all day! Priority YouTube listing! Everyone will hear your thoughts on agricultural derivatives!"

"That's just a town crier with extra steps," I mumbled.

"And," Shakuni added, slamming a fist onto the table. "I will forcefully enroll one million citizens of Gandhara and Hastinapur to follow you. They will walk directly behind you wherever you go. Forced followership! One million subscribers in the physical realm!"

"I don't have the catering budget for one million followers," I protested, my voice squeaking. "Bhima would bankrupt us trying to feed them!"

"Listen," Shakuna cut in smoothly. "I will agree to minimum of 5,000-year non-compete lock-in period. That is the final offer. Rahul will provide backend structural advice to MBHT Media, but he will retain independent IP rights to his own inventions, and I will guarantee his physical safety from Vyasa's increasingly unhinged plot lines."

Shakuni narrowed his eyes. He stopped rolling the dice. The silence stretched out, thick and agonizing. I mentally prepared myself to run. 

Finally, a wide, razor-sharp smile broke across Sahdev's face and he says, "Agreed".

"You drive a hard bargain, Sahadeva," Shakuni sighed, sounding deeply impressed. "Five thousand years. A blink of an eye in cosmic terms, but I suppose I can work with a shorter timeline. For our boy Rahul, to ensure his happiness and corporate wellness... I agree."

He stood up, walking around the table, radiating the specific energy of a Silicon Valley angel investor who had just successfully lowballed two desperate founders. He grasped both my hand and Sahadeva's hand, shaking them with terrifying enthusiasm. 

"We are partners now!" Shakuni declared warmly, patting my shoulder. "MBHT Media will change the world. You negotiated beautifully, my boys. You really squeezed me! I almost feel robbed! Look at you, titans of industry! Go, celebrate! I must update my LinkedIn scroll to reflect our new partnership."

Sahadeva and I walked out of the room, the heavy wooden doors thudding shut behind us. For a brief, shining moment, I felt like a king. I puffed out my chest. I had just gone toe-to-toe with the greatest manipulator in mythological history and walked away with a massive reduction in my contractual servitude.

"Did you see that?" I laughed, elbowing Sahadeva as we walked down the corridor. "We crushed him! We cut his demands right in half. We really owned that negotiation room."

Sahadeva was nodding, a small, proud smile on his face. "Yes. I believe we did. We maintained our leverage and secured optimal terms."

We kept walking toward the royal gardens. The sun was shining. The peacocks were singing. Everything felt wonderful. 

And then, about four corridors later, I stopped dead in my tracks. The warm glow of victory suddenly felt like cold ice water sliding down my spine. 

"Wait," I said slowly, turning to Sahadeva. 

"What is it, titan of industry?" Sahadeva asked comfortably. 

"Sahadeva... a 5,000-year lock-in period..." I calculated the math in my head, the numbers hitting me like a physical blow. "5,000 years from 3200 BCE... means I am still contractually locked into MBHT Media until basically the year 1800 AD. I gave him the entire span of recorded human history."

Sahadeva froze. His psychic foresight, which was notoriously bad at reading the immediate fine print of corporate contracts, finally caught up. His eye twitched violently. 

"Oh gods," Sahadeva whispered in abject horror. 

"He did it again," I groaned, rubbing my forehead. "He gaslit us with a smile and a firm handshake. He made us feel like we won the negotiation while literally stealing five millennia of my professional life! I don't even get equity!"

"We were played," Sahadeva muttered, returning to his usual baseline of immense depression. "I am the foremost oracle of this era, and I was effortlessly outmaneuvered by a man whose entire business strategy relies on weighted dice and screaming orphans."

"We are never doing business again," I declared miserably.

***

Before I could fully process my newly acquired status as a five-thousand-year indentured startup employee, a heavy, deafening horn blast echoed across the palace complex.

It was the royal summons. The deep, guttural sound that usually meant someone had either declared war, a neighboring king had insulted someone's honor, or Dhritarashtra had discovered a new joke and demanded an immediate audience. 

Reluctantly, Sahadeva and I dragged ourselves to the grand central assembly hall. 

The Hall of the Kurus was packed. The entire royal family was present. The hundred Kauravas were lined up on one side, looking like a heavily armored rugby team. The five Pandavas stood opposite them, radiating righteous energy and aggressive muscularity. Bhima winked at me and patted his stomach, silently signaling that the pizza dough was currently proofing perfectly in the kitchen. 

King Dhritarashtra sat upon his golden throne, adjusting his massive jeweled crown. To his right stood the terrifyingly severe Grand-uncle Bhishma, whose permanent frown looked like it had been carved out of granite. Below them, swaying slightly on his feet and looking incredibly flushed, was Guru Dronacharya. 

The legendary weapons master of Hastinapur was currently blinking very hard and leaning heavily on his wooden staff. He possessed the distinct, glazed aura of a man who had started drinking somras at roughly 7:00 AM and was now passionately surfing the wave of a profound buzz.

"Quiet! Quiet everyone!" Dhritarashtra boomed, waving a hand blindly in the direction of the court. "The great Guru Dronacharya has an announcement! But first... Rahul! Where is Rahul?!"

I winced, slowly raising my hand from the back of the room. "Present, My King."

"Ah! My Chief Product Officer!" Dhritarashtra bellowed happily. "Tell me, Rahul... What is the difference between a bad archer and a stressed-out tax collector?"

A heavy silence fell over the hall. I locked eyes with Vikarna, who was leaning against a pillar smoking his pipe. Vikarna gave me a sympathetic, deeply exhausted shrug.

"I do not know, My King," I replied dutifully, playing my role. "What is the difference?"

"A bad archer misses the mark, but a stressed tax collector misses the... MARK-UP!" Dhritarashtra hit his own knee, throwing his head back and wheezing with laughter. 

In the past, the royal guards would immediately slam their spears on the ground and scream 'LAUGH! REJOICE!' to enforce mandatory mirth. But this time, nobody moved. The guards remained perfectly still. The silence stretched out, interrupted only by the blind king’s solitary giggling. 

Finally, Suyodhana, the eldest Kaurava, stepped forward. He looked utterly humiliated. 

"Father, please," Suyodhana groaned, burying his face in his hands. "I beg of you. You are embarrassing us in front of the entire geopolitical community. The ambassadors from Magadha are right there. They are writing this down. Just get to the point, please."

"Tough room," Dhritarashtra grumbled, clearing his throat and attempting to look regal. "Very well. Let us skip the corporate icebreakers. Guru Drona, the floor is yours. State your demand for Guru Dakshina! What payment do you seek from these boys for training them in the deadly arts?"

Guru Drona staggered forward, hiccups interrupting his legendary, fearsome presence. 

"My dear pupils," Drona slurred, waving his hands majestically. "You have all become... *hic*... magnificent warriors. You are the strongest, most... *hic*... deeply synergized batch I have ever produced. And now, I demand my fee."

The princes stood at attention. 

"I want you to attack Panchaal!" Drona suddenly yelled, slamming his staff down. "I want you to march into that arrogant kingdom, break past their walls, and drag King Drupad here! Bring him to me so I can personally... *hic*... spit on his extremely expensive sandals! That guy owes me money from college!"

The princes exchanged deeply confused looks. 

"Wait," Yudhishthira stepped forward, raising his hand like a polite student in a lecture hall. "Guru-ji, respectfully... you want us to start a massive international incident and invade a sovereign nation because of a personal grudge spanning decades? Is that ethically sound?"

"Quiet, HR department!" Drona snapped, pointing a wavering finger at Yudhishthira. "Drupad insulted me! He said we weren't friends anymore because I was poor! I want him captured! And I want you to tie him up with... with... what’s that really itchy rope called?"

"Jute?" Ashwatthama, Drona's immortal son, offered helpfully from the sidelines, looking mildly embarrassed by his father's state. 

"Yes! Rough, unrefined Jute!" Drona roared. "Capture him!"

The Kauravas and Pandavas looked at each other. Despite the absolute absurdity of the half-drunk demand, the concept of a violent road trip clearly appealed to their aggressive youth. Suyodhana and Arjuna both nodded, silently agreeing to the mission. 

"Wait!" Dhritarashtra suddenly called out. "If my boys are going on a majestic military conquest, they need to document the progress. They need strategy! They need Rahul!"

I choked on my own spit. "Me?! My King, I am terrified of loud noises and pointy sticks! I have no combat experience! My primary tactical achievement is creating an MS Excel macro for inventory tracking!"

"Nonsense!" Dhritarashtra beamed blindly. "Change of scenery will be good for you! You can advise them on... whatever it is you do! Synergies! Supply chains! You will accompany them to Panchaal!"

"I agree," Drona nodded, leaning heavily on Ashwatthama. "But no jokes! This is a serious, highly dramatic... *hic*... revenge plot! I am irritated by how lightly everyone is taking this! This is profound mythological warfare!" Drona paused, looking around aggressively. "Now, where did I put my flask?"

As the assembly concluded and the princes excitedly began discussing logistics for the invasion, Sahadeva quietly pulled me aside. 

"I am not going," Sahadeva whispered. "And neither is Vikarna."

"What? Why?" I pleaded, panicked at the idea of losing my only two sensible friends on a military expedition. "You can't leave me alone with them! Bhima is fully packing a siege engine with leftover pizza!"

"Vikarna and I have a separate, covert mission," Sahadeva explained, pulling me behind a large decorative pillar. "While the others attack Drupad, we are going to neutralize Drona's immediate influence."

"How?"

"We have set up a secret meeting with the Guru," Sahadeva said smoothly. "We are going to buy out our portion of the Guru Dakshina."

"Buy it out? With what?"

Sahadeva smirked. "With a one-year, unlimited subscription to the finest, triple-distilled, export-quality Somras from the royal cellars."



Later that night, I accidentally bore witness to the greatest bribery in the history of the ancient world. 

I was walking past the armory when I saw Sahadeva and Vikarna slipping into a dimly lit side room. Curious, I peeked through the heavy wooden door. 

Guru Drona was sitting on a low stool, looking extremely severe but with a slight wobble to his majestic posture. Sahadeva and Vikarna were standing before him, holding what appeared to be the largest, most intricately carved clay jug of alcohol I had ever seen. 

"Guru-ji," Vikarna began, his voice smooth and entirely devoid of martial respect. "We have come to offer our Dakshina personally. As you know, both Sahadeva and I are deeply committed to pacifism, data analytics, and preserving our own lives. We do not wish to go to Panchaal."

"Cowardice!" Drona spat, though his eyes kept darting to the massive clay jug. "A warrior must test his steel! He must face the enemy! He must—what is in that jug?"

"This," Sahadeva said smoothly, pulling the cork out with a tantalizing *pop*, "is the Platinum Reserve Somras. Aged for twelve years in oak barrels from the southern mountains. Triple distilled. Notes of honey, violently strong ancient herbs, and deep spiritual contentment. Vikarna and I pooled our allowances. We are prepared to gift you an unlimited, uninterrupted, priority-delivery supply for one entire calendar year. Delivered straight to your chambers. No questions asked."

The smell that wafted out of the jug was so potent I almost got a contact buzz just standing in the hallway. Drona’s eyes widened to the size of dinner plates. His severe, warrior-like facade completely crumbled. 

He leaned forward, sniffing the air like a heavily armed bloodhound. 

"One entire year?" Drona whispered, his voice trembling with deep, emotional vulnerability. 

"Twelve full moons, Guru-ji," Vikarna nodded solemnly. "You won't even need to leave your room. You can just sit back, meditate on the cosmic universe, and enjoy the premium reserve while the others get heavily bruised in Panchaal."

Drona aggressively snatched the jug from Vikarna's hands. He took a massive swig, his eyes rolling back in sheer delight. He slammed the jug down, wiping his magnificent beard. 

"I have always said," Drona slurred happily, "that true warrior spirit is found in diplomatic logistics! You two are excused from the campaign! Let the others die in glory. You have achieved enlightenment! Now get out of here before I drink the rest of this and start aggressively reciting poetry!"

***

Three days later, I found myself sitting in the back of a violently bumpy chariot, regretting every life decision that had led me to the Bronze Age. 

We had marched upon the Kingdom of Panchaal. The combined forces of the Pandavas and Kauravas looked undeniably terrifying as they descended upon the border. The princes rode at the front, decked out in shining armor, waving heavy maces, and looking absolutely thrilled for some sanctioned violence. 

I, meanwhile, was wearing a helmet that was clearly two sizes too large and holding a leather clipboard to track inventory. 

As we breached the outer tree line and came over the crest of the final hill overlooking the plains of Panchaal, the entire army suddenly ground to a massive, confused halt. 

"What is that?" Suyodhana asked, lowering his glowing mace and squinting into the distance. 

Down in the valley, the army of King Drupad was not lined up in standard infantry blocks. They were not standing in a clean line of engagement. 

Instead, they were arranged in a massive, terrifyingly complex, rotating circular maze composed of tens of thousands of heavily armed soldiers, spiked chariots, and massive angry elephants. It was spinning slowly, a flawless geometric engine of death. 

"It's a Chakravyuh," Arjuna breathed, his eyes wide with a mix of horror and deep professional admiration. "A spinning labyrinth of death. The ultimate defensive formation."

I stared at it. "That’s not a labyrinth. That’s an aggressive ancient fidget spinner."

Before anyone could formulate a tactical plan, a lone chariot broke from the edge of the spinning Chakravyuh and rode out toward us. Standing upon it was a fiercely muscular warrior with arrogant eyes and armor that glided beautifully in the sun.

"That’s Dhrishtadyumna!" Bhima growled, cracking his massive knuckles. "The son of Drupad! Commander of the Panchaal forces!"

Dhrishtadyumna halted his chariot a safe distance away. He pulled out a massive brass megaphone-like cone and raised it to his lips. He didn’t shout challenges of combat. He didn't demand surrender. 

He started trash-talking. 

"Oye! Kauravas and Pandavas!" Dhrishtadyumna’s voice boomed across the valley. "Is this the great army of Hastinapur?! You look like a bunch of nervous interns at a dairy farm! Are you actually going to fight, or do you need to ask your blind Daddy for permission first?!"

A collective gasp of outrage ran through the Kuru ranks. 

"Hold the line!" I screamed desperately from the back. "It’s a trap! He’s aggressively leveraging toxic masculinity to bait you into the labyrinth! It’s basic emotional manipulation! Do not engage!"

But Dhrishtadyumna was a master. He moved down his list of targets.

"Where is the big one?!" Dhrishtadyumna bellowed, looking directly at Bhima. "Where is Bhima?! Oh, there you are! Tell me, Bhima, why are you wearing armor? We all know you are just a soft little baby! A little kid who just wants to cuddle up in a blanket and eat hot parathas! Put down the mace, baby boy! Come into my maze and I'll give you a warm glass of milk!"

Bhima’s eye instantly started to twitch. The ultimate insult had been delivered. His dietary manhood had been questioned. 

"Oye khoti deya puttra!" Bhima roared, completely relapsing into heavily accented, furious Punjabi. "Tainu main parathe banake khwawanga! Tainu main tandoor vich rohroti wangu sekaanga! Main aa reha haan tainu dhasan layi!" *(O son of a donkey! I will make parathas out of you! I will roast you in the tandoor like a roti! I am coming to crush you!)*

"Bhima, wait! It's a localized trap!" I screamed, waving my clipboard frantically. 

But it was too late. Bhima let out a berserker roar that literally shook the trees, raised his mace, and charged headlong straight down the hill and directly into the spinning jaws of the Chakravyuh. The Panchaal soldiers seamlessly absorbed his massive frame, the rings shifting to lock him inside. 

"One down!" Dhrishtadyumna laughed menacingly through the cone. "Who’s next?! What about the mighty Duryodhana?! Oh wait, sorry, I mean 'Suyodhana'! I heard you changed your brand name because you were insecure about your public perception! You couldn't even keep your own name! Your ego is as fragile as wet papyrus!"

Suyodhana’s face turned violently purple. "My branding is flawless!" he shrieked, totally losing his composure. "I will crush your marketing department!" 

He kicked his horses, and his entire chariot plunged down the hill, diving straight into the second layer of the labyrinth. 

"Guys! Stop! This is a textbook funneling strategy!" I pleaded, practically pulling my hair out. "You are falling into their conversion funnel!"

"And what about Dushasana?!" Dhrishtadyumna yelled, practically skipping with joy. "I heard a rumor you gave up warfare to pursue classical dance! I heard you do pirouettes in the courtyard! Come on in, twinkle-toes! We have a special stage set up for you right in the center! Come do a little tap-dance on our spears!"

Dushasana screamed in absolute fury, ripped off his upper armor to expose his chest, and sprinted down the hill, aggressively doing what looked like a furious marching-band high-step all the way into the death maze. 

I turned to Arjuna, my last hope for strategic sanity. Arjuna was calmly checking his arrows, looking entirely unbothered by the taunts. 

"Thank God, Arjuna," I gasped. "You’re the smartest one here. You see the tactical error. We need to flank them—"

"Hey, Arjuna!" Dhrishtadyumna called out, interrupting me. "I heard you're a decent shot! Well, I have a challenge for you! Inside the center of this maze, I have placed a small wooden bird. Inside the bird's eye is a very small, incredibly tiny mole! We brought people from the Guinness Book of World Records over from Greece! If you can hit the mole on the bird's eye while spinning through the maze, you get the official world record!"

Arjuna froze. The concept of an officially documented, internationally recognized world record for impossible archery was his literal kryptonite. 

"They have the Guinness verifyers... here?" Arjuna whispered, his eyes gleaming with mad ambition. 

"Arjuna, no! The Guinness book won't be invented for another four thousand years! It's fake news!" I screamed, desperately grabbing onto his chariot wheel. 

"I must secure my legacy, Rahul!" Arjuna yelled dramatically, kicking my hands away. He practically flew down the hill, his Gandiva bow singing as he vanished into the rotating rings of the Chakravyuh. 

In less than ten minutes, the entire command structure of the Kuru army had been successfully lured, baited, and trapped inside the deadliest formation in ancient warfare because of some mid-level trash talk. 

And then, there was just me. Sitting alone on the hill, holding a clipboard, wearing an oversized helmet. 

The Panchaal army continued to spin. I could hear the distant sounds of maces clashing, Bhima swearing loudly in Punjabi, and Suyodhana yelling about his brand equity. But they were trapped deep inside the rings, unable to break the outer layers. 

Dhrishtadyumna looked up to the hill, spotting me standing there awkwardly. 

"And you!" Dhrishtadyumna pointed at me via the cone. "The weird guy in the blue trousers! Come on down! Let me mock your life choices!"

"I’m good!" I yelled back, politely raising a hand. "I am not a combatant! I am an operational consultant!"

"Come fight, coward!" 

"I literally have no ego to bruise!" I shouted back. "I possess profoundly low self-esteem! Your psychological warfare has no leverage over my chronic millennial depression!"

Dhrishtadyumna paused, lowering the cone slightly, apparently unsure how to counter an opponent who openly admitted to being emotionally void. 

I sat down on a rock and watched the maze spin. My friends—despite their massive egos and absolute lack of self-control—were slowly getting ground down inside the formation. Drupad’s army was incredibly strong, heavily armored, and structurally perfect. 

I needed to do something. But I couldn't fight. If I walked down there, a stray spear would instantly end my long, pointless existence. 

I stared at the Chakravyuh. I stopped looking at it like a magical death maze and started looking at it the way I used to look at broken code on a Monday morning. 

It was a system. A rotating, algorithmic system based on synchronized movement. 

"Wait..." I muttered, pulling out my stylus and flipping the clipboard to a blank leaf. I began charting the movements. The outer ring spun clockwise. The middle ring spun counter-clockwise. There were gaps—tiny, fleeting gaps that opened up every time the seventh chariot in the third ring synchronized with the twelfth foot soldier in the fourth ring. 

It was essentially a massive analog clock. And it had a glitch. 

Every forty-five seconds, there was a three-second window where the entire left quadrant opened up because the heavy elephant cavalry turned too slowly to close the gap. 

I stood up, adjusting my oversized helmet. I grabbed a medium-sized wooden shield from a fallen chariot and took a deep breath. 

"Okay, Rahul," I whispered to myself. "It's just like navigating the Delhi Metro at Rajiv Chowk during rush hour. Find the gap. Push through. Don't make eye contact."


I started jogging down the hill. Not walking proudly like a hero. Not sprinting aggressively like a warrior. I just did a light, high-knees warm-up jog, holding the wooden shield awkwardly over my head like an umbrella in a rainstorm. 

I reached the outer ring of the Chakravyuh. The soldiers, towering men with spears, looked at me in utter confusion. 

"Forty-three... forty-four... forty-five!" I counted out loud. 

The heavy elephant cavalry began its slow, lumbering turn. A three-foot gap opened up between the outer ring and the second layer. 

I didn't hesitate. I leaped forward, sucking in my stomach, and slipped through the gap right as the elephant swung past. I was in ring two. 

A soldier swung a heavy mace at my head. I instinctively ducked, screaming in genuine terror, and scrambled on all fours under the spinning legs of an oncoming horse. I rolled out the other side, popping up in ring three. 

The whole process felt exactly like navigating through a highly aggressive game of Frogger. I didn't try to engage. I didn't try to parry. I just used pure, unadulterated cowardice and high-anxiety reflexes to sprint, duck, roll, and aggressively apologize my way through the labyrinth. 

"Excuse me! So sorry! Just passing through! Watch the shield!" I yelled, wildly sidestepping a spinning blade chariot. 

I hit the forty-five-second gap again. Slipped through ring four. Crawled under ring five. Squeezed past a group of heavily armored guards in ring six. 

Before anyone could comprehend what was happening, I stumbled out into the clear, open center of the Chakravyuh. 

The center of the maze was completely calm. Sitting on a massive, highly decorated war elephant was King Drupad himself, an older, fierce-looking man with a flowing grey beard and an expression of absolute, supreme confidence. Standing next to the elephant on a chariot was the general, Dhrishtadyumna. 

They were casually drinking water from bronze flasks, watching the outer rings slowly grind down the Kuru princes. 

I collapsed onto my knees in front of them, gasping for air, dropping my wooden shield. I brushed the dust off my blue trousers and stood up, panting heavily. 

Drupad and Dhrishtadyumna slowly turned their heads to look at me. Their jaws dropped simultaneously. 

"What... what just happened?" Dhrishtadyumna stammered, dropping his flask. "How did you breach the seven rings? Where is your weapon? Who did you kill?!"

"I didn't kill anyone!" I gasped, clutching my chest. "I just... I just found the latency loop in your algorithm and exploited the packet delay!"

Drupad stared at me, his fierce warrior eyes searching my dust-covered face. "You walked through the deadliest military formation ever conceived by the gods... without throwing a single punch? You solved it purely with intellect?"

Suddenly, the adrenaline fully hit my system. I had survived. I wasn't dead. I had beaten the ancient death maze. A wave of overwhelming, pure dopamine crashed over my brain. 

"YES!" I screamed at the top of my lungs. 

I threw my hands in the air. Overcome with entirely unearned triumph, I started doing a wildly uncoordinated victory dance right in the center of the battlefield. It was a mix between the Macarena, a terrible salsa step, and straight-up uncontrolled jumping. 

"YAYYY! I WON! I ACTUALLY WON!" I yelled, spinning around and pointing aggressively at Drupad. "IN YOUR FACE, GRANDPA! BEAT YOUR MAZE! I'M THE KING OF THE WORLD!"

I did a final, deeply embarrassing hip-thrust towards Dhrishtadyumna. 

The entire battlefield seemed to grind to a halt. The outer rings stopped spinning as the Panchaal soldiers slowed down in sheer confusion, watching this strange man in blue trousers dance like a lunatic in the very center of their impenetrable fortress. 

King Drupad did not look angry. He did not signal his guards to execute me. 

Instead, a strange, profound calm settled over his face. He looked at me—sweaty, unarmored, dancing horribly, and clutching a broken wooden shield—with an expression of absolute reverence. 

"Incredible," Drupad whispered, his voice trembling with awe. "To conquer violence without violence. To possess a mind so sharp that it sees the very fabric of movement and geometry. To celebrate victory not with the blood of your enemies, but with a joyful, slightly disturbing physical convulsion."

"Uh..." I stopped dancing, mid-shimmy. "Are you going to kill me?"

"Kill you?" King Drupad let out a massive, booming laugh that echoed across the silent battlefield. "My boy! You are exactly the kind of strategic genius this kingdom needs! You are not a brute like the Kurus! You are a man of profound intellect and entirely unthreatening physical presence!"

He climbed down from his elephant, walked completely ignoring the ongoing battle, and placed both his massive hands on my shoulders. 

I swallowed hard, looking up at him. 

"I have made a decision," King Drupad announced loudly, his voice carrying over the silent ranks. The Kauravas and Pandavas, finally breaking free from the stalled outer rings, rushed toward the center, arriving just in time to hear the King’s proclamation. 

"I was planning to host a Swayamvar for my beautiful, brilliant daughter," Drupad declared, beaming down at me. "But forget the Swayamvar! I have found the perfect man! I want you to marry my daughter, Draupadi!"

My heart stopped. The world violently flatlined. 

Behind me, I could hear Bhima drop his mace with a loud clatter. Arjuna stopped breathing entirely. Suyodhana let out a high-pitched squeak of profound confusion. 

I stared into King Drupad’s excited, hopeful eyes. My mouth opened and closed silently, like a terrified goldfish. 

Marry Draupadi? Me? The most important female figure in the entire Mahabharata epic? The woman who was supposed to be the catalyst for the greatest war in human history? 

I had just accidentally engaged myself to the central plot point of the entire mythological universe. 

"I... I think I need to submit a ticket to HR," I croaked, right before my vision swam, the overwhelming stress overloaded my system, and I promptly passed out face-first into the dirt of Panchaal.


I am not sure how long I remained unconscious in the dust of the Panchaal battlefield. When I finally came to, my face was entirely coated in dirt, my mouth tasted like stale adrenaline and fear, and I was staring directly up into the vibrant orange sky of an ancient evening. 

There was a massive circle of faces hovering above me. 

To my left, Bhima was aggressively shaking my shoulders, still slightly covered in the soot of the Chakravyuh, yelling my name repeatedly in a volume that could have cracked a glacier. "Raool! Uth ja oye sher! Aankhan khol! Tainu Draupadi mil rahi hai! Mainu lagga si tu bas gol roti banana jaanda hai, par tu taan kamaal kar ditta!" *(Rahul! Wake up, tiger! Open your eyes! You are getting Draupadi! I thought you only knew how to make round bread, but you did wonders!)*

To my right, Suyodhana was pacing furiously back and forth, dragging his massive, terrifyingly heavy mace through the dirt. "This ruins the brand cohesion! The Kaurava Consulting Group was supposed to secure the Panchala alliance! If he marries her, the equity split is going to be completely ruined! We need to file an injunction!" 

Arjuna was the only one not entirely focused on me. He was standing on the edge of the circle, angrily staring at the shattered remains of the Chakravyuh maze, muttering endlessly about how the Guinness verifying committee was a hoax and how his legacy had been unjustly stripped from him by a technicality. 

But it wasn’t my friends who finally pulled me out of my stupor. 

The crowd parted respectfully as King Drupad knelt beside me. He didn’t look like a defeated monarch. He looked like a man who had successfully secured Series A funding from the most prominent angel investor in the world. He beamed down at me, his eyes wide with a terrifying fusion of paternal affection and strategic triumph. 

"My beautiful, passive, intellectually superior son-in-law!" Drupad boomed enthusiastically, helping me sit up. My head spun violently. "Do not worry! We have called the royal physicians! They say your nervous system merely shut down due to the sheer magnitude of your own tactical genius!" 

"My tactical genius was essentially a panic attack with high mobility," I whispered hoarsely, rubbing my throbbing temples. "Your Majesty, please listen to me. There has been a massive error in the backend processing of this timeline. I am from the year 2026. I still have a rapidly accumulating student loan debt. I have absolutely zero social skills. I am in no position to enter a holy, politically charged matrimony with the most important female protagonist of this era!" 

Drupad patted my back with such force I nearly coughed up an ancient lung. 

"Nonsense!" the King roared happily. "Modesty! That is exactly what this era lacks! A man who can dismantle the ultimate military formation of the Bronze Age without a weapon and still claims to be an unskilled peasant? You are a master of humility! My daughter Draupadi is brilliant—she has completely overhauled my accounting department and aggressively unionized the palace staff. You two will be an unstoppable administrative force!" 

I looked wildly around for Sahadeva, desperately needing my only sensible friend to aggressively invoke a paradox law or cite an obscure Vedic scripture to save me. But Sahadeva was back in Hastinapur, probably sipping premium twelve-year-old Somras with Vikarna, completely unaware that I had just fundamentally derailed the entire romantic and political engine of the Great Epic. 

I looked past Drupad. Standing just outside the circle, observing the absolute chaos with a terrifyingly calm, intensely analytical expression, was a woman. 

She was incredibly beautiful, but not in the soft, tragic way the old television serials had portrayed her. Draupadi stood tall, examining me with the sharp, calculating eyes of a brilliant CEO assessing a highly unpredictable new acquisition. She didn’t look swooning. She looked like she was already drafting my quarterly performance review in her head. 

She caught my eye, raised one perfectly sculpted eyebrow, and offered a very slow, slightly terrifying smirk. 

My stomach plummeted. Vyasa was going to kill me. Shakuni was going to try and monetize my wedding. Bhishma was definitely going to have an aneurysm over the procedural mismatch. 

And as the Panchaal soldiers began to aggressively cheer my name, hoisting my deeply unwilling, exhausted body into the air atop a large bronze shield, a singular, horrifying thought crystallized in my panicked mind. 

I was officially the groom. 

Kurukshetra: Error 404 had finally stopped being a glitch. I had officially become the main feature.
