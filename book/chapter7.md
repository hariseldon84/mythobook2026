# Chapter 7: The House of Wax and the Nayada Migration

The journey to Varnavata was, by all reasonable standards of ancient travel, extremely luxurious. Yudhishthira had ensured that the royal convoy was equipped with heavy shock-absorbing chariot suspensions, four different types of ergonomic silk pillows per passenger, and a highly structured, heavily audited rest-stop schedule. 

But despite the comfort, the atmosphere within our massive travel pavilion during the mandatory thirty-minute hydration breaks was suffocatingly tense for me. I knew exactly what awaited us at the destination: a highly flammable death-trap designed by an author who was rapidly losing his grip on his own narrative. 

However, my anxiety regarding impending arson was constantly being interrupted by profound, soul-crushing awkwardness. 

During our third rest stop near a beautifully scenic riverbank, I was sitting on a small rock, trying to mentally calculate the combustion temperature of ancient lacquer. I was vigorously chewing on a neem twig, deep in corporate survival mode, when a shadow fell over me. 

It was Queen Mother Kunti. 

She wasn't flanked by her usual entourage of royal maids. She stood alone, clutching a very beautifully woven basket. Ever since the archery tournament, Kunti had firmly established her own deeply tragic, entirely incorrect head-canon regarding my origin. She still believed, with terrifying maternal conviction, that my bad posture, terrified demeanor, and general uselessness were the obvious markers of a secret demigod son she had abandoned long ago. 

"Rahul, beta," Kunti whispered softly, looking around to ensure Nakula and Sahadeva were distracted by a nearby waterfall. 

I instantly froze, spitting out the neem twig. "My Queen. Is the... is the hydration schedule acceptable?"

She smiled—a sad, beautiful, profoundly maternal smile that made me feel incredibly guilty for being a thirty-four-year-old software engineer from the future. She gracefully sat down on the rock next to me, opening the woven basket. 

The immediate, overwhelming scent of freshly made ghee and roasted cardamom filled the air. 

"You must eat, my child," Kunti murmured, pulling out a large bronze bowl of kheer and pressing it firmly into my hands. "You are looking so strained. Are the chariot accommodations suitable? Have your... have your 'lumbar support' requirements been met?"

"They are fine, My Queen. The pillows are very efficient," I stuttered, taking the bowl. "But really, you do not need to constantly bring me specialized desserts. Bhima has already forced me to consume three dozen laddoos today."

Kunti reached out, gently brushing a speck of dust off my violently out-of-place blue t-shirt. Her eyes were shimmering with unshed tears. 

"A mother always knows when her child needs nourishment," she whispered passionately. "Especially a child who has spent his life wandering the harsh, uncaring world, forced to wear such bizarre, constricting blue fabrics instead of the royal silks of his birthright."

"My Queen," I sighed, putting the bowl down. This had gone too far. I couldn't let her keep believing this tragic soap opera. It was time to clear the air, even if it risked a minor temporal paradox. "Please. Listen to me. I know what you are doing. I know *why* you think I am... associated with you."

Kunti gasped softly, bringing a hand to her mouth. "You... you know your truth?"

"I know the truth of the boon you received," I clarified slowly, trying to be as gentle as possible. "I know about the secret. But I am not him. The person you are looking for—the brave, tragic figure born with armor and earrings—is currently hanging out with Suyodhana in Hastinapur. I think you should probably talk to... Karna."

Kunti’s eyes went wide. She stared at me, the color draining slightly from her face. I braced myself for the revelation to hit, for her to finally understand that Karna was the lost son of the Sun God, and I was just an IT guy who couldn't drive a manual transmission. 

But instead of dramatic realization, Kunti just shook her head, tears finally spilling over her cheeks. She aggressively grabbed my hand. 

"Oh, you brave, foolish boy," Kunti sobbed softly, squeezing my hand with terrifying strength. "Trying to deflect your own pain! Trying to assign your divine tragic backstory to that incredibly handsome, highly skilled archer just to spare my feelings! You are so selfless, beta!"

I blinked. "Wait, no. Seriously. It's Karna. He literally has the solar earrings built into his earlobes—"

"Shhh," Kunti hushed me, putting a finger to my lips. "The heart can only take so much pain. Let us not speak of 'Karna' or 'boons.' In my heart, right now, I am just so incredibly glad that you are finally here, traveling with your... *with the Pandavas*. You are with your brothers now. And that is enough for my soul."

She patted my cheek, stood up, and hurried away toward the royal tent, dabbing her eyes with her silk pallu. 

I sat alone on the rock, holding the bowl of kheer, profoundly defeated. 

"Well," I muttered to myself, taking a spoonful of the pudding. "I tried. If she wants to believe the heavily-indebted tech worker is the Sun God's secret champion, that's her prerogative."

We arrived in Varnavata three days later, just as the sun was beginning to set, casting a deep, fiery orange glow over the landscape. 

The town itself was beautiful—a thriving, peaceful settlement surrounded by lush forests and rolling hills. The locals had gathered on the streets to throw flower petals as our chariots rolled through the main thoroughfare. 

But the true spectacle was the holiday home itself. 

The Lakshagriha. 

It was a massive, sprawling luxury mansion set on a raised plateau overlooking a dark, calm lake. From the outside, it looked like an architectural masterpiece, featuring sweeping grand arches, massive wooden pillars, and intricately carved balconies. 

However, because I knew what it was actually made of, the entire structure looked less like a luxury resort and more like the world's largest, most excessively decorated matchstick. 

Standing at the grand entrance to formally receive us was Purochana. 

Purochana was Vyasa's hired architect and chief saboteur. Myths painted him as a dark, brooding, evil servant of the Kauravas. In reality, Purochana looked like an incredibly slick, highly caffeinated real estate agent who had just snorted a massive line of pure ancient adrenaline. He was wearing an aggressively bright yellow tunic and possessed a smile so wide it looked physically painful. 

Standing next to him, clutching a clipboard and smiling viciously, was a woman who I could only assume was his wife, the property manager. 

"Welcome! Welcome to the Varnavata Royal Retreat!" Purochana screamed enthusiastically, throwing his arms wide as Yudhishthira stepped off the lead chariot. "We are absolutely *burned* out from all the preparation, but we are thrilled you made it! The atmosphere here is truly... *combustible*!"

I paused, halfway off Arjuna's chariot. I stared at Purochana. Was he seriously doing fire puns right to their faces? 

"The architecture is quite impressive," Yudhishthira noted, adjusting his robes and entirely missing the aggressive subtext. "What material is this? It has a very unique sheen."

"Oh, it's a proprietary blend!" Purochana laughed loudly, turning to his wife and aggressively high-fiving her. Her vicious smile widened slightly. "We utilized a heavy base of dried bamboo, coated entirely in premium, highly concentrated lacquer, and sealed it with clarified ghee! It really traps the heat! Once you're inside, you'll find it incredibly... *warm*!"

"Warmth is good for my joints," Kunti noted appreciatively. 

"Exactly, My Queen!" Purochana beamed. "It's the hottest property on the market! Truly a blazing success of modern engineering! Come, let me give you the grand tour! We have amenities! We have luxury dining! We have... multiple, highly conspicuous escape routes!"

I froze. "Escape routes?"

"Of course!" Purochana nodded enthusiastically, checking something off his wife's clipboard. "Sage Vyasa was very specific during the architectural consultation. We have a beautiful subterranean exit tunnel conveniently marked by decorative glowing stones that leads directly into the forest! It’s perfect for... late-night jogging! Or violently fleeing impromptu structural fires!"

Purochana dissolved into hysterical, slightly unhinged laughter, aggressively high-fiving his wife again. 

I looked at Sahadeva. The psychic just shrugged his shoulders, grabbed his duffel bag, and walked calmly toward the giant matchstick house.

"He respects the bit," Sahadeva murmured as he passed me. "You have to admire the commitment to the theatrical villainy."


Purochana led us inside the mansion. I had expected the interior to smell heavily of chemicals and imminent death, but instead, it smelled like expensive sandalwood and roasted pine. The floors were polished to a mirror shine, and massive oil lamps were strategically placed near highly flammable silk curtains in an architectural choice that screamed 'insurance liability.'

As the Pandavas dispersed to find their assigned rooms, I hung back, pretending to examine an intricately carved wooden pillar that was literally oozing combustible resin. 

I watched Purochana excuse himself, walking briskly toward a small utility closet near the rear kitchens, followed closely by his viciously smiling wife. I waited until the heavy wooden door clicked shut before creeping over and pressing my ear against the keyhole. 

I needed to know the exact timeline of the arson. I assumed Vyasa had given Purochana strict instructions to light the match at midnight in three days, ensuring maximum dramatic tension. 

But what I heard through the door was significantly more terrifying than a standard murder plot. 

"Did you show them the tunnel?" Purochana's wife hissed, her voice muffled slightly by the thick wood. 

"I showed them the tunnel, my love!" Purochana replied enthusiastically. "I literally lined the path with bioluminescent rocks! A blind man could find the escape route!"

"Good," she muttered, the sound of parchment unrolling following her words. "Because Sage Vyasa just sent a priority courier bat with an updated script contingency."

"Contingency? What contingency?"

"Vyasa is hedging his bets," the wife explained, tapping her stylus against the parchment. "He said that ideally, they escape through the tunnel, believe the Kauravas tried to incinerate them, and we proceed to the great Kurukshetra War as planned. Standard mythological protocol."

"Standard protocol," Purochana agreed nervously. "But?"

"But," the wife's voice dropped to a conspiratorial whisper, "Vyasa said if they *don't* find the tunnel, and they actually burn to death in the house... he is entirely okay with it."

My stomach plummeted. I almost choked on my own breath. 

"He's okay with it?" Purochana sounded just as shocked as I was. "But... they are the protagonists! The central heroes of the epic! If they die in Chapter 7, the entire narrative collapses!"

"Vyasa is pivoting," she said grimly. "He said if they die, he's just going to change genres. He's going to turn the *Mahabharata* into a fast-paced, high-stakes Sheednay Shalaadon murder-mystery thriller. He said he has already drafted a fifty-page outline titled *'The Hastinapur Conspiracy: Who Fried the Pandavas?'* featuring Vidura acting as a hard-boiled detective!"

"A Sheednay Shalaadon thriller?!" Purochana shrieked happily. "Oh, I love a good trashy airport novel! Does it have scandalous romance sub-plots?"

"Yes! Vyasa said he’ll have Bhishma entirely compromise his vows and run away with a mysteriously wealthy widow from Gandhara!"

"Incredible! So... we burn the house down either way!"

"Either way!" his wife confirmed, the sound of an aggressive high-five echoing loudly against the door. "We strike the match on the night of the dark moon! Tomorrow night! Prepare the kerosene!"

I backed away from the utility closet, my mind reeling. 

Vyasa wasn't just trying to initiate the war; he was actively apathetic to our survival! He had a backup plan that involved turning the greatest spiritual epic in Indian history into a pulp-fiction whodunit that competes with The Sidney Sheldon. If we didn't escape that fire, there would be no Bhagavad Gita, no Kurukshetra, just Vidura walking around in a trench coat trying to remember who he was investigating. 

I sprinted toward the main living quarters. I needed to assemble the Pandavas. I had exactly twenty-four hours to organize a full strategic extraction. 

But organizing the Pandavas during a designated holiday was apparently harder than organizing a military campaign. The 'vacation mindset' had aggressively infected the entire Hastinapur HR department. 

I found Bhima first. He had entirely bypassed the luxurious bedrooms and set up a permanent base camp in the grand kitchens. He was wearing a makeshift apron and enthusiastically stirring a boiling vat of sweet milk. 

"Bhima!" I yelled, skidding into the kitchen. "We need to talk! Tomorrow night—"

"Oye Raool!" Bhima roared, cutting me off with a massive wooden spoon. "Tu bilkul sahi time te aaya hai! Dekh, Purochana banda bada hi ghaint hai! Ohne specially mere layi ithe do tandoor lagwaye ne! Aur piche ik poora kamra sirf desi ghee da bharya hai! Main kal raat nu 'Mega-Langar' plan kar reha haan!" *(Hey Rahul! You came at the exact right time! Look, Purochana is a great guy! He specially installed two tandoors just for me! And in the back, an entire room is filled with desi ghee! I am planning a 'Mega-Langar' tomorrow night!)*

"Bhima, the ghee isn't for cooking!" I screamed in frustration. "It's a highly flammable accelerant! The whole house is a trap!"

"Trap-shrap kuch nahi hunda," Bhima laughed, casually tossing a handful of cashews into the boiling milk. "Tainu humesha tension rehndi hai. Tu jaake aaram kar, kal tainu main special ghee-roast khawanga!" *(There is no trap-shrap. You always take tension. You go and rest, tomorrow I will feed you special ghee-roast!)*

Realizing Bhima was entirely blinded by the culinary potential of an arson plot, I bolted down the hall to Yudhishthira's room. 

Yudhishthira was sitting cross-legged on a silk mat, surrounded by dozens of thick legal scrolls. He was meticulously highlighting paragraphs with a piece of colored chalk. 

"Yudhishthira! We have a crisis!" I gasped, leaning against the doorframe. "Purochana is going to burn this place down tomorrow! Vyasa has authorized a genre-shift to a murder mystery! We have to initiate an evacuation!"

Yudhishthira didn't look up from his scrolls. He simply held up a hand, demanding silence, before aggressively highlighting another line. 

"That is legally impossible, Rahul," Yudhishthira said calmly. 

"What do you mean legally impossible?! I literally heard them discussing kerosene!"

"I have just reviewed the building codes for the Varnavata municipality," Yudhishthira explained, tapping a scroll with profound bureaucratic satisfaction. "According to Section 4, Clause 12b of the Royal Zoning Ordinance, any residential structure constructed with more than 40% flammable materials requires a minimum of three weeks' written notice before scheduled demolition. Furthermore, arson violates the core tenets of hospitality Dharma."

"They don't care about the zoning ordinance!" I yelled. "They are villains!"

"A villain without a properly filed grievance permit is just a nuisance, Rahul," Yudhishthira replied sternly. "I will draft a formal cease-and-desist letter to Purochana immediately. If he tries to burn the house down tomorrow without submitting the correct paperwork, I will take him directly to the Supreme Court of Hastinapur." 

I stared at the eldest Pandava, utterly defeated by the power of weaponized bureaucracy. "You are going to burn to death clutching a compliance form."

"At least I will die on the right side of the law," he replied virtuously.

I backed out of the room, rubbing my temples. Arjuna was nowhere to be found, completely absent on some long, brooding walk through the woods to practice his archery and undoubtedly complain about Draupadi’s critique of his elbow tension.

I wandered into the eastern wing, desperate for an ally. I pushed open the door to Nakula's chambers.

"Nakula, please tell me you are willing to flee—"

I stopped dead in my tracks. 

Nakula, the spectacularly handsome, sword-wielding warrior prince, was currently sitting on a velvet chaise lounge, wearing a thick, green mud-mask on his face and having his cuticles aggressively pushed back by a very confused local maid. 

"Ah, Rahul!" Nakula mumbled happily, trying not to crack the mud-mask. "Come! Sit! The lighting in this house is aggressive on the T-zone. I am trying to re-hydrate my pores before the evening breeze hits."

"You are getting a manicure?" I whispered, deeply confused. 

"Self-care is a weapon, Rahul," Nakula lectured seriously, gesturing broadly with a perfectly buffed nail. "When you look impeccable, your enemies hesitate for exactly 0.5 seconds out of pure jealousy. That is the tactical advantage of a flawless pedicure. You should join me. Your cuticles look like they survived an earthquake."

"My cuticles are fine! Nakula, the house is a giant matchstick! We have to leave!"

"We can't leave now," Nakula gasped, looking horrified. "The mud needs another fifteen minutes to set! And tomorrow I have scheduled a full herbal blowout for my hair! Relax, Rahul. The structural integrity of the house is irrelevant when your aesthetic integrity is at peak performance. Here, let her file your thumb."

Before I could protest, the maid grabbed my hand and aggressively started filing a hangnail. 

I sighed, physically allowing my thumb to be buffed while realizing I was essentially the only adult trapped in an apocalyptic frat house. 

My last hope was Sahadeva. I left the impromptu spa session and marched down to the basement where the psychic had secured a quiet, dark room. 

I pushed the door open. The room was pitch black, lit only by a single small oil lamp. Sahadeva was sitting perfectly still in a deep lotus position, his eyes rolled back in his head, completely lost in a deeply profound meditative trance. 

"Sahadeva," I whispered, shaking his shoulder gently. "Sahadeva, snap out of it. The extraction plan is actively failing. Nobody wants to leave. We need to use your psychic foresight to convince them the fire is real."

Sahadeva didn't move. He just smiled slightly, his eyes still rolled back. 

"Do not disrupt the broadcast, Rahul," Sahadeva murmured, his voice echoing slightly in the dark room. 

"Broadcast? What broadcast?"

"The narrative streams of the future," Sahadeva sighed happily. "I have finally managed to focus my psychic energy past the immediate localized timeline. I am currently projecting my consciousness into the digital ether of 2026."

"Wait. Are you... are you watching Netflix?!"

"I am three episodes deep into a show called *Stranger Things*," Sahadeva confessed, an expression of pure, unadulterated bliss on his face. "This 'Eleven' child possesses remarkable telekinetic Dharma. Do not interrupt me. I plan to binge-watch the entire season before Purochana lights the match."

"Sahadeva! We are going to die!"

"I foresee that we will not die," Sahadeva replied calmly, "Because I foresee that you will invent a highly deceptive, culturally inappropriate plan to evacuate us without triggering Vyasa's Sidney Sheldon backup script. Now please, hush. The demogorgon is approaching the swimming pool."

I walked out of the basement, realizing the horrific truth. Sahadeva wasn't going to help me. He had weaponized his clairvoyance purely for streaming entertainment. I was completely alone. 

I had exactly twenty-four hours to figure out how to secretly evacuate five super-powered, hyper-stubborn princes and one fiercely maternal Queen from a highly monitored, heavily guarded house made of extremely flammable lacquer, without anyone noticing we were gone.


To fully understand the sheer magnitude of our ridiculous extraction through the Halloween party scheme, I need to elaborate on exactly how structurally failing my prior attempts had been earlier that day, specifically during my interactions with Yudhishthira and Nakula. 

After my failed attempt to get Sahadeva to stop binge-watching *Stranger Things* in the basement, I had sprinted back upstairs to the main living quarters, hoping that at least one of the elder Pandavas had regained some sense of survival instinct. 

I found Yudhishthira exactly where I had left him: deeply invested in municipal zoning legislation. But this time, Purochana was standing in the doorway of his room, looking incredibly anxious. 

"My Prince," Purochana was saying, his oily smile stretched thin. "I truly do not believe a formal audit of the structural load-bearing pillars is necessary. The wood is perfectly safe. Extremely safe. Practically inflammable!" He practically screamed the last word, aggressively winking at his wife hovering in the hallway. 

"Nonsense!" Yudhishthira replied briskly, unrolling a scroll that was at least six feet long. "Section 4, Clause 12b explicitly states that any structure built within eighty nautical miles of a royal forest must undergo a stringent termite-resistance check. I have drafted an official mandate for you to submit to the Hastinapur public works department. You cannot light any... ceremonial hearths... until this is approved!"

Purochana stared at the massive legal scroll being shoved into his face. His eye twitched violently. 

"Prince Yudhishthira," Purochana choked out, gripping the doorframe. "It is a highly urgent ceremonial hearth. Sage Vyasa specifically requested a massive bonfire tonight to... to celebrate your arrival!"

"Celebrations without proper safety permits are the leading cause of unauthorized joy," Yudhishthira declared sternly. "I expect the paperwork filed in triplicate by 8:00 PM tonight. Or there will be severe HR repercussions." 

I watched Purochana stumble away down the hallway, looking like he wanted to actively cry or just set himself on fire out of pure frustration. 

"Yudhishthira," I whispered, stepping into the room. "You just handed the chief arsonist a cease-and-desist letter. He is going to burn the house down right now out of sheer bureaucratic spite!"

"He wouldn't dare," Yudhishthira sniffed, picking up his colored chalk again. "I have established a clear paper trail." 

Realizing I was arguing with a man who believed a strongly worded email could stop a guided missile, I abandoned him and ran back to Nakula's room. 

The impromptu spa session had escalated. Nakula was now lying on his back, cucumber slices over his eyes, while the bewildered local maid attempted to aggressively exfoliate his left calf. 

"Nakula! Get up!" I yelled, throwing a pillow at him. "Purochana is literally losing his mind in the hallway! He has the kerosene! We have to leave right now!"

"Rahul, please," Nakula mumbled sleepily, not removing the cucumbers. "My pores are finally opening. Do you know how hard it is to find a decent aesthetician in these rural areas? She has magic hands. Heavy, calloused agricultural hands, but magic nonetheless. If I leave now, I will break out in stress-hives."

"You are going to break out in actual third-degree burns!" I shrieked. "The house is made of ghee!"

"Ghee is an excellent natural moisturizer," Nakula noted thoughtfully. "Perhaps I should ask them to rub down my shoulders before we evacuate." 

It was in that exact moment of absolute despair, surrounded by men who valued manicures and zoning laws over their own lives, that an idea originally hit me. It was born purely out of the necessity to bypass their dignity. If I couldn't convince them to flee as warriors, I would convince them to flee as idiots.

The next morning, the deadline weighed heavily on me. I stood in the grand courtyard of the Lakshagriha, staring at the highly flammable architecture, desperately running simulations in my head.

If we just walked out the front door, Purochana’s heavily armed corporate security force would immediately engage us, causing exactly the sort of violent, dramatic conflict Vyasa wanted. We needed an extraction plan that featured absolute stealth. We needed to disguise ourselves so completely that even if we walked right past the guards, they wouldn't realize the royal targets were fleeing. 

And then, looking at a nearby royal tailor carefully dressing a wooden display mannequin with Dushasana's returned dry-cleaning... it hit me. 

Disguises. Massive, absurd cultural disguises. 

I immediately called an emergency all-hands meeting in the main drawing room. 

"Listen to me!" I announced, standing in front of the highly confused Pandavas and a very concerned Queen Kunti. "Tonight is the 31st of October."

"What is an October?" Arjuna asked, looking up from aggressively polishing an arrow. 

"Irrelevant!" I cut him off. "In my future culture, tonight is a massive, sacred religious festival called... 'Halloween.' It is a night where humans must disguise themselves as terrifying monsters, ghouls, or culturally relevant pop-culture icons to ward off evil spirits. If we do not participate in this festival, the gods of New York will curse us with terrible Wi-Fi latency for a thousand years!"

Yudhishthira gasped, clutching his legal scrolls. "A curse on the communication infrastructure? That is highly disruptive to the workflow! We must obey the festival protocols! What must we do?"

"We must throw a massive Costume Party!" I declared triumpantly. "We will dress up in the most elaborate, unrecognizable outfits we can find! We will order the tailors to build six wooden mannequins, dress them in our normal royal clothes to fool the evil spirits into attacking them instead, and then we will leave the house and wander the town asking locals for 'Tricks or Treats'!"

"Tricks or Treats?" Bhima asked, suddenly leaning forward with intense interest. "Treats matlab... mithai? Khana? Muft da khana?!" *(Treats means... sweets? Food? Free food?!)*

"Yes, exactly, Bhima! Free food from strangers!"

"Main ready haan!" Bhima roared, standing up enthusiastically. "Tainu ki ban na hai? Main te ek bada wala Ravan bananga! Ya phir ek flying Samosa!" *(I am ready! What do you want to be? I will be a big Ravan! Or maybe a flying Samosa!)*

"A flying Samosa is terrifyingly perfect," I nodded. "Nakula, your flawless aesthetic is required for costume design. Arjuna, you need to disguise your tragic heroic jawline. And Yudhishthira, you can literally dress as a giant spreadsheet for all I care, as long as nobody can recognize your face!"

The princes threw themselves into the Halloween protocol with astonishing enthusiasm. For a group of highly trained ancient warriors, they were incredibly eager to abandon their Kshatriya dignity for the sake of free candy and spooky costumes. 

By nightfall, the transformation was complete. The results were deeply traumatic to look at. 

Bhima had utilized three mattresses, a massive amount of yellow silk, and Nakula’s premium makeup supplies to transform himself into a horrifying, deeply unsettling humanoid Samosa. His face was painted bright turmeric yellow, and he was physically wider than the hallway doors. 

Arjuna had somehow constructed a fully functional pair of giant, feathered wings and was walking around aggressively telling everyone he was the "God of Aerodynamics." 

Nakula, refusing to compromise his pores for a costume, had simply wrapped himself entirely in shimmering gold foil and declared himself a "flawless celestial mirror."

Yudhishthira, missing the point entirely, had dressed as a highly specific, ancient HR complaint form. He had pinned actual palm leaves to his robes detailing proper dining etiquette. 

Sahadeva was dressed identically to his normal outfit, but had put a bucket on his head and drawn two eyeholes, claiming he was currently "buffering."

And Queen Kunti... Queen Kunti had aggressively embraced the maternal monster archetype. She had wrapped herself in white bandages, claiming to be a resurrected mummy from the western deserts. 

"Perfect!" I said, looking at the team of idiots. "Now, the tailors have set up the mannequins in the bedrooms. The decoys are in place. When the evil spirits come tonight to consume our souls—or burn the house down with kerosene—they will attack the wooden dummies! The trick-or-treat infiltration protocol is active! Let us walk right out the front door!"

We stumbled out of the Lakshagriha, an apocalyptic group of trick-or-treaters looking absolutely insane. 

Purochana was standing by the front gate, directing a group of heavily armed guards carrying large, suspicious-looking pots of fiery liquid. When he saw our bizarre procession marching toward him, he physically recoiled in horror. 

"What... what in the name of Indra is this?" Purochana gasped, staring at Bhima the Samosa. 

"We are angry forest spirits!" I yelled, waving my arms wildly. "We are going to haunt the village! Do not disturb us, mortal!"

"Are the princes inside?" Purochana asked nervously, actively stepping out of our way. 

"Yes! They are sleeping heavily in their beds!" Sahadeva yelled from inside his bucket. "I foresee them sleeping very soundly through all localized temperature spikes!"

Purochana nodded, his slick real-estate smile returning. "Excellent! Have a wonderful haunting, spirits! Try not to start any... unscheduled fires!"

We successfully marched right out of the compound. The disguise had worked perfectly. By leaning aggressively into the absolute absurdity of the situation, we had completely bypassed the intense security parameters. 

We wandered down into the dense forest surrounding the town. 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima boomed loudly into the quiet ancient night, his turmeric-painted face glowing slightly in the moonlight. "Ithe koi ghar nahi hai! Main trick-or-treat kithe karunga?! Mainu mithi lassi chahidi hai! Mera Samosa suit badi garmi kar reha hai!" *(Hey Rahul! There are no houses here! Where will I do trick-or-treat?! I need sweet lassi! My Samosa suit is making me very hot!)*

"Keep your voice down, Bhima!" I hissed frantically, pulling him behind a large oak tree. "You're a giant pastry! If anyone hears that thick Punjabi accent coming from a baked good, our incognito status is instantly ruined!"

"Tainu meri accent te problem hai?!" Bhima argued enthusiastically. *(You have a problem with my accent?!)*

"Everyone shush," Arjuna whispered, raising a hand. "Look."

We turned around and looked back up the hill toward the Lakshagriha. 

Which brings us back to the forest, after our heavily costumed procession had successfully tricked Purochana and walked out the front gates. 

While we were waiting for the inevitable explosion of the Lakshagriha, Bhima had aggressively pursued the "Treats" portion of my Halloween mandate.

Despite my strict instructions to maintain a low profile in the dark woods, Bhima had stumbled upon a small group of highly confused, terrified traveling merchants camped near the edge of the trees. 

I watched in total horror as a massive, towering humanoid Samosa—painted entirely yellow and smelling heavily of turmeric—aggressively approached their campfire. 

"Oye mere shera!" Bhima bellowed joyfully at the screaming merchants. "Trick or Treat! Mainu treats deyo! Tuhade paas koi mithai hai?! Jalebi? Rabdi?!" *(Hey tigers! Trick or Treat! Give me treats! Do you have any sweets?! Jalebi? Rabdi?!)*

The merchants, firmly believing they were being accosted by an angry, demonic pastry god, immediately abandoned their camp and threw all their supplies at Bhima in sheer terror. 

Bhima happily gathered four large clay pots of fresh milk and a giant sack of unrefined jaggery, completely ignoring the men sprinting away into the night screaming about yellow demons. 

"Excellent haul," Bhima noted proudly, returning to our hiding spot just as the Lakshagriha finally ignited in the distance. 

It started slowly. A small, bright orange flicker in the eastern wing. Then, almost instantaneously, the heavily saturated lacquer caught the flame. 

*FWOOSH.*

A massive, terrifying pillar of fire erupted into the night sky. The entire structure of the holiday home violently ignited, the dried bamboo cracking like thunder as the flames consumed the mansion in seconds. The heat was so intense we could feel it radiating all the way down in the forest. 

The Pandavas stared in absolute, stunned silence. The reality of the assassination attempt finally shattered their vacation mindset. 

"Mother of God," Yudhishthira whispered, a palm leaf falling off his HR costume. "They didn't file the proper demolition paperwork. That is a flagrant violation of Article 8."

But it was Bhima who reacted violently. The Samosa suit suddenly ripped at the seams as his massive muscles tensed with unadulterated fury. His fists clenched so hard his knuckles popped. 

"Suyodhana!" Bhima roared, a sound that genuinely terrified the surrounding wildlife. "Ohne sadda ghar fook ditta! Main ohnu chadanga nahi! Ohdi brand-equity main mitti ch mila daanga!
