# Chapter 3: The Prophet and the IT Guy

I slept on a silk cushion the size of a yoga mat, under a tapestry depicting what appeared to be seventeen gods simultaneously winning at different sports, in a side chamber of the Hastinapur royal guest wing that a distressed palace servant had assigned me after the after-party wound down. The servant had taken one look at my jeans, my graphic tee, and my general aura of temporal displacement, and made the tactical decision to put me as far from the main royal quarters as architecturally possible.

The cushion was, objectively, the most expensive thing I had ever slept on.

I slept for approximately forty-five minutes.

This was partly because the palace woke up at an ungodly hour — the Hastinapur morning shift appeared to begin at around 4:30 AM. It was announced by the simultaneous crowing of approximately nine hundred roosters, the rhythmic pounding of grain in the kitchens, and what sounded strongly like Bhima doing intense cardiovascular exercise directly above my ceiling. He was singing a very aggressive Punjabi folk song about copious amounts of white butter, and the sheer baritone of his voice was making my expensive silk cushions physically vibrate across the marble floor.

But mostly I couldn't sleep because I had been informed by a bruised psychic prince that I was, through no fault of my own, marooned in 3200 BCE until further notice.

I lay on the silk cushion, staring at the ceiling, doing the most natural thing a software engineer does when confronted with a crisis he cannot control: I mentally filed a bug report.

*Bug ID: RahulSharma-001. Severity: P0 - Critical. Summary: User has been temporally displaced to incorrect epoch. Root cause: Uncle Jaggi's Bluetooth implementation. Affected system: Entire personal timeline. ETA for fix: Unknown. Assignee: One depressed prophet. Status: Blocked.*

I got up at 5 AM, accepted that sleep was not happening, and went to find Sahadeva.

***

The royal guest wing was a long, wide corridor lined with carved stone archways, each opening to a different chamber. It smelled of jasmine oil and old wood. The oil lamps along the walls had burned low overnight, casting everything in the dim, amber half-light of pre-dawn.

I found the first door I thought was Sahadeva's and knocked twice.

The door opened immediately, as if the person inside had been standing directly behind it.

I am not a man who is easily made to feel inadequate about his physical appearance. I have made peace with the hairline situation. I have accepted the 5'8" with dignity. I have even, on certain well-lit days, considered myself reasonably presentable.

Nakula destroyed all of that in approximately four seconds.

He pulled the door open entirely bare-chested, draped only in a silk dhoti that looked like it had been tailored by fashion-conscious celestial beings. He was Sahadeva's twin, but the universe had clearly decided to use one of them as the functional prototype and the other as the luxury final product. Nakula was, to use the only accurate word, unreasonably radiant. He had cheekbones that could cut glass, and his hair—at five in the morning, after a night of heavy drinking—looked like he had just stepped out of an expensive men's shampoo commercial. 

As if perfectly timed, a passing handmaiden carrying a tray of fresh linen took one look at his glistening bare torso, let out a soft, dramatic sigh, and completely fainted. The tray hit the floor with a loud clatter. Nakula didn't even blink. He just gracefully flicked a stray lock of hair perfectly back into place.

He looked at me for one long, silent beat, processing my crumpled graphic tee, my skinny jeans, and the sheer depressing aura of an IT guy who hadn't showered.

"Wrong room, thin-legs," Nakula flashed a blinding, million-dollar smile, and pleasantly closed the door.

I stood in the corridor for a moment, processing this.

I knocked on the next door.

***

Sahadeva's room was everything Nakula's was not.

It was small, spare, and aggressively functional. Every wall was covered from floor to ceiling in palm-leaf scrolls organized into dense, overlapping layers — not messily, but with the cold precision of a man who had categorized every piece of information he had ever received and filed it by subject, chronology, and degree of cosmic horror. A single oil lamp on the floor cast a tight circle of yellow light. The reed mat in the center was surrounded by fresh scrolls, an inkpot, a stylus, and what appeared to be the architectural equivalent of a doctoral dissertation rendered in ancient Sanskrit on pressed leaves.

Sahadeva was sitting cross-legged in the middle of all of this, wide awake, writing calculations. He had clearly not slept. He looked up at me when I entered with the expression of a man who has been expecting a particular pigeon to arrive and is moderately relieved it hasn't flown into a wall.

He also had, in the far corner of his chamber, partially disassembled, surrounded by its own separate stack of annotated palm-leaf notes, the Maruti 800.

My mouth opened. Nothing came out for several seconds.

"How," I finally managed. "How did you get that in here?"

"Forty servants," Sahadeva said, returning to his calculations. "I told them it was a sacred chariot of the gods."

"It has 'I AM HERE BECAUSE YOU BROKE SOMETHING' written on the back."

"They assumed it was a divine prophecy." He gestured at the reed mat across from him. "Sit down."

I sat, mostly because my legs weren't working correctly. I stared at the Maruti 800. Uncle Jaggi's catastrophic masterpiece had been carefully propped on wooden blocks. Every panel that could be removed had been removed and laid out in order on the stone floor. Every wire was labelled in Sanskrit. Every component had its own palm-leaf note, annotated in the same dense, precise handwriting that covered the walls. It looked like a crime scene where the detective had solved the crime but had decided to stay anyway out of sheer academic interest.

"You've been working on it," I said.

"Since midnight." Sahadeva dipped his stylus. "Your uncle's chronal mathematics are unexpectedly sophisticated. He has solved three problems in applied temporal mechanics that Vedic physicists won't formally attempt for another two thousand years. Using a Dell Inspiron and duct tape."

"He'll be very pleased to hear that."

"He is the most dangerous man in your timeline."

"He's currently wearing welding goggles over his reading glasses and talking to his refrigerator."

Sahadeva paused his writing for exactly one second. "As I said."

***

"I need you to ask me things," Sahadeva said, without looking up.

"What?"

"I cannot speak first. I cannot volunteer information. If you ask me something directly, I must answer honestly. If I try to say something unprompted—" He stopped. "Ask me something. Any question."

"Okay. How do I get home?"

"Not yet."

"WHAT."

"I mean the answer to that question is not yet determinable. Ask something else."

I stared at him. The curse mechanics were becoming clearer, and I did not like them. Sahadeva was a man sitting on approximately five thousand years' worth of the universe's most critical information, and I was a man who had to find the exact right way to phrase a question to unlock any of it. It was like being given access to an all-knowing oracle whose entire interface was a buggy, undocumented API.

"Okay," I said slowly. "How does the curse work, exactly? The mechanism."

"I can only answer what is directly and sincerely asked. I cannot offer, suggest, hint, imply, or volunteer. If the question is imprecise, I can only answer what the question technically asks. If you ask me 'is everything going to be fine,' the honest answer is no, but I am not able to tell you *why* without a more specific question. The curse does not distinguish intent. It only distinguishes whether you asked."

"So you're basically under oath. All the time. Since you were born."

Sahadeva looked at me briefly. "Yes."

"That sounds genuinely exhausting."

"Ask something else."

I thought for a moment. Then: "If I asked you, hypothetically, what line the payment gateway bug in my codebase was on—"

"Line 247."

I blinked. "So hypotheticals work?"

"Any direct question works. The conditional framing is irrelevant. You asked. I answered."

"Line 247," I repeated slowly. I had been staring at that bug for three weeks. Three weeks of late nights and Sumit's passive-aggressive progress checks and cold office coffee. Line 247. I felt a complex emotion that I can only describe as relief mixed with profound, retroactive fury at the universe.

"The loop does not account for null returns from the secondary database," Sahadeva added, apparently deciding that this additional context was technically within the bounds of a direct answer.

"THREE WEEKS," I said, my voice pitching slightly. "I have been staring at that for three weeks."

"I know." Something in his tone shifted — barely, imperceptibly, the way a building settles. Almost gently, he said: "It was almost physically painful to observe."

***

A servant appeared at the door with a wooden tray. Breakfast — a clay bowl of something that smelled like warm ghee and spiced grain, a stack of flatbreads, and a second bowl containing what appeared to be a meat preparation of some kind.

Sahadeva's eyes went to the second bowl.

He looked at it for two beats. Three. Then, without touching it, without commenting on it, without any change in expression whatsoever, he moved it to the very far edge of the mat. Pushed it approximately two feet away. Turned back to his scrolls as if this had not happened.

The bowl sat there in my peripheral vision for the rest of the morning like a small, unanswered question.

I thought about asking. I didn't ask.

"What is going to happen in my world?" I said instead. "In the near future. After I left."

Sahadeva set down his stylus.

"In your immediate near future," he said, with the flat precision of a man reading from a highly disappointing quarterly report, "two tech billionaires will challenge each other to a cage match, back out, and then sue each other over the rights to the promotional poster. Global trade is significantly disrupted because another container ship gets stuck diagonally in a canal. Also, they release another *Fast and Furious* movie where a car is successfully driven into low earth orbit using purely horsepower and family values."

I absorbed this. I had left in April 2026. Hearing the utter stupidity of global events described in the past tense by a 3200 BCE prophet was a specific kind of existential vertigo I had not previously experienced.

"Within four years of your departure," Sahadeva continued, "the majority of the work you perform daily is automated. Artificial intelligence systems handle backend integration, legacy code migration, and payment gateway development at a fraction of the cost and time. Your team is replaced by a chatbot."

"Great," I said. "So I'm being made redundant by robots."

"Your boss Sumit loses his job three months before you would have been promoted."

I sat up very straight. "I GET promoted?"

Sahadeva looked at me steadily.

"...Ask something else."

"NO. No, that is not an acceptable answer. You just told me I get promoted—"

"I told you when Sumit loses his job relative to when you would have been promoted. That is what you asked."

"That is the most technically correct and emotionally unsatisfying answer I have ever received."

Sahadeva returned to his stylus. "Welcome to my life."

***

I leaned forward. "Does Sumit call me a badger again?"

A pause so brief it was almost not a pause.

"In the Q3 planning meeting," Sahadeva said, "Sumit tells the assembled team, and I quote: 'Rahul, you are not a badger. I want to evolve that metaphor. You are a load-bearing wall. Essential to the structure. Completely invisible to anyone who doesn't understand construction. We don't celebrate load-bearing walls, Rahul, but we absolutely cannot function without them.' He then does finger guns and leaves the meeting."

I closed my eyes.

"He says 'load-bearing wall.'"

"With finger guns."

"I want to go home."

"I know."

"Also," I said, "your Netflix password is your mother's birthday, reversed. Change it," I said, anticipating him.

"Your Netflix password," Sahadeva said, exactly simultaneously, "is your mother's birthday, reversed. Change it."

We looked at each other.

"You were going to say that regardless of whether I asked," I said.

"I was waiting for you to ask something that gave me technical permission to include it."

"Is that how this works? You look for adjacent questions that let you slip in what you actually want to say?"

Sahadeva did not answer this. He looked at his calculations. This was, I realized, itself an answer.

***

"Did you watch movies?" I asked.

Sahadeva's stylus slowed almost imperceptibly. Just one degree of friction entering the motion. "In what sense."

"In my time. From your meditations. Did you follow pop culture?"

The stylus stopped.

He put it down with great care.

"A man named Salman Khan," Sahadeva said, and something shifted in the quality of his voice — as if he were discussing high theology, "takes off his shirt in a movie for the fifty-seventh consecutive time. The audience's response creates a minor seismic event measuring 2.1 on the Richter scale. The box office crosses five hundred crores. Your nation rejoices, ignoring the complete lack of a coherent plot."

I looked around slowly at the palace walls around us, thinking about Bhima crushing a chariot yesterday and Nakula's bare-chested routine at 5 AM.

"So basically, human entertainment hasn't evolved at all in five thousand years," I said.

"Correct." Sahadeva sighed deeply. "Though I do genuinely miss the CGI."

And then — barely audible, so quiet I almost thought I'd imagined it — Sahadeva started humming.

It wasn't much. A few bars. Soft and slightly melancholy, the way a man hums something when he isn't entirely aware he's doing it.

"What is that?" I asked.

He stopped. Looked faintly surprised, as if catching himself. "Mohammed Rafi. 1924 to 1980 CE. Greatest human voice ever recorded."

"You listen to Mohammed Rafi."

"I have listened to everything," Sahadeva said simply. "In meditations across the timeline. Every musician who ever lived. Every voice." A brief pause. "Mohammed Rafi brings me comfort."

The oil lamp flickered. Outside, the city was beginning its day. I thought about what it would mean to have heard every piece of music ever recorded — every composition, every concert, every voice — and to sit alone with all of it, in 3200 BCE, unable to tell anyone why you knew what you knew, or that you knew anything at all.

It was possibly the loneliest thing I had ever heard.

I didn't say this. I said: "Avengers: Doomsday. December 2026. Tell me everything."

Sahadeva went very still.

"Robert Downey Jr. comes back as—"

"Doctor Doom," Sahadeva said. "I am aware."

"You've known this for years."

"Yes."

"And you haven't been able to tell anyone."

"No."

"Tell me. The whole plot. Everything. I haven't seen it — it's not out yet when I left — just tell me."

A pause that lasted approximately three geological epochs.

"I cannot."

"I AM ASKING. I am directly, sincerely asking—"

"Revealing specific future entertainment outcomes to a viewer who has not yet experienced them creates localized temporal recursion loops. Your emotional reaction to the information would alter your behavioral patterns, which would alter your interactions in this timeline, which would compound the existing chronal anomaly by a factor of—"

"Are you," I said, very carefully, "citing a chronal Terms and Conditions document. To avoid giving me spoilers."

Sahadeva breathed in through his nose.

"The Russo brothers deliver," he said, in the tone of a man defusing a bomb with his bare hands while refusing to accept that the bomb exists.

"THAT IS NOT ENOUGH."

"The audience cries. That is all I will say."

"Does Tony—"

"THAT IS ALL I WILL SAY." He picked up his stylus with great finality and returned to his calculations, and his jaw was set with the precise expression of a man who has been sitting on Avengers: Doomsday spoilers for the better part of a decade and has decided, with tremendous personal sacrifice, to die with them.

I stared at the back of his head. "The mid-credits scene—"

"DO NOT."

"JUST THE TONE OF IT. IS IT HOPEFUL OR—"

"I am working on your car," Sahadeva said loudly, in the direction of the Maruti 800. "Please allow me to concentrate."

***

I was midway through a flatbread and deeply regretting not having pushed harder on the Doomsday question when I noticed the runner.

A young man appeared at the window of Sahadeva's chamber — not climbing through it, just arriving beside it with the efficient, unhurried momentum of someone who did this exact thing many times a day. He placed a small sealed clay tablet on the windowsill without a word and immediately left. Not urgently. Just... done.

"What was that?" I asked.

"A message."

"From who?"

Sahadeva read the tablet. A particular expression crossed his face — a mixture of profound exhaustion and the desire to throw a clay tablet out the window. "My brother Yudhishthira has forwarded a heavily plagiarized motivational quote about the nature of dharma to the family group. At five in the morning. Bhima has responded with a detailed, life-sized painting of a paratha. Nakula has replied with a painting of himself looking perfectly groomed. Arjuna has simply left the chat."

I put down the flatbread.

"You have a family group chat."

"I have seventeen groups."

"SEVENTEEN."

"The main family group. A sub-group for Pandava brothers only, which I was accidentally added to and from which I have not been removed. A group for tournament logistics. A group called 'Hastinapur Intellectual Discourse' which exclusively contains Yudhishthira forwarding old Bhishma speeches. A group called—"

"What is the main family group called?"

"WhatsApp."

I stared at him.

"Sorry," I said. "The group is called *WhatsApp*?"

"The entire platform is called WhatsApp."

The room was very quiet.

"Did you," I began.

"Yes."

"You invented—"

"I invented a rapid-message relay network. I named it WhatsApp because I had encountered the name in temporal meditation and found it appropriately meaningless to a Bronze Age audience." A pause. "A meaningless name builds no conceptual resistance. People adopt what they cannot categorize."

"And Dhritarashtra sends good morning messages at four in the morning."

"A lotus painting every day. The caption reads: *'Good morning, blessings of Brahma, rise and shine, remember your dharma, also: update me on the treasury numbers by noon.'*" Sahadeva paused. "It requires forty-seven simultaneous runners to deliver to all palace recipients. They leave the royal dispatch center at precisely 4 AM. You can hear them in the corridors."

"That's — that's why the palace wakes up at four-thirty," I said slowly. "It's not roosters. It's the push notification."

Sahadeva neither confirmed nor denied this. He appeared to consider it self-evident.

"But hold on," I said. My software engineer brain had been quietly loading in the background and was now arriving with a list of objections organized by severity. "WhatsApp. Instagram. YouTube. 40,000 users across the subcontinent. How. You don't have internet. You don't have servers. You have people, birds, and dirt roads. The message latency alone would make these completely unusable — a WhatsApp in this network would take three days to arrive. Instagram would be a painting that shows up next Tuesday. This does not work. This *cannot* work."

Sahadeva stood up. "Come here."

He led me to the window.

Below, in the pale gold of early dawn, Hastinapur's main avenue was alive in a way I hadn't registered as anything other than the general chaos of an ancient city waking up. But now, looking properly, I saw it.

Dozens of runners. Moving in every direction with a clipped, purposeful calm that had nothing random in it. Each one carrying a sealed packet — clay tablets, rolled leaf scrolls, small painted squares — each packet marked with a distinct sequence of colored symbols. At the junction where three avenues met, runners arrived and departed with mechanical precision, exchanging packets mid-stride without breaking pace. No confusion. No collisions. Four seconds per handoff.

"Watch the runner with the red and yellow mark," Sahadeva said.

I watched. Red-yellow arrived at the junction, pressed his packet into the hands of a waiting runner, turned, and headed back the way he came. The new runner accelerated east. The whole thing had taken the time it takes to blink twice.

"What am I looking at?"

"A distributed relay network. The city is partitioned into geographic sectors. Each runner owns exactly one sector — knows every lane, every household, every delivery point within it by memory. No runner travels more than eight hundred meters. Packets pass between sectors through fixed relay nodes. The system covers all of Hastinapur in under forty minutes. Inter-city routes scale the same logic outward — sector to sector, relay to relay — across the subcontinent. The average delivery time within Hastinapur is twenty-three minutes. Cross-kingdom: two days."

My mouth was open. "That's packet switching," I said. "That's literally how internet data routing works — break the message into packets, route each one through the nearest available node, reassemble at the destination—"

"The delivery accuracy is 99.9 percent."

"That's Six Sigma. That's — where did you *learn* this?"

Sahadeva turned from the window. "In meditation, I observed a network of delivery workers in a city called Mumbai. They deliver approximately two hundred thousand lunch boxes per day across a dense, chaotic metropolis. No technology. A color-coded geographic sector system. Near-perfect accuracy. Harvard Business School studies them formally in your century. They are called Dabbawaalas."

I stared at him.

"I had approximately five thousand years of lead time," Sahadeva said. "I used it."

"You copied homework from the Dabbawaalas."

"I referenced validated future research."

"And nobody questioned why you named it WhatsApp."

"People do not question names they cannot categorize," Sahadeva repeated, sitting back down. Then, with absolute composure, he picked up a fresh clay tablet, marked it in three swift strokes, and handed it to the window runner, who vanished.

"What did you just send?"

"A message to Bhima."

We waited. The dawn light moved across the floor in slow degrees. I ate another flatbread. Sahadeva wrote calculations. Somewhere far below us, I heard the rhythmic percussion of the relay network doing its invisible, miraculous work through the waking city.

Seventeen minutes later, a runner appeared at the window and placed a tablet on the sill.

Sahadeva held it up.

It was a painting. A small, meticulous, lovingly rendered painting of a towering stack of parathas, each one glistening with an amount of ghee that could generously be described as medically ambitious. Below it, in large, enthusiastic Punjabi script: *SUBAH BLESSINGS FROM YOUR BHIMA. EAT SOMETHING. GHEE IS LOVE.*

I looked at the painting for a long time.

"Did Bhima just reply to your WhatsApp with a good morning paratha painting."

"He does this every morning."

"To everyone in the family group?"

"To everyone in the family group. He has sent 4,380 consecutive paratha paintings across twelve years of operation. He has never sent anything else. Not a single message. Only parathas."

Something about this — the absolute, unconditional, bronze-age consistency of Bhima's good morning paratha — broke something loose in my chest. I started laughing. Really laughing, the kind that starts in your stomach and embarrasses you slightly. There was something so fundamentally, universally *human* about it — a giant ancient warrior prince who used a sophisticated distributed relay network built on future research about Mumbai lunch deliveries to send his family a painting of breakfast every single morning without fail.

Sahadeva watched me laugh. And something happened on his face — something small and careful. The corner of his mouth moved. Not quite a smile. The closest approximation of one that a man can manage when he is thirty-four years old and has known every terrible thing that is going to happen since he was approximately eight.

"The YouTube latency problem," I managed eventually. "How does Dronacharya have two hundred thousand views?"

"Trained scribes attend every performance. They produce detailed illustrated narrative scrolls — action-by-action accounts with drawings. These are distributed to all subscribers within twenty-four hours via the relay network. Subscribers receive the scroll, experience the content, and send written reactions via return message."

"YouTube comments are *letters*."

"Dronacharya has received fourteen thousand written comments on his tutorials. He has responded to none of them. He does not understand how to operate the reply function. He does not know the reply function exists." A pause. "Ashwatthama manages the comments section. He deletes the negative ones. He insists that he doesn't. He does."

"What's Dronacharya's channel called?"

Sahadeva closed his eyes briefly, as a man does when recalling something that causes him low-level ongoing pain.

"*Guru Drona's Unstable Archery Academy*. Tagline: 'Subscribe or face my wrath.' He means it. Three non-subscribers were made to run laps."

"And Instagram?"

"Chitralekha was my working title," Sahadeva said. "I named it Instagram. A platform for sharing paintings, sketches, and illustrated observations across the kingdom. It has 40,000 active users." He paused. "The most-followed account belongs to a woman in Magadha who exclusively posts paintings of her cows. Twelve thousand followers. She was recently approached by a ghee manufacturer for a sponsorship arrangement."

"You created an influencer."

"I created a platform for human expression and cross-regional connection." A beat. "Humans used it to argue about cows and post paintings of their lunch."

"What does everyone else post?"

"Bhima: extreme close-up paintings of food. Always captioned in Punjabi. He uses a very wide brush and paints extremely fast, which gives his food portraits an aggressive, almost threatening energy that his followers find compelling." Sahadeva's voice remained entirely flat. "Arjuna has the highest follower count in Hastinapur. He has posted nothing. He joined, commissioned a single portrait of himself in flattering afternoon light, used it as his profile image, and has not touched the platform since. People find this enigmatic. It increases his follower count by approximately two hundred per month."

"That is the most Arjuna thing I have ever heard."

"Karna posts inspirational quotations. About destiny. About being born unrecognized. About earning your place in a world that assigned you none." Something shifted, just slightly, in Sahadeva's delivery. "They are very popular." He paused. "He also posts sunsets. He paints them himself. He is genuinely talented."

The room was quiet for a moment.

"And you?" I asked. "What do you post?"

"Nothing." No hesitation. "I observe."

***

Later — the lamp long dead, the morning fully arrived, the sounds of the palace day bleeding through the walls — Sahadeva said a name.

"Vikarna."

He said it quietly, the way you say the name of something you've been thinking about for a while and have finally decided to release into the air.

"What about him?" I asked.

"He will be the only Kaurava who speaks against Draupadi's humiliation in the dice hall. The only one, in a room full of princes and elders, who argues for dharma when everyone else has decided that dharma is inconvenient."

"Good," I said, and then, carefully: "What happens to her? In the dice hall?"

Sahadeva was quiet for a moment. His voice, when it came, was measured in a particular way — the way you measure words when you are technically answering a question but being very selective about which part of the truth to give.

"What you know of the Mahabharata — from your history books, your school serials, your grandmother's Sunday television — is Vyasa's version of events."

"What does that mean?"

"It means a frustrated author working for a media company that needs dramatic content will make certain editorial choices. Draupadi is brilliant. She is a political mind of extraordinary caliber. She marries Arjuna. What happens in the dice hall is—" he paused precisely, "—considerably less dramatic than what Vyasa will eventually write."

I stared at him. "He's going to fabricate her humiliation."

"He will call it 'narrative dramatization for dramatic continuity and commercial viability.'"

"That is a PR stunt. He's running a PR stunt on the Mahabharata."

"That is what I said. In a room containing Bhishma, the five Pandavas, forty Kauravas, and three visiting dignitaries. I said it clearly, in complete sentences, with supporting evidence." Sahadeva looked at his calculations. "They smiled. They changed the subject. Later, Nakula said the same thing as a joke. Everyone laughed and asked him if he wanted more somras."

"And Vikarna?" I asked softly. "He speaks against it. And then?"

Sahadeva began to hum. Barely there, under his breath. Mohammed Rafi.

"Ask something else," he said.

I didn't ask something else for a moment. I sat with the weight of what hadn't been said, and it was heavier than anything that had been.

***

"Does anyone else know?" I asked eventually. "About everything you can see. Your brothers. Nakula."

"My twin is aware of the general capability. He considers it primarily useful for predicting the outcomes of horse races. This month he has won seventeen consecutive races. They have begun calling him 'The Horse Whisperer of Hastinapur.'" Sahadeva's delivery was so completely without affect that it took a moment to absorb the full devastation of it. "There are songs."

"Songs."

"His name is in them. In a very complimentary context. Emphasizing his natural instincts and his harmonious connection with animals." A pause. "People assume good fortune follows beautiful people. This is statistically inaccurate but emotionally convenient."

I looked at the wall of calculations. At seventeen years of chronal mathematics annotated in a room that nobody visited except one servant who was deeply afraid of the scrolls. 

"I told my brothers about the fire plot at Varnavat six months ago," Sahadeva said, in the same flat tone someone might use to describe a very minor administrative inconvenience. "They smiled and changed the subject. Two months ago, Nakula accidentally referenced the outcome of the dice game in a family dinner conversation. He immediately said, 'I mean — wild guess — who knows, anything could happen,' and everyone laughed and embraced him and told him he was very wise." He was quiet for a moment. "I have been telling them for years. He says it once, accidentally, while reaching for more rice, and they write a song about his instincts."

"That must be—" I started.

"Ask something else."

***

The scrolls cast long shadows in the midmorning light. The Maruti 800 sat in its corner in pieces, patient as a puzzle waiting to be solved. Somewhere outside, Bhima was apparently engaging in a spirited diplomatic disagreement with the kitchen staff about the paratha-to-ghee ratio for the midday meal, his voice carrying the kind of confident authority that only men who have never doubted themselves in their lives can manage at full volume.

It had been hours. It felt simultaneously like minutes and like several geological periods.

Sahadeva had, somewhere in the last hour, begun answering faster. Not dramatically — nothing about Sahadeva was dramatic. But where he had previously paused between answers with the careful deliberation of a man choosing which classified files to declassify, he was now occasionally answering before I had fully finished asking. Once, he had corrected a misconception I had before I'd even voiced it. He'd caught himself and gone quiet, and I'd pretended not to notice, and we'd both moved on.

"Can I ask you something personal?" I said.

"Everything is personal when you know everything."

"Are you okay? As a person. Carrying all of this."

The morning sounds continued outside. A cart. A vendor. The relay network's soft percussion of purposeful feet.

"I know every terrible thing that will happen," Sahadeva said, carefully, as though assembling something delicate. "I know exactly who will die and in what sequence and by whose hand. I know the dice in the game are loaded. I know nobody will formally ask me about this until it is too late for the information to be useful. I know the complete three-act structure of Avengers: Doomsday, including the post-credits scene." He paused. "Prior to your arrival, I had not spoken to anyone about any of it in—" A very long pause. "Some time."

"How long?" I asked.

He looked at the wall of calculations.

"Ask something else."

***

"Okay," I said finally, feeling warmer than I had since landing in a haystack outside Hastinapur. "Back to practical matters. The car. How long until you can send me home?"

Sahadeva picked up the calculation scroll. Held it.

"The chronal vectors require a specific temporal convergence to realign. The Maruti's charge can be reinstated, but I can only safely transmit you back once the primary timeline event that this temporal breach is anchored to has fully concluded. The residual chronal signature is too large. Transmitting early would—" He stopped. "It would be problematic."

"Which event?" I said. "The primary event."

"The war."

Silence.

I heard Bhima, far below, winning his paratha negotiation.

"The Kurukshetra war," I said.

"Yes."

"When is that?"

A very long pause. Sahadeva picked up his stylus. He began, with great deliberation, to write calculations that I was now reasonably certain he had already completed.

"There is the matter of Draupadi's swayamvar to occur first. Then the establishment of Indraprastha — construction alone takes some years. Then the dice game, which has not yet occurred. Then the exile—"

"The exile," I said. "How long is the exile?"

Sahadeva wrote a line of calculation.

"Thirteen years."

The word landed in the room and did not move.

"Sorry," I said. "You said—"

"Thirteen years."

"For the exile alone."

"Yes. And then failed peace negotiations. Then Kurukshetra, which runs eighteen days of active combat. And then post-war chronal stabilization before the temporal field is safe for transmission." He put down the stylus. "The total duration from today is approximately thirteen years. Give or take six months."

The room was very quiet.

The oil lamp had been dead for hours. The morning light was doing what morning light does, which is to make everything look precisely as it is, which was not helping.

Bhima's voice filtered up from below, victorious about the parathas.

I said, very softly: "My Netflix subscription is going to auto-renew fourteen times."

Sahadeva said nothing.

He began to hum.

*Kya Hua Tera Wada.* Soft. Low. The same melody that had floated from behind a tapestry at a barbecue twelve hours ago, while a bruised psychic winked at a terrified software engineer across a dust-filled arena.

I looked at the disassembled Maruti 800 in the corner. At the forty-volume palm-leaf dissertation on Uncle Jaggi's wiring. At the wall of calculations that went back, I now understood, not weeks or months but years.

"You've known this since last night," I said. "Since you pulled me behind the tapestry."

The humming continued.

"You pulled me aside at the party specifically so you could tell me I was stuck here."

Still humming.

"Sahadeva." I kept my voice level, which required some effort. "How long have you known I was coming?"

The melody hung in the air.

"Ask something else," he said.

And outside, distantly, the relay network of ancient Hastinapur carried its morning messages through the waking city — forty-seven lotus paintings, fourteen thousand YouTube comments, twelve thousand cow paintings, and one paratha, delivered with 99.9 percent accuracy across a network five thousand years ahead of its time — built by a man who had been waiting, alone, for someone to finally ask him the right questions.
