# Chapter 9: The Hippies of Americanapur and The Lord's Arrival

Securing time off for a mythological rescue mission in the Bronze Age was surprisingly identical to navigating modern HR portals in a mid-level tech firm. 

The Pandavas needed to go to Panchaal for Draupadi’s Swayamvar, but we couldn't just walk out of Indraprastha. We were all contractually bound by King Takshaka’s aggressive employment policies. So, early on Monday morning, I gathered the brothers in the main antechamber of the royal palace to formally submit our collective annual leave request to the King. 

Takshaka was seated on his emerald throne, aggressively signing trade decrees while Nakula meticulously trimmed the monarch’s beard with tiny silver scissors. 

"Leave?" Takshaka asked, not looking up from a scroll, raising one perfectly manicured eyebrow. "You have only been employed for six months. You haven't triggered your mandatory accrual vesting period."

"Your Majesty," I stepped forward, holding a highly formatted piece of palm leaf that I had structured exactly like a modern PTO request form. "We are requesting an immediate week-long offsite. The team has been under tremendous pressure. Bhima’s kitchen metrics are off the charts, Yudhishthira’s bureaucratic output has paralyzed three rival states, and Arjuna has officially driven two of your assassins to early retirement through sheer stress."

"We require a psychological reset," Yudhishthira agreed smoothly, adjusting his glasses. "We are requesting a retreat to a localized hill station. For a change in atmospheric pressure and improved lung capacity."

Takshaka stopped signing. He looked at us, his dark, calculating eyes scanning our faces. He knew exactly who we were, and he knew a "hill station retreat" was a massive fabrication. The stock markets in Panchaal regarding the Swayamvar were active; he definitely suspected our true motive.

But as a ruthless politician, he also knew that keeping his most competent staff happy prevented high turnover rates. 

"Very well," Takshaka drawled, casually flicking his wrist. Nakula instantly paused his trimming to avoid a fatal scissor incident. "I approve the vacation. However, the HR guidelines are clear. I will authorize exactly five days of paid leave. If your... 'hill station excursion'... extends beyond day six, you will be placed on Leave Without Pay (LWP). Is that understood?"

"LWP?" Yudhishthira gasped, violently offended by the concept of unpaid labor. "But the royal ordinance—"

"Furthermore," Takshaka interrupted sharply, pointing a jeweled finger at me. "I expect you to remain reachable. My intelligence network relies on highly trained, venomous cobras currently deployed across the subcontinent. If my treasury department has any doubts regarding your amortization schedule, a snake will drop from a tree and hiss the query at you. You are required to respond within a three-hour SLA."

"An SLA managed via snakes," I muttered, rubbing my temples. "Understood. We will have our out-of-office autoreplies physically tied to our wrists."

"Excellent. Go touch some grass. But return before my audit is due," Takshaka dismissed us with a wave. 

The moment we were safely back in our suburban compound in Nayada, the real preparation began. And it was highly complicated. 

"We cannot just walk into Panchaal," Arjuna stated, pacing the dusty courtyard. "The Kauravas will be there. Drupad will be there. We are supposed to be dead, tragic ashes moldering in Varnavata. If we arrive looking like Kshatriya princes, the entire geopolitical conspiracy collapses, and Takshaka will sell our coordinates to the highest bidder."

"Vyasa’s original draft stated that we should disguise ourselves as poor Brahmins," Sahadeva noted, looking up from his meditation mat. "We were supposed to wear simple dhotis, cover ourselves in ash, and act extremely humble."

"Humble?!" Bhima roared in pure Punjabi outrage. "Main koi garib Brahmin kyu bana?! Mera biceps dekhe ne tussi?! Main Brahmin banunga taan log kahenge eh pandit gym kithe janda hai!" *(Why should I become a poor Brahmin?! Have you seen my biceps?! If I become a Brahmin, people will ask where this priest goes to the gym!)*

Bhima had a point. There was no amount of ash in the ancient world that could successfully hide the fact that he was built like a mythological tank. And Nakula fundamentally refused to cover his glowing, perfectly exfoliated skin in dirt. 

"We need a disguise," I said, pacing rubbing my chin. "Something so absurd, so entirely out of context for the Bronze Age, that nobody will even question who you are. They will just assume you are crazy."

The idea hit me with the force of a speeding truck on the Delhi-Noida Expressway. 

"I've got it," I announced excitedly. "You aren't going to be Brahmins. You are going to be foreigners. Exotic travelers from a distant, highly confusing land located thousands of miles to the West. A land of complete chaos, strange clothing, and extremely high fructose corn syrup."

"What land is this?" Yudhishthira asked curiously. 

"You are going to be Hippies," I declared, pointing at them. "From the fabled, democratic utopia of... Americanapur." 

"Americanapur?" Arjuna repeated the word, rolling it around his tongue. "It sounds heavily industrialized. What is the tactical advantage of this disguise?"

"The advantage is that nobody expects anything from you," I grinned enthusiastically. "I am going to design your entire wardrobe. You are going to speak a foreign dialect. You will be entirely unidentifiable."

For the next twenty-four hours, I utilized every piece of cheap linen, vegetable dye, and crude stitching needle in Nayada to transform the legendary Pandavas into the worst tourist stereotypes of the 1970s. 

It was a visual tragedy. 

I cut their flowing royal robes into what were essentially aggressively bright tie-dye t-shirts and extremely short cargo shorts. I used crushed berries to dye Nakula and Arjuna’s hair a strange, patchy blonde-white. And for Bhima, who needed the most distraction, I used dark henna to draw massive, fake tribal tattoos entirely down both of his massive arms. 

"What does this symbol mean?" Bhima asked proudly, flexing a bicep that now had a crude drawing of an anchor and a heart containing the word "MOM".

"It signifies naval dominance," I lied smoothly. "Now, the most important part is the accent. You cannot speak pure, formal Sanskrit. You have to speak English. But since none of you know English, you just have to fake an incredibly annoying, heavily nasal accent and occasionally say the word 'Bro' or 'Awesome'."

"Osom," Yudhishthira tried, pronouncing it like a slow, painful sneeze. "Like this?"

"Close enough. Just stay quiet and let me do the talking. I am your local travel guide."

The transformation was absolute. When we finally marched out of Nayada, we didn't look like the greatest heroes of the epoch. We looked like a group of wealthy, deeply confused foreign exchange students who had gotten hopelessly lost on their way to a spiritual retreat in Rishikesh. 

Our journey to Panchaal took three days, and the disguise paid immediate dividends when we arrived at the massive, sprawling tent city erected outside the capital for the Swayamvar. The place was absolutely packed with royals, merchants, and scholars from every corner of Bharata. 

Nobody looked twice at the "dead" Pandavas. They just stared in sheer, baffled horror at their shorts. 

While trying to navigate the chaotic outer markets to find the main arena, we accidentally bumped into a local Panchaal Brahmin who was carrying a large stack of ceremonial fire-wood. 

"Watch it, you painted hooligans!" the Brahmin snapped, dropping a log. He was a small, intensely angry man with heavily oiled hair and an incredibly judgmental stare. 

"Like, chill out, my dude," Arjuna attempted smoothly, leaning against a tent pole and flicking his blonde-dyed hair. "We are just soaking in the local vibes. Osom."

The Brahmin stared at Arjuna’s cargo shorts and tie-dye shirt in absolute, unadulterated disgust. 

"What in the name of the Vedic gods is wrong with your dialect?" the Brahmin demanded, thoroughly offended. "And why are your knees entirely exposed?! Are you suffering from a severe textile shortage?! Who are you people?"

"Greetings, wise scholar," I stepped forward quickly, putting a hand on my chest and bowing. "Forgive my clients. They are wealthy, deeply spiritual seeking tourists from the distant, western shoreline of Americanapur. They are... 'Hippies'. Here to witness the cultural majesty of the Swayamvar."

"Americanapur?" The Brahmin snorted derisively, looking us up and down. "Never heard of it. Sounds like a place where they don't teach basic grammar. My name is Nathu. I am the chief catering priest for the third tier of the stadium. And since you look like you possess absolutely zero brain cells, if you want to see the arena, you can carry my firewood."

"Listen here, little man," Bhima growled, his aggressive Punjabi accent violently breaking through the fake American slang. "Main tenu ithe hi... I mean... You wanna go, bro?! What the hell man! Sada dimaag kharab na kar nahi taan teri lakkad tere muh te maar danga! Peace out!" *(I will right here... You wanna go, bro?! What the hell man! Don't spoil my mood otherwise I will hit this wood on your face! Peace out!)*

It was the most terrifying linguistic combination I had ever heard. An aggressive Punjabi-American-Hippie accent delivered by a tattooed giant. 

Nathu the Brahmin actually took a step back, looking genuinely alarmed at Bhima’s horrific grammar. 

"Control your massive, tattooed idiot!" Nathu yelled at me, pointing a shaky finger at Bhima. 

"He's just jet-lagged!" I panicked, desperately shoving Bhima backward while Sahadeva grabbed his other arm. "He hasn't had his morning avocado! Please, Nathu-ji, lead the way. We will carry the wood."

For the next hour, we tramped through the festival grounds, with the greatest warriors of Hastinapur being actively bullied and forced to carry firewood by an aggressively short, highly judgmental priest. Nathu never stopped insulting our "Americanapur" culture, completely unaware that he was verbally abusing the men who were about to hijack his kingdom's most sacred event. 

When we finally pushed through the massive bronze gates of the central arena, the sheer scale of the event hit us. The stadium was gigantic, built to hold tens of thousands of people. The stands were packed with roaring crowds, and the central pavilion was overflowing with practically every crowned head in the ancient world. 

And directly in the center, sitting on a massive, highly elevated throne looking deeply, profoundly unenthusiastic, was King Drupad. 

The Swayamvar was about to begin.

Nathu unceremoniously dumped us in the standing-room-only section near the eastern bleachers, aggressively telling us not to "pollute the ceremonial geometry" with our cargo shorts. 

We blended into the massive, surging crowd, trying to maintain a low profile. But in an arena filled with heavily armored royalty, a group of brightly colored hippies naturally drew a few confused glances. 

I scanned the VIP pavilions. The Kauravas had secured the absolute best seating right near the royal dais. Suyodhana was visibly ignoring the massive, highly complex bow mounted in the center of the arena. Instead, he was aggressively whispering to Dushasana, loudly pointing at different sections of the crowd, clearly strategizing how to turn a failed archery attempt into a successful political networking opportunity. 

Next to him sat Karna, his arms crossed over his golden armor, scanning the crowd with an intense, deeply suspicious gaze. He was looking for the Pandavas. I yanked Arjuna’s hood lower just to be safe. 

But as my eyes swept across the Kaurava bench, I locked eyes with Vikarna. 

The youngest, surprisingly rational Kaurava prince paused mid-sip from a silver goblet. He stared directly at my blue t-shirt and modern digital watch. He didn't look confused. He just smiled slightly and gave me a very deliberate, highly suspicious wink. 

My stomach plummeted. Vikarna knew. He didn't know *why* we looked like unemployed beach bums, but he knew exactly who was under the tie-dye. 

Before I could process the panic of Vikarna’s wink, a heavy, deeply chilling hand clamped onto my shoulder from behind. 

"Ah," a raspy, horribly familiar voice whispered directly into my ear. "The elusive CEO returns. And wearing such... avant-garde branding."

I slowly turned around. 

Standing right behind us, having somehow slipped through the dense crowd without making a sound, was Shakuni. The King of Gandhara was dressed in dark silks, leaning on his carved cane, a deeply malicious smile playing on his lips. 

"Shakuni," I choked out, trying to sound like a confused American. "Like... totally wrong guy, bro. I am just a traveler from Americanapur."

Shakuni chuckled, a dry sound like bones rattling together. He didn't say a word. He simply raised his left hand, slowly pulling back his silk sleeve to reveal his palm. 

Carved into the skin of his palm, glowing with an uncomfortable, binding mystical energy, were the exact terms of the MBHT Media startup contract. *5,000 Years. Non-Compete.* 

"Hippies from Americanapur," Shakuni drawled sarcastically, looking at the poorly disguised Pandavas behind me. "Fascinating. I always knew Yudhishthira had the legs for short trousers. Do not worry, Rahul. Your secret is safe with your chief investor. For now." 

He melted back into the crowd before Arjuna could draw a concealed dagger, leaving me sweating profusely. 

A loud trumpet blast echoed through the stadium, instantly completely halting the chaotic noise of ten thousand people. 

On the central dais, King Drupad stood up. He looked haggard. The fire at Varnavata had deeply impacted his strategic planning. He had lost his designated son-in-law, and he was currently staring out at a crowd of men he largely despised. 

"Welcome," King Drupad announced, his voice booming across the arena but completely lacking enthusiasm. "To the Swayamvar of my daughter, Princess Panchali. We are gathered here today to find a warrior worthy of her intellect and strategic brilliance."

He paused, letting out a heavy, deeply unprofessional sigh. 

"I must admit, my heart isn't fully in this," Drupad complained loudly to the entire stadium. "I had already found the perfect groom. A visionary! A man who understood third-party vendor outsourcing and lateral infantry paradigms! But sadly, Rahul was tragically incinerated in a poorly zoned suburban arson attack."

I winced. Yudhishthira aggressively elbowed my side. 

"But we must move forward," Drupad waved his hand dismissively. "My son will explain the barbaric physical challenge you must complete because apparently, geopolitical alliances are still decided by who can shoot a stick the hardest."

Dhrishtadyumna stepped forward, looking vastly more impressive than his depressed father. He pointed to the massive, intricately carved bow resting on the center pedestal, and then pointed up. 

High above the arena, suspended completely out of normal bow-range, was a small, golden target spinning violently. Below it was a shallow, wide pool of dark oil. 

"The rules are absolute!" Dhrishtadyumna yelled. "You must string the divine bow of Shiva! You must select five arrows! And you must shoot the golden target! But... you may only look at its reflection in the oil! He who succeeds shall claim the princess!"

The crowd erupted in a mixture of awe and immediate complaining. It was an impossible shot. 

But before the first king could step forward to inevitably embarrass himself, the entire energy of the stadium shifted. 

It didn't happen with a trumpet blast or a grand announcement. It happened with a profound, sudden wave of absolute, spiritual silence. The air itself seemed to vibrate with a sudden, overwhelming sense of peace. The aggressive heat of the stadium vanished, replaced by the scent of lotus flowers and petrichor. 

The heavy, golden gates on the southern end of the arena slowly swung open. 

A chariot rolled into the stadium, pulled by four magnificent white horses. 

Standing in the chariot, radiating an aura of such intense, majestic divinity that my knees instantly felt weak, was Lord Krishna. 

He was dressed in vibrant yellow silks, a peacock feather resting perfectly on his crown. His dark skin seemed to literally absorb the sunlight and reflect it back as a profound, calming energy. He didn't just look like a handsome king; he looked like the absolute center of the universe. Standing next to him was his massive, incredibly intimidating older brother, Lord Balarama. 

For the first time since I arrived in 3200 BCE, my cynical, sarcastic, corporate survival-instincts completely vanished. 

Tears immediately pricked my eyes. I was a man from 2026. I had grown up hearing his stories, watching his serials on Sunday morning television, listening to my grandmother sing his bhajans. And here he was. Alive. Real. Breathing the same air. 

My vision blurred. A profound, overwhelming urge to fall face-first into the dirt and weep hit me. I took a shaky step forward, raising my hands to bow. 

"Control yourself," Sahadeva hissed urgently, grabbing my t-shirt collar and hauling me violently back into the crowd of commoners. 

"It's him," I choked out, a tear sliding down my cheek, fighting Sahadeva’s grip. "Sahadeva, it's the Lord. I have to touch his feet. I have to ask him why my code never compiled on the first try." 

"Rahul, pull it together!" Sahadeva whispered furiously, shaking me. "We are incognito! And more importantly, practically nobody in this stadium knows he is the avatar of Vishnu! To 90% of these kings, he is just a very clever, highly influential ruler wrapped in excellent PR! If you start screaming about his divinity in cargo shorts, you will ruin everything!"

I took a deep, shuddering breath, aggressively wiping my eyes. Sahadeva was right. This was his political era, long before the ultimate revelation of the Gita. 

Lord Krishna’s chariot stopped near the highest VIP pavilion. Every king, prince, and warrior in the stadium stood up and bowed respectfully to the ruler of Dwarka. Even Princess Draupadi, sitting quietly on her balcony, offered a deep, reverent bow. 

Krishna smiled his famous, devastatingly charming smile, waving to the crowd. Balarama just crossed his massive arms and looked bored. 

But as Krishna took his seat, his dark, beautiful eyes slowly swept over the massive crowd. He completely bypassed the front rows. He bypassed Suyodhana and Karna. His gaze tracked perfectly across the stadium, cutting through thousands of people, and locked directly onto our specific patch of standing-room bleachers. 

Lord Krishna looked directly at the Pandavas in their ridiculous Hippie disguises. 

A deeply mischievous, knowing sparkle flashed in his eyes. And then, his gaze shifted slightly, landing squarely on me. 

The Lord of the Universe looked directly at the time-traveling IT guy from Noida. And he gave me a very small, incredibly amused smile. 

My soul practically left my body. He knew. He absolutely knew. 

"He sees us," Yudhishthira whispered nervously, adjusting his fake blonde wig. "Should we... should we wave?"

"Don't wave, you idiot," Bhima muttered, aggressively flexing his tattooed arms. "Act cool! Like an Americanapur local! *Waassup, Lord!*"

"Do not say 'Waassup' to the Supreme Being!" I hissed frantically. "Just stand still!"

The tournament began immediately after Krishna took his seat. And it was a spectacular display of absolute aristocratic failure. 

King after king stepped forward. Most couldn’t even lift the divine bow off the pedestal. Those who did manage to lift it were violently thrown backward when they attempted to string it, the sheer tension of the wood acting like a coiled spring of humiliation. 

Then, it was Suyodhana’s turn. 

The Kaurava prince strode to the center of the arena, his armor gleaming. But instead of approaching the bow, he turned to face the incredibly massive, captive audience. This was his moment. This was the PR recovery campaign he desperately needed after the Varnavata disaster. 

Suyodhana cleared his throat loudly. 

"Citizens of Bharata!" Suyodhana bellowed, practically ignoring the matrimonial challenge behind him. "As I look around this arena, I do not just see a competition for a princess! I see an opportunity for structural unity! For too long, our internal supply chains have been blocked by archaic trade tariffs! Listen to my vision for the future! Join my network!"

He pulled out a massive scroll, prepared to deliver a heavily audited political manifesto. 

"Prince Suyodhana!" King Drupad yelled furiously from his throne. "This is a Swayamvar, not a TED Talk! String the bow or return to your seat!"

"But my polling numbers require a public statement!" Suyodhana protested indignantly. 

"Go back to your seat!" Drupad roared. 

Suyodhana aggressively threw his scroll on the ground and stormed back to his pavilion, having completely failed to string the bow or repair his public image. 

The crowd went quiet. There was only one man left in the royal seating who had the physical strength to even attempt the shot. 

Karna stood up. He walked calmly to the center of the arena. He didn't deliver a speech. He simply looked at the heavy bow. He reached out, lifted the massive weapon with one hand, and effortlessly slid the bowstring into place. The stadium erupted in gasps of absolute shock. 

Karna looked down at the pool of oil. He picked up an arrow. 

"Wait!" 

A sharp, authoritative voice cut through the silence. Princess Draupadi stood up on her balcony, her eyes flashing fiercely. 

"I will not accept this suitor!" Draupadi shouted, entirely breaking protocol. 

Karna froze, looking up at the balcony, clearly offended. "Why not, Princess? I have the physical strength. Are you rejecting me because of my caste? Because of my background?"

"I am rejecting you because you are already married, you absolute lunatic!" Draupadi yelled back, gesturing aggressively at him. "Your wife Vrushali is lovely! I am not entering a polygamous corporate merger just because you have an obsessive need to prove your archery metrics!"

"I don't even want to marry you!" Karna yelled back defensively, shifting his weight. "I just wanted to prove that my refractive targeting is mathematically superior! Just let me take the shot for the sportsmanship of it!"

"No! Put the bow down!"

"I am taking the shot!" Karna argued. 

Without even looking down at the oil, Karna aggressively yanked the bowstring back, aimed purely by instinct, and fired an arrow into the sky. It missed the golden target by an incredibly narrow margin and embedded itself deeply into the royal canopy right above King Drupad’s head. 

"Alright! I missed! Are you happy?!" Karna yelled angrily at Draupadi. He threw the bow back onto the pedestal and stormed toward the exit, muttering fiercely about wind interference. 

Drupad stared at the arrow trembling an inch above his skull, looking incredibly tired. 

"Is there no one else?" Dhrishtadyumna yelled out over the chaos. "Is there no warrior in this entire stadium capable of claiming the Princess of Panchaal?!"

This was the moment. The narrative required it. 

I aggressively shoved Arjuna forward. 

"Go, bro," I whispered out of the corner of my mouth. "It's time to disrupt the market."

Before Arjuna could casually stroll out of the crowd, our aggressively hostile tour guide intervened. 

Nathu the Brahmin practically shoved Arjuna into the center aisle. 

"Look at this absolute state of moral decay!" Nathu squeaked loudly at the stadium guards, pointing at Arjuna’s cargo shorts. "This tourist has been complaining about my firewood techniques all morning! Throw him out!" 

"Are you entering the challenge, foreigner?!" Dhrishtadyumna yelled down from the stage, pointing his sword at Arjuna. 

Arjuna casually adjusted his patchy blonde wig. "Like, totally, my dude. The aerodynamic variables are extremely favorable right now."

The entire stadium went completely silent. Thousands of kings stared at the incredibly muscular, poorly disguised hippie casually walking toward the divine bow of Shiva. 

"Oye Arjuna," Bhima aggressively stage-whispered from the bleachers, forgetting to use his hippie slang. "Jaldi maar nishana! Mainu badi zor di bhukh lagi hai! Nathu de lakkad chak chak ke mere muscles thak gaye ne!" *(Hey Arjuna, shoot the target quickly! I am very hungry! Carrying Nathu's wood has tired my muscles!)*

"Like, chill out, bro!" Yudhishthira yelled back at Bhima, trying desperately to sound American while citing legal precedent. "You must respect the procedural timeline, dude! Clause 4 clearly states the authorized combatant must have a moment of spiritual alignment before discharging the kinetic weapon! Cowabunga!"

I buried my face in my hands. It was the worst cultural infiltration in human history. 

Beside me, Nakula was completely ignoring the tension of the moment. He had found a small, highly polished bronze shield left behind by a fleeing king and was currently staring intensely into it, adjusting the fake lighting on his cheekbones. 

Arjuna reached the center pedestal. He looked at the divine bow. He didn't hesitate. With one smooth, fluid, terrifyingly powerful motion, he lifted the bow and snapped the heavy string into place. The sound echoed through the stadium like a thunderclap. 

The crowd gasped. 

Arjuna didn't even look up at the spinning golden target. He looked down into the dark pool of oil. He selected five arrows from the quiver, nocked them simultaneously—a physically impossible feat of archery—and drew the bowstring back to his ear. 

He paused for exactly one second. 

"Refractive index calculated," Arjuna muttered softly to himself. "Adjusting for lateral wind-shear."

He released. 

The five arrows shot upward with terrifying speed. They hit the spinning golden target simultaneously, not just piercing it, but fundamentally destroying it. The golden pieces rained down into the pool of oil with a dramatic splash. 

The stadium erupted. It was a roar of absolute, chaotic disbelief. A random tourist from Americanapur had just humiliated the entire ruling class of Bharata. 

Draupadi stood up on her balcony, her jaw slightly dropped. She stared down at Arjuna. She was incredibly confused, but as her eyes trailed over his broad shoulders and the effortless way he held the massive bow, she clearly wasn't disappointed. 

She turned rapidly to her sister, Shikhandi, who was standing nearby. 

"Shikhandi," Draupadi whispered loudly enough for the acoustics of the stadium to carry her voice. "This is perfect! Look at him! He is wearing entirely un-aerodynamic clothing, yet he possesses flawless executive execution! Do you know what this means?"

"That you just married a peasant with terrible hair dye?" Shikhandi asked dryly. 

"No! This means I have secured a foreign exchange program!" Draupadi clapped her hands excitedly. "If I marry this foreigner from... wherever he is from... I get a completely unobstructed pathway to hone my global marketing and CEO skills in a completely new international territory! It's the ultimate corporate lateral move! I won't have to deal with Hastinapur's archaic bureaucracy!"

Draupadi practically ran down the stairs of the royal dais, carrying the matrimonial garland. She reached Arjuna and confidently threw it around his neck, actively ignoring the fact that his t-shirt smelled like cheap tie-dye and Nathu's firewood. 

"Greetings, mysterious suitor," Draupadi said, giving him a sharply calculating, highly capable smile. "I look forward to restructuring your financial portfolios."

"My draw-weight was optimal," Arjuna replied happily. It was a match made in hyper-competent heaven. 

I immediately jogged out into the arena with the other Pandavas, firmly surrounding Arjuna to ensure no angry kings tried to assassinate our newly acquired CEO. 

"Who are you people?!" King Drupad yelled, rushing down from the throne, looking absolutely horrified by his new son-in-law's cargo shorts. 

"We are from Americanapur, Your Majesty," I lied loudly, shaking Drupad's hand. "Very wealthy. Massive agricultural holdings. Huge fans of your daughter's leadership potential."

Drupad looked like he was going to cry, but the rules of the Swayamvar were absolute. The celebrations officially began. 

The reception that evening in the grand Panchaal pavilion was incredibly awkward. The defeated kings of Bharata were aggressive and heavily intoxicated. The Kauravas were sulking in a dark corner, Suyodhana furiously rewriting his PR manifesto while Karna stared moodily into his wine cup. 

And then, just as I was trying to secure a plate of appetizers, the heavy cane tapped my ankle. 

Shakuni pulled me behind a large decorative pillar, out of sight of the main party. His dark eyes glittered with absolute capitalist malice. 

"A brilliant play, Rahul," Shakuni whispered. "Securing Draupadi’s administrative brilliance for your little startup. Very stealthy. Unfortunately, I hold absolute veto power over your future." He raised his palm, the 5,000-year contract glowing faintly. 

"What do you want, Shakuni?" I sighed in defeat. 

"We are renegotiating the terms of your existence," Shakuni smiled his terrible smile. "I will keep quiet about the 'dead' Pandavas being terrible hippies. But... the contract is extended to 10,000 years. And your equity stake drops to 5%."

"Fine," I agreed quickly, shaking his hand, mentally cursing ancient corporate logic. "We have a deal."

As Shakuni limped away, laughing to himself, I finally let out an exhausted breath. The stress of managing these geopolitical nightmares was destroying my health. 

"You look troubled, time-traveler." 

The voice was soft, incredibly melodious, and completely bypassed my ears to resonate directly in the center of my skull. 

I spun around. 

Standing entirely alone in the shadows of the pillar, far away from his massive entourage and the celebrating crowd, was Lord Krishna. 


He was no longer projecting the overwhelming, stadium-silencing aura of the Supreme Being. He just looked like a deeply amused, incredibly handsome prince who had successfully snuck away from a boring networking event. 

I instantly dropped to my knees, aggressively swiping at my cargo shorts, desperately trying to touch his feet in the traditional sign of absolute reverence. 

"Oh, get up, Rahul," Lord Krishna laughed softly, stepping back so my hands just hit the marble floor. "The cargo shorts really ruin the dramatic reverence of the gesture. Besides, we are incognito in a dark corner. If you start aggressively weeping at my feet, people will think I am attempting to aggressively acquire Americanapur."

I scrambled back to my feet, my heart pounding against my ribs like a trapped bird. "My Lord. You... you know me."

"I know everything," Krishna smiled, his dark eyes sparkling with infinite mischief. "For instance, I know that when you were seven years old in 2005, you accidentally swallowed a one-rupee coin because you thought it would make you highly magnetic."

I blushed violently. "That was... a scientific experiment."

"I also know," Krishna continued, casually leaning against the decorative pillar, "that you once faked a stomach ache for three consecutive days just to avoid a mandatory company HR seminar regarding 'Synergistic Output Metrics'. And finally, I know that your secret password for your central banking application is literally just 'Password123!', which frankly, offends me on a cosmic level."

"I am changing it immediately upon my return," I stammered, deeply humiliated by the Lord of the Universe critiquing my cyber-security. 

"See that you do," Krishna nodded, turning slightly serious. But the warmth in his eyes never faded. 

This was my moment. The ultimate cross-temporal existential interview. I took a deep breath. 

"My Lord," I asked, my voice trembling slightly. "If... if you are here now. Orchestrating, guiding, saving these idiots from their own destruction... why aren't you in 2026? Delhi NCR is literally suffocating. The world is heavily polarized, deeply cynical, and structurally terrifying. Why aren't you coming back to save humanity like you promised in the Gita?"

Krishna didn't look angry at the question. He just laughed—a highly amused, slightly exhausted laugh. 

"Rahul," Krishna sighed softly. "Who told you I ever left?"

I blinked, confused. "You're... you're there? In 2026?"

"Of course I am," Krishna said, gesturing broadly with his hand. "But do you have any idea how difficult it is to reveal my divinity in the modern era? I tried! Just last week in your timeline, I went to a heavily crowded local temple in Gurugram. I stood in the center, manifested a minor glowing aura, and loudly proclaimed, *'I am the Supreme Lord Krishna, the Alpha and the Omega, returned to guide you!'*"

"What happened?!" I gasped. 

"A man wearing a Bluetooth earpiece bumped into me," Krishna complained, looking deeply annoyed at the memory. "He spilled espresso on my sandals and yelled, *'Yeah sure buddy, and I am Elon Musk! Move out of the way, I have a Zoom call!'* And then two teenagers filmed me for their social media and called my aura a 'cheap holographic filter filter'. It was incredibly embarrassing."

I stared at him, absolutely speechless. 

"The modern world does not want majestic revelations," Krishna explained softly. "They want undeniable empirical data, preferably delivered in a fifteen-second video format. So, I gave up the grand entrances. I operate silently now. I sit in the heavy traffic on the Delhi-Noida Expressway and I ensure the ambulances get through. I randomly delay the alarm clocks of people who desperately need thirty more minutes of sleep. I help those who need and deserve my help, entirely off the grid."

His words hit me with profound, immense weight. I felt the tears prick my eyes again, and this time, I didn't fight them. I quickly bent down and, completely ignoring his previous instruction, touched his lotus feet, drawing a profound, overwhelming sense of absolute peace into my chaotic, tech-bro soul. 

Krishna gently placed a hand on my head. A surge of pure warmth washed through me. 

"You have done well, time-traveler," Krishna blessed me softly. "You have kept the timeline somewhat functional despite the overwhelming bureaucratic incompetence of your companions. However... I must warn you."

His voice lost the playful edge. The Supreme Being was speaking now. 

"I have a Karmic duty to protect these five brothers," Krishna stated, his eyes locked onto mine. "It is the fundamental structure of this age. But you must understand, Rahul... no matter how many compliance reports Yudhishthira writes, or how cleverly you hide them in Nayada... you cannot avoid the Kurukshetra War. The violence is destined. It is the necessary cleansing of this era's corruption. Do you understand?"

"I understand, My Lord," I whispered, the crushing reality of the impending epic war settling heavily on my shoulders. 

"Good. Now, on a much more immediate, highly stressful note," Krishna said, his mischievous smile returning instantly. "When you return to your squalid little house in Nayada tonight with your new bride... do **not** let Yudhishthira perform the formal introduction to Kunti."

"Why not?" 

"Because," Krishna sighed heavily, "in Vyasa's original draft, Yudhishthira walks into the house, completely ignores context, and yells, *'Mother, look what we brought home!'* And Kunti, engaged deeply in cooking without looking up, automatically replies, *'Whatever it is, divide it equally among all five of you!'*"

My jaw dropped in sheer horror. The legendary polyandry origin story!

"Exactly," Krishna nodded, seeing my panic. "It was the most socially devastating communication breakdown in literary history. If Yudhishthira says that line, Vyasa will immediately force the narrative, and Draupadi will be legally bound to marry all five of them. It will complicate the startup equity distribution terribly. Stop him."

"I will put duct tape over his mouth," I promised fiercely. 

"Excellent," Krishna winked. "Safe travels, Rahul. And seriously... change your passwords."

Before I could say another word, a group of arguing kings walked past the pillar. When I turned back, Lord Krishna was entirely gone, vanished into the shadows of the ancient party as if he had never been there. 

I immediately ran back to our table and forcefully initiated a highly aggressive extraction protocol. 

"We are leaving!" I hissed, grabbing Bhima by his tattooed arm. "Grab the CEO! Start the chariots! We have a severe incoming HR disaster!"

The journey back to Nayada that night was profoundly weird. Arjuna was sitting next to Draupadi, explaining the optimal tension of organic bowstrings, while Draupadi actively critiqued his lack of long-term sustainable supply chains. They were horrifyingly perfect for each other. 

The moment our chariot pulled into the dusty compound of Sector 15, I aggressively blocked the front door before anyone could enter. 

"Listen to me!" I yelled quietly to the Pandavas. "Under no circumstances is Yudhishthira allowed to speak to your mother right now! Nobody says *'look what we brought'*! Do you understand?!"

Sahadeva casually stepped off the chariot, removing his fake blonde wig. 

"Rahul," Sahadeva said smoothly, placing a hand on my highly stressed shoulder. "Relax. You are absorbing the stress of Vyasa's terrible fan-fiction."

"Fan-fiction?! Lord Krishna literally just warned me!"

"The Lord has a highly developed sense of dramatic irony," Sahadeva smiled slightly. "Do you honestly think we are that stupid? Do you think Draupadi—the most fiercely independent, highly educated CEO of Panchaal—would ever agree to a five-way matrimonial split just because my mother mumbled something while making parathas?"

I paused, looking at Draupadi, who was currently yelling at Bhima for failing to properly log the chariot mileage. 

"That was just Vyasa's folklore," Sahadeva reassured me. "A scandalous narrative hook designed to boost palm-leaf sales in the southern sectors. Draupadi is marrying Arjuna. The rest of us are simply her subordinate executive board members."

I let out a massive, shuddering breath, the tension finally leaving my body. Sahadeva was right. The mythological lore was heavily exaggerated for ratings. 

"However," Sahadeva continued quietly, not looking back toward the dark forest surrounding our compound, "you should probably be aware that we are currently being surveilled."

My adrenaline spiked instantly. "What? By who?"

"Dhrishtadyumna," Sahadeva murmured, casually adjusting his robes perfectly masking his psychic detection. "He secretly followed our chariot all the way from the stadium. He is currently hiding behind the third cactus on the left, actively trying to determine why his sister's legendary husband lives in a dusty shack in Sector 15."

"Should I intercept him?" Bhima asked eagerly, cracking his knuckles. 

"No," Sahadeva shook his head. "Be silent. Let him assume we are participating in a highly secretive, deeply mystic ascetic ritual. Let him take the geographical coordinates back to King Drupad. The startup requires investors, and King Drupad is extremely wealthy."

I nodded slowly, looking at the dark outline of the cactus in the distance. 

We had successfully infiltrated the Swayamvar. We had secured the ultimate corporate co-founder. I had literally received career advice from God. And we were safely back in ancient Noida. 

The corporate slavery phase of our timeline was officially coming to an end. It was time to launch the independent empire of Indraprastha.
