# Chapter 11: The Assasination of the Co-Founder

We skipped the heavily romanticized, slow-motion travel montage entirely. We were operating on a heavily optimized corporate timeline, and returning from the dead required maximum narrative efficiency. 

Four days after our strategic boarding meeting in the Chattarpur Farmhouse, the Pandavas—no longer disguised as terrible tourists from Americanapur, but dressed in the absolute height of royal Kuru finery funded entirely by espresso sales—marched proudly through the towering brass gates of Hastinapur. 

I walked slightly behind Arjuna, carrying Draupadi's highly aggressive, leather-bound CEO portfolio, essentially acting as the Chief of Staff for the most disruptive royal faction in history. 

The grand central courtyard was absolutely packed. The intelligence network of Hastinapur had apparently functioned perfectly for once, alerting the Kuru elders that the 'dead' princes were casually walking up the main avenue.

Standing on the high marble stairs were the central pillars of the dynasty. Grand-sire Bheeshma looked absolutely majestic, his long white beard flowing, his eyes wide with profound, unadulterated shock. Beside him stood the royal advisor Vidura, clutching a scroll so hard it looked ready to snap in half. And hovering nervously in the shadow of a pillar was Sage Vyasa, looking like a man who had just realized his multi-volume manuscript had completely crashed and deleted itself. 

Even the Kauravas were there. Suyodhana, standing at the forefront of his ninety-nine brothers, looked utterly bewildered. 

But it was King Dhritarashtra who broke the silence. The blind king stood up from his mobile throne, adjusting his deeply heavy crown, a massive, genuinely joyful smile spreading across his face. 

"They live?!" Dhritarashtra bellowed, his voice echoing powerfully off the marble walls. "My nephews have returned from the ashes! The gods have blessed Hastinapur today!"

He paused, a mischievous, deeply inappropriate glint entering his sightless eyes. He immediately turned his head toward the royal guard captain standing beside him. 

"Captain!" Dhritarashtra ordered loudly perfectly audible to the entire courtyard. "Do not bring the sharp-pointed spears out today! And tell the archers to entirely stand down!"

"Your Majesty?" the Captain asked nervously, his hand floating near his sword hilt. "Are we under attack?"

"No, you absolute fool!" Dhritarashtra barked happily. "For the last twenty years, every time I have attempted a light-hearted contextual joke, I have literally forced you to point spears at my family just to generate a polite laugh! Today, my nephews have miraculously returned from a catastrophic architectural disaster! I refuse to threaten anyone! Today, I await an entirely organic, un-coerced reaction to my humor! Watch this!"

The King turned back to the crowd perfectly facing the wrong direction toward a marble fountain before Vidura gently physically redirected him toward the Pandavas.

"Yudhishthira!" Dhritarashtra called out. "I was extremely worried that I would have to entirely restructure my annual succession planning! But seeing as you survived a burning house, I suppose you finally possess... excellent *firewall* protection!"

Absolute, dead silence descended upon the vast courtyard. The Kauravas winced. Bheeshma closed his eyes in deep spiritual pain. I physically felt myself age five years. It was quite possibly the worst tech joke in ancient history.

But then, an incredibly strange sound echoed from directly beside me. 

It was a sharp, sudden snort. Followed by a deep, rumbling, entirely genuine bout of laughter.

We all whipped our heads around.

Queen Kunti, standing beside Yudhishthira, had her hands covering her mouth, her shoulders shaking violently as she actively failed to suppress a massive laugh.

"Oh, by the gods!" Kunti giggled, wiping a tear from her eye. "Firewall! Because Varnavata was a literal wall of fire! Oh, Bhraata Dhritarashtra, that was absolutely terrible but contextually flawless!"

The entire Kuru dynasty stared at Kunti in absolute, paralyzing shock. Kunti, the tragic, long-suffering matriarch, had just organically laughed at the King's joke. 

Dhritarashtra gasped, his unseeing eyes widening in pure, overwhelming elation.

"She laughed!" Dhritarashtra practically screamed, pointing aggressively toward the sky. "Did you hear that, Vidura?! It was organic! No spears were involved! I am officially a comedic genius! Cancel all state executions for the month! Throw a feast!

The tension in the courtyard immediately evaporated, replaced by an atmosphere of genuinely baffling joy. 

I looked over at the Kauravas. I expected them to be furious. I expected Suyodhana to be quietly signaling assassins hiding in the balconies. Instead, the Kaurava brothers genuinely looked... happy. 

Dushasana enthusiastically waved at Bhima from across the stairs. Bhima, immediately dropping his serious warrior facade, grinned vastly and waved back. 

"Wait," I whispered to Sahadeva, leaning close to him. "Why aren't they plotting our destruction? They literally tried to barbecue us in Varnavata."

"Check your narrative biases, Rahul," Sahadeva murmured back quietly, a small smile playing on his lips. "Remember the core rewrite. Suyodhana is a terrible PR-obsessed tech-bro who relies heavily on outsourced consultants. He didn't order the fire. Purochana initiated it independently to inflate his architectural billing hours. Suyodhana actually missed us. We provide excellent competitive drive for his quarterly reports."

Before I could fully process that deeply corporate family dynamic, a heavy, familiar hand dropped onto my shoulder, accompanied by the distinct rattling of bone dice. 

"Welcome back from the dead, time-traveler," Shakuni purred directly into my ear. 

I turned around. The King of Gandhara was leaning on his intricate cane, looking down at me with a deeply calculating, highly satisfied smile. He was the only one in Hastinapur who had already known we were alive, thanks to his terrifyingly efficient intelligence network—and our 10,000-year binding non-compete contract. 

"Hello, Uncle Shakuni," I replied quietly, maintaining my formal tone. 

"My spies in Indraprastha are highly efficient," Shakuni whispered softly, dragging me slightly away from the main family reunion. "They tell me you have successfully launched an incredibly lucrative, highly addictive caffeinated beverage monopoly called the Rajsooyi Karyafe."

"We are managing acceptable profit margins," I said defensively. 

"Do not be modest, Rahul. You are practically printing currency," Shakuni grinned, his dark eyes sparkling with unadulterated capitalist greed. "Which brings me to an extremely crucial point of business integration. You have established regional dominance, but you require global market penetration. And luckily for you, you already have an agency on retainer."

"Wait," I stared at him. "Are you seriously pitching me a marketing campaign right now?"

"MBHT Media is the premier public relations firm in Bharata," Shakuni stated proudly, tapping his cane. "We hold your contract. We are practically business partners. When you launch your next quarterly marketing campaign for this 'coffee' substance, you will hire me to handle the brand messaging. I can practically guarantee a 40% increase in sales merely by spreading highly aggressive rumors that the local water supply is poisoned. It is the ultimate synergistic corporate maneuver!"

"We will... we will circle back to that in Q3," I deflected desperately, terrified of unleashing Shakuni’s manipulative brilliance onto a commercial beverage market. 

"I look forward to the Zoom call," Shakuni winked, slipping back into the crowd just as Yudhishthira stepped forward to formally address the royal court. 

Yudhishthira walked to the center of the marble floor, taking the hand of his newly acquired CEO. 

"Grand-sire Bheeshma. King Dhritarashtra," Yudhishthira announced, his voice carrying the perfect, projected authority of a man who had successfully optimized an entire regional supply chain. "We formally apologize for the lack of communication regarding our survival. We were forced into a structural pivot."

"A structural pivot?" Bheeshma asked, completely bewildered by the corporate terminology. "My child, we thought you were ashes in the wind."

"We utilized the incognito phase to establish a massive, highly successful entrepreneurial venture in Indraprastha," Yudhishthira continued smoothly, ignoring the emotional trauma entirely. "We are currently the dominant market force in the Delhi NCR region. Princess Draupadi here is serving as our Chief Executive Officer. And we have returned to Hastinapur today... to personally invite every single member of this court to our official house-warming party."

"A party?!" Dushasana practically yelled, incredibly excited. 

"Yes," Arjuna nodded proudly. "We have purchased a highly premium, intensely luxurious piece of real estate on Chitrapur Road. It has Italian architecture."

"Wait, what is Italian?" Vidura asked, frantically writing on a scroll. "Is that a new southern kingdom?"

Before Yudhishthira could explain Sahadeva's terrible Mathura lie, Suyodhana stepped forward from the Kaurava ranks. The Crown Prince smoothed his silk robes, his eyes darting frantically around the courtyard, assessing the "optics" of the situation. 

Suyodhana loved good optics. 

"Citizens of Hastinapur! Family!" Suyodhana raised his hands, projecting his voice to ensure the royal scribes captured every word perfectly. "For too long, I have fought against terrible, deeply unfair negative reviews from local bards! They painted me as a jealous, power-hungry rival! But today, you see the truth!"

Suyodhana walked over and aggressively hugged a very stiff Yudhishthira, looking directly at the court painters slightly off stage so they could capture the moment. 

"I am a just man!" Suyodhana declared emotionally, shedding a single, highly calculated PR tear. "I love my cousins! And I am heavily committed to supporting small, rural businesses! To prove my commitment, I officially declare that I will fully support the Pandavas' new startup empire! Hastinapur shall graciously provide logistics and trade routing for their caffeinated expansion!"

The court erupted in massive applause. Bheeshma looked incredibly relieved. Dhritarashtra was still chuckling over his firewall joke. It was a picture of absolute, unbreakable familial harmony.

As the applause died down, Suyodhana quickly turned over his shoulder toward Shakuni, who was standing a few feet away.

"Uncle," Suyodhana hissed out of the side of his mouth, his fake emotional smile still perfectly plastered on his face. "If I publicly endorse their beverage monopoly, that secures them as our primary financial backers for my upcoming Indraprastha localized election campaign, right? They essentially become a PAC (Political Action Committee)?"

"Flawless corporate integration, my boy," Shakuni whispered back, giving him a massive thumbs up. "You just securitized their massive coffee revenue into your PR budget."

I watched this highly advanced political maneuvering in sheer awe. The mythological rivalry of the epic had been completely reduced to aggressive corporate sponsorships and high-level campaign funding. It was utterly brilliant, and incredibly peaceful.

Everything was perfect. The war was officially averted. 

But I had aggressively underestimated the stubborn, unyielding power of the man who wrote the script.

The rest of the royal reception was an absolute blur of highly functioning, deeply confusing corporate networking. 

The Kaurava brothers immediately swarmed us, eager to leverage the massive political capital of our sudden resurrection.

"Bhima! Rahul!" Dushasana practically skipped over to us, his massive frame radiating genuine joy. He aggressively hugged Bhima, nearly cracking the giant's ribs. "It is so good to see you! Majaa hi aa gawaa (Its so fun to see you!). I genuinely missed our tactical sparring sessions! My local gym trainer has absolutely zero functional combat metrics!"

"Aaho!" Bhima laughed, completely dropping his ancient grudges, patting his cousin's back with bone-shattering force. "Next time you come to Chattarpur, I will personally optimize your core strength training by making you lift massive stone pillars for the garden!"

"I would love that, Bhaiya Ji!" Dushasana beamed. 

I stood there, slightly bewildered by the intense cousinly bonding, when a quiet voice spoke directly in my ear. 

"I always knew those terrible tie-dye shirts in Panchaal were a completely fabricated counter-espionage tactic."

I spun around. Vikarna, the smartest, most perceptive Kaurava, was leaning against a marble pillar, perfectly nursing a cup of wine. He gave me the exact same highly suspicious wink he had given me back at the Swayamvar arena.

"You knew?" I asked, instantly going into damage control mode. "Listen, Vikarna, the Americanapur branding was purely a tactical localized pivot. We had to establish a—"

"Save the corporate jargon, Rahul," Vikarna laughed softly, waving a hand. "I completely respected the operational secrecy. Frankly, I thought it was a brilliant maneuver. In fact, Sahadeva and I were actively discussing the logistical brilliance of it on WhatsApp just last night."

I froze entirely. My brain effectively crashed. 

"You... you were what?" I stammered, staring at him. 

"WhatsApp," Vikarna repeated clearly. "The telepathic network infrastructure. Sahadeva set it up months ago. How do you think we managed to secretly coordinate the funding for Guru Dronacharya’s annual Dakshina payout without crashing the royal Hastinapur treasury? Sahadeva and I have a combined chat group. It’s highly secure."

I aggressively spun around and stared at Sahadeva, who was currently standing near the buffet table, calmly predicting the exact moment the paneer tikka would run out.

"You have been secretly messaging the 'enemy' for months?!" I hissed across the room.

Sahadeva paused, his psychic eyes meeting mine. He simply tapped his finger against his temple and projected a deeply sarcastic thought directly into my brain. 

*We are optimizing intra-family communication protocols, Rahul. Constant geopolitical warfare is terrible for long-term investments. Welcome to the modern era.*

I rubbed my forehead, suddenly realizing that while I had been desperately fighting to rewrite mythology, the smartest people in the actual epic had simply bypassed the drama entirely by setting up a group chat. The Mahabharata wasn't being averted by my brilliant modern strategies; it was being outsourced and resolved through efficient internal communication. 

Yudhishthira spent the next two hours meticulously inviting everyone to the Chattarpur Farmhouse. Even Dronacharya and Kripacharya RSVP'd positively, extremely eager to see the "avant-garde Italian load-bearing principles" that Sahadeva kept aggressively bragging about. 

It was the most beautiful, entirely peaceful family reunion in the history of the Kuru dynasty. 

Which is exactly why the author of the epic decided to violently interject himself. 

While the family celebrated in the main courtyard, I stepped away to grab a cup of water from a small silver fountain tucked into a dark alcove. 

As I approached the fountain, I heard deeply furious, highly panicked whispering coming from behind a large marble statue of a lion. 

"It is completely ruined!" a voice hissed. I immediately recognized the frantic, overly dramatic tone. It was Sage Vyasa, the legendary author and narrator of the epic. 

"Calm down, Vyasa," a deep, ancient, and highly authoritative voice replied. I peered around the statue. Grand-sire Bheeshma was standing there, his arms crossed over his massive chest, looking profoundly stressed. 

"Calm down?!" Vyasa literally pulled at his long beard in sheer frustration. "Bheeshma, do you not understand the structural collapse of the narrative timeline?! The Pandavas were supposed to return and immediately demand their half of the kingdom! Suyodhana was supposed to aggressively refuse! There was supposed to be a massive, kingdom-shattering property dispute that would inevitably escalate into a genocidal war!"

"They did not ask for property, Vyasa," Bheeshma sighed heavily, rubbing his temples. "They asked for tax breaks and logistical supply routes for a caffeinated beverage monopoly. Suyodhana actually hugged them. He called them a PAC. I do not understand what a PAC is, but it sounded completely non-violent."

"This is impossible!" Vyasa paced back and forth, holding a crumpled piece of palm leaf that contained his outline for the epic. "My entire thematic arc of 'Dharma vs Adharma' is collapsing into a mundane regional corporate merger! The audience will absolutely hate it! It lacks dramatic tension! I dont even know what kind of GenZ or Millenial writer is doing all this. I heard a name my spies told me - they call him - The Arora! Is that real?" - looking out of this book straight into the readers eye i.e. you (the one who purchased this book), this time.

"Perhaps we can subtly manipulate the taxation laws to create friction?" Bheeshma suggested tiredly, clearly not looking at the reader i.e you, staying in the character and not wanting a war but feeling obligated to fulfill the narrative structural requirements. 

"No," Vyasa stopped pacing. His ancient eyes narrowed down to terrifying, intensely focused slits. "The problem isn't the taxation laws. The problem is the anomaly. The catalyst for all this peaceful corporate synergy."

"The catalyst?" Bheeshma frowned. 

"You don't see it, Bheeshma, because you lack directorial vision," Vyasa grabbed Bheeshma’s heavy shoulder. "Every single time the plot completely deviates from my script... he is there. The one wearing the terrible modern clothing. The 'consultant'. The man who refuses to speak Sanskrit properly and constantly talks about 'metrics' and 'synergy'. Rahul."

My breath completely stopped. I flattened myself against the marble wall, my heart suddenly pounding like a jackhammer. 

"Rahul?" Bheeshma looked confused. "He seems like a highly efficient administrator. He successfully managed the Vernavata evacuation."

"He is destroying my creative vision!" Vyasa practically roared, though keeping his voice to an aggressive whisper out of fear of discovery. "He convinced the Pandavas to start a business instead of a war! He made Suyodhana care about public polling! He is actively rewriting the metadata of the epic! As long as Rahul exists, the Mahabharata will never happen!"

"Then what is your proposed solution, Vyasa?" Bheeshma asked solemnly. "You are the chronicler. You hold the pen."

"There is only one way to correct a structural anomaly," Vyasa said, his voice dropping into a dark, terrifyingly cold frequency. "We must delete the corrupted file. Rahul must be completely removed from the narrative equation before the housewarming party. Before they consolidate their corporate empire."

"Removed?" Bheeshma’s eyes widened. "You mean... assassination?"

"It is exactly what the plot needs right now," Vyasa nodded grimly, already mentally rewriting his chapters. "A tragic murder. It will shatter the peaceful corporate alliance! The Pandavas will blame the Kauravas! Suyodhana will blame the Pandavas' terrible coffee supply chain! War will be inevitable! It is the perfect narrative catalyst!"

Bheeshma looked deeply conflicted, his vows warring with his basic human morality. But Vyasa was the author. His word was literal, undeniable fate. 

"Who will execute this... deletion?" Bheeshma asked softly. 

"I know a man," Vyasa smiled a terrifying, authorial smile. "A mercenary hidden in the deep forests. Entirely off the grid. Very deadly. And incredibly desperate for professional validation. I will contract him immediately."

I didn't wait to hear another word. 

I literally scrambled backward, my cargo shorts slipping on the smooth marble, and sprinted away from the alcove as fast as humanly possible, entirely panicked. 

The author of the literal universe had just ordered a hit on me. I was a dead man walking.

I sprinted blindly down the outer corridor, desperately looking for Arjuna, or Bhima, or literally anyone with superhuman combat abilities to protect me, when a heavy wooden cane shot out from an intersection and violently tripped me. 

I crashed onto the floor, sliding a solid five feet on the polished stone. 

"Running in the palace is highly unprofessional, partner," a smooth, raspy voice chuckled from the shadows. 

I rolled over, gasping for air. Shakuni stood over me, twirling his bone dice and looking highly amused. 

"Shakuni!" I wheezed, scrambling to my feet. "I need to find the Pandavas! Vyasa—"

"I know exactly what the old manuscript-scribbler is planning, Rahul," Shakuni interrupted, his amusement instantly vanishing, replaced by cold, calculating intensity. He stepped forward and grabbed my arm, dragging me into a small, highly secure map room and slamming the heavy brass door shut. 

"You... you know?" I asked, my chest heaving. 

"My intelligence network is absolute," Shakuni sneered. "I heard Vyasa’s tantrum in the alcove. The arrogant fool actually believes he can assassinate my primary business partner."

"He holds the narrative pen!" I panicked perfectly. "He's going to delete me from the epic!"

"He will do no such thing," Shakuni tapped his cane on the floor sharply. "You and I have a 10,000-year binding non-compete contract regarding the MBHT Media startup. Do you honestly think I will allow an unpaid author to completely void my equity stake by assassinating the primary architect of our corporate monopoly? Your survival is directly tied to my future revenue streams. I am protecting my investment."

I stared at the King of Gandhara. The greatest villain in mythological history was actively protecting me from the author of the epic purely for financial gain. 

"How?" I swallowed hard. "He’s hiring an off-the-grid mercenary! Someone from the deep forests!"

"I know exactly who he is hiring," Shakuni sneered, moving to a small wooden table. "And we are going to completely hijack the assassination script."

Before I could ask for the details of Shakuni’s highly questionable corporate protection plan, a soft knock echoed on the map room door. 

Vikarna slipped in, quickly locking it behind him. 

"The Pandavas are leaving," Vikarna announced quietly, nodding at Shakuni. "They are heading back to Indraprastha ahead of the rest of the family to actively finalize the caterers for the Chitrapur housewarming party. Apparently, Bhima does not trust the local vendors."

"Excellent," Shakuni nodded smoothly. He turned back to me. "Rahul. You will not return to Indraprastha with them. You are going to stay right here, in the Hastinapur palace, under my direct supervision."

"Stay here?!" I protested "But I am the CTO! Draupadi needs me to optimize the guest list metrics!"

"If you travel the royal highway with the Pandavas, Vyasa’s mercenary will ambush your chariot," Shakuni stated bluntly. "He is an ancient forest tracker. He will easily isolate you from Arjuna's protection and assassinate you while you are aggressively complaining about the chariot's suspension. You must remain here, out of sight. Tell Yudhishthira that you need to audit the Kaurava treasury regarding the campaign funding. He will completely understand."

I hesitated. But remaining in Hastinapur meant I had direct access to Vikarna. And if I had direct access to Vikarna, I could potentially finalize a permanent peace treaty and entirely avoid the Mahabharata before Vyasa could even write a new draft. 

"Fine," I agreed. "I will stay."

The Pandavas left an hour later, blissfully unaware that the narrative timeline was actively hunting their CTO. Yudhishthira actually gave me a clipboard and told me to specifically verify Suyodhana’s liquid assets, which gave me perfect cover to essentially hide in the Kaurava wing of the palace. 

While I was auditing the Kaurava treasury, Vyasa was executing his hit. 

Deep in the dense, terrifyingly wild forests bordering the Kuru kingdom, the author of the epic met his assassin. 

Vyasa stood in a small clearing, looking deeply uncomfortable in the humidity, holding a small pouch of gold. 

A shadow dropped from the trees above, landing silently on the forest floor. 

The mercenary was terrifying. He was clad entirely in crude animal skins, practically vibrating with intense, unyielding focus. His eyes were wild, and he carried a massive, incredibly complex hunting bow. 

This was Eklavya. The legendary Nishada warrior. 

"Assassin," Vyasa announced solemnly, adopting his traditional, grand narrative tone. "I require your highly lethal services. A time-traveling corporate anomaly must be deleted from the timeline. The fate of the entire epic rests upon your shoulders. You must infiltrate Hastinapur and eliminate Rahul."

Eklavya stared intensely at Vyasa for several completely silent seconds. Then, he aggressively pounded his chest and let out a bizarre, deeply confusing string of guttural noises. 

"Glargle bloof! Shrakna binga-bong!" Eklavya yelled fiercely, pointing passionately at his bow. "Glabnuk bongo wonga!"

Vyasa blinked, adjusting his glasses. He was the most brilliant writer in the ancient world, but he apparently didn't speak 'wild forest gibberish'. 

"I... I will assume that means you accept the contract?" Vyasa asked nervously. 

Eklavya gave an intense, aggressively violent thumbs-up. Except, it wasn't a thumbs-up. Because his right thumb was completely missing. 

"Glargle! Bloof!" Eklavya shouted again, snatched the pouch of gold from Vyasa's hand, and violently front-flipped back into the dense canopy of trees. 

Vyasa let out a heavy sigh of relief. The plot was correcting itself. 

Back in Hastinapur, I was sitting in Vikarna’s highly modernized (for the Bronze Age) private chamber, desperately reviewing the lore. 

"Eklavya?" I practically shrieked, dropping Yudhishthira's clipboard. "Vyasa hired Eklavya to kill me?! But he is the greatest archer in the world! He literally trained himself by watching Guru Drona from afar!"

"Ah, yes," Vikarna said smoothly, pouring a cup of wine. "The great, mythical origin story. Allow me to correct your highly romanticized historical understanding."

"Correct what?" I asked, completely confused. "The thumb story? Guru Drona demanded Eklavya's right thumb as Dakshina (guru fee) because he didn't want any archer in the world to be better than Arjuna! It is one of the most tragic, unfair moments in the entire epic!"

Vikarna and Shakuni exchanged a highly amused look. 

"Rahul," Vikarna chuckled softly, sitting down across from me. "Dronacharya did not demand his thumb out of jealousy. He demanded it because Eklavya was an absolute culinary hazard."

I stared at him blankly. "What?"

"Eklavya did not learn archery by silently watching Dronacharya," Shakuni explained tiredly, completely destroying my childhood mythology. "Eklavya was Dronacharya’s personal camp assistant when they lived near the forest. He was the cook. Do you know what *chakhana* is?"

"Snacks?" I supplied automatically. "For... drinking?"

"Exactly," Vikarna nodded seriously. "One evening, Drona and Kripacharya were enjoying a highly relaxed evening of intense drinking. Drona asked Eklavya to quickly chop some onions and spicy roots for their *chakhana*. Eklavya, being highly enthusiastic but entirely lacking fine motor control, aggressively slammed the cleaver down and accidentally chopped off his own right thumb."

"He chopped it off by accident?!" I gaped. "While making bar snacks?!"

"It was a catastrophic culinary accident," Vikarna confirmed. "Dronacharya was furious because the blood entirely ruined the spicy roots. The story about Dronacharya 'demanding the thumb' to protect Arjuna was completely fabricated by Dronacharya’s PR team to make the entire incident sound mystical and authoritative instead of deeply embarrassing."

"It gets worse," Shakuni sneered. "Because Eklavya is deeply ashamed of the onion-chopping incident, he aggressively overcompensates by acting like a highly lethal, mute forest assassin. He speaks entirely in fabricated gibberish to sound mysterious, and he shoots arrows using his index and middle fingers. He is highly dangerous, completely unhinged, and currently tracking you."

"We have to warn the guards!" I panicked completely. "I won't survive a silent assassin strike!"

"The royal guards are useless against a highly motivated forest tracker," Shakuni dismissed coldly. "Eklavya will slip through the palace defenses like water. If he realizes you are alive, he will not stop until he fulfills Vyasa's contract. The only way to stop a hyper-focused assassin is to provide him with proof of completely unquestionable success."

"Proof of success?" I asked nervously. 

"We need a body," Vikarna nodded grimly. "We need to trick Eklavya into believing he successfully assassinated you. We need to leave a 'dead Rahul' in a highly visible, easily accessible location for Eklavya to find and report back to Vyasa."

"Where exactly are we going to find a dead body that looks like an IT worker from Noida?!" I yelled.

"Oh," Shakuni smiled his terrible, deeply unsettling capitalist smile. "I can supply the inventory. Come with me."

And so began the most horrifying detour of my entire time-traveling existence. 

Late that night, when the palace was completely asleep, Shakuni led Vikarna and me down a series of dark, highly restricted spiral stairs deep beneath the Hastinapur armory. The air grew cold, smelling strongly of incense and terrible chemicals. 

Shakuni unlocked a heavy iron door and ushered us inside. 

I immediately violently gagged. 

It was a fully functioning, entirely illegal secret laboratory. The stone tables were covered in strange tools, small carving knives, and hundreds of entirely polished human bones. It looked like an apocalyptic nightmare. 

"What is this place?!" I croaked, covering my nose with my shirt. 

"This is my primary manufacturing facility," Shakuni said proudly, gesturing to the literal piles of bones. "Where do you think my magical dice come from, Rahul?"

"I... I always assumed you imported them!" I squeaked, horrified. 

"A poor corporate assumption," Shakuni sneered, walking over to a table and picking up a finely carved bone cube. "My dice always roll exactly what I desire because they are crafted from the bones of my dead family members. Specifically, my father. They contain their deeply frustrated, highly compliant spiritual energy."

I stared at the King of Gandhara in absolute, profound horror. 

"But I realized," Shakuni continued, completely ignoring my absolute terror, "that simply limiting myself to familial bones was a terrible waste of resources. So, I established a highly secret, entirely illegal regional business franchise."

He pointed to the far corner of the lab. 

"When criminals die in the lower dungeons, or when mercenaries fall in battle, their bodies are quietly rerouted here," Shakuni smiled cleanly. "I carve their bones into small, highly magical items. Loaded dice. Charms. Small tactical talismans for merchants. It is extremely profitable."

"You... you are running an illegal magical bone-carving franchise?!" I backed away slightly. "That is completely horrifying!"

"It is optimized recycling," Shakuni corrected sharply. "The dead must give something back to the economy. Now, stop trembling. We need to assemble an alternative version of you."

Shakuni moved to a dark alcove and pulled out what appeared to be the fresh, highly intact body of a recently deceased bandit who had unfortunately suffocated in the lower dungeons. The bandit was roughly my height, had somewhat similar hair, and was currently not complaining about the smell. 

"This will suffice for the structural decoy," Shakuni noted clinically. 

"He looks absolutely nothing like me!" I protested frantically. "He has a massive scar on his cheek and he lacks my sophisticated, corporate exhaustion!"

"Eklavya operates primarily in the dark and possesses terrible eyesight entirely due to a poor forest diet," Vikarna explained calmly, helping Shakuni drag the body onto a table. "He will not check your facial biometrics. He is simply looking for the required clothing."

"Take off your shorts," Shakuni ordered, extending a hand. 

I hesitated, but the terrifying reality of an incoming assassin overrode my dignity. I awkwardly hopped on one foot, pulled off the terrible cargo shorts and tie-dye t-shirt I had worn since Panchaal, and handed them to the King of Gandhara. 

Ten minutes later, the deceased bandit was fully dressed in the official "Hippies of Americanapur" tactical uniform. In the dim lighting of the laboratory, it was a shockingly passable decoy. They then took the body to Vikarna's room for changeover. 

"Now," Shakuni wiped his hands efficiently. "Vikarna and I will transport the decoy to your assigned guest suite. We will pose the body to suggest a swift, terrifyingly sudden demise. Eklavya will enter, 'assassinate' the decoy in the dark, and return to Vyasa claiming absolute success."

"What do I do?!" I asked, shivering slightly in only my linen undergarments in a terrifying bone-lab. 

"You," Vikarna grinned, "are going to undergo an immediate, highly aggressive corporate rebranding."

The strategy to keep me alive in Hastinapur was based firmly on one of the most fundamental, logistical absurdities of the entire Mahabharata epic. 

"We are going to hide you exactly where Vyasa will least logically expect to look," Vikarna said an hour later, aggressively pulling a heavy, silk Kaurava princely robe over my head in his private chambers. 

"A secret dungeon?" I asked, struggling with the incredibly tight silk sash. 

"No," Shakuni said flatly, tossing a heavy golden necklace around my neck. "We are hiding you in plain sight. You are going to become a Kaurava."

I froze. "I am going to become what?!"
