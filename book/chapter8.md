# Chapter 8: The Nayada Commute and The DaahLee Daud

Living in prehistoric Noida—or *Nayada*, as Yudhishthira proudly called his real estate investment—was profoundly demoralizing.

In the year 2026, Noida was a concrete jungle of towering apartment complexes, massive glass-front IT parks, unnavigable flyovers, and aggressive dust storms. It was a city fueled by caffeine, corporate anxiety, and the terrifying aggression of local drivers. 

In 3200 BCE, Nayada was surprisingly… exactly the same, minus the glass IT parks.

Yudhishthira’s "suburban getaway" in Sector 15 was a large, sturdy mud-brick compound surrounded by vast, empty plains of aggressively hostile scrubland. It was dry, incredibly dusty, and completely devoid of any cultural refinement. The only difference between ancient Nayada and modern Noida was that instead of dodging massive SUVs that aggressively honked at you, you spent your mornings dodging massive herds of wild boar that aggressively charged at you. 

"This is the pinnacle of undisturbed pastoral living," Yudhishthira declared on our third morning, standing on the dusty porch of the compound, taking a deep breath of the intensely dry air. "No compliance audits. No royal decrees. Just the raw, unyielding embrace of absolute boredom."

The rest of the Pandavas were struggling severely to adapt to suburban mediocrity. 

They were demigod princes. They required high-stakes drama, training montages, and constant validation from loyal subjects. Putting them in Sector 15 Nayada was like putting five highly lethal tigers into an empty sandbox and telling them to enjoy a quiet retirement. 

Bhima, deprived of a fully stocked royal kitchen and a functioning tandoor, had taken to aggressively hunting the local wildlife with his bare hands. He would disappear into the scrubland for hours, returning covered in mud and carrying an entire stag over his shoulder, muttering bitterly in Punjabi about the complete lack of accessible cumin in the sector. 

"Ithe koi dukan nahi hai, Raool!" Bhima would complain, aggressively skinning a deer on the front lawn. "Mainu thoda jeha garam masala chahida hai! Saara din ghas-phus te jeena painda hai! Eh Sector 15 di society badi bekaar hai!" *(There is no shop here, Rahul! I just need a little garam masala! Living on grass and herbs all day! This Sector 15 society is useless!)*

Arjuna had taken to pacing. He walked laps around the compound perimeter, constantly drawing and un-drawing his legendary Gandiva bow, scanning the empty horizon for any sign of a target. Occasionally, he would shoot an arrow into a distant cactus, dramatically wait for applause, and then visibly deflate when the only response was the sound of cicadas buzzing in the heat. 

"The atmospheric pressure here is utterly stagnant," Arjuna complained, dramatically staring into the setting sun. "If Draupadi were here, she would accurately diagnose that my aerodynamic velocity is being actively suppressed by suburban ennui."

Nakula, cut off from his network of rural aestheticians, was having a full-blown existential crisis. He spent his days desperately trying to invent a functional Exfoliating scrub using ground river-stones and camel milk. He had commandeered the only large water basin in the compound and spent hours glaring critically at his own reflection. 

"Rahul," Nakula whispered one afternoon, grabbing my arm with terrifying intensity. "My pores are rebelling. I haven't seen a drop of rosewater in ninety-six hours. If my T-zone breaks out, I am surrendering to the Hastinapur guards. I refuse to die with bad skin."

Sahadeva, predictably, handled the adjustment gracefully. Freed from the chaotic emotional outbursts of his family, he set up a small meditation cushion in the darkest corner of the compound and returned immediately to projecting his psychic consciousness into 2026 streaming services. I assumed he was currently halfway through season four of *Breaking Bad*, based entirely on how frequently he muttered about the geopolitical implications of blue crystal. 

But as the days stretched on, the isolation began to wear on all of us. Nayada was far removed from the actual center of geopolitical relevance. We were essentially living in the mythological equivalent of a sleepy college town during summer break. 

The real power, the absolute, undeniably dominant force of the entire region, lay fifty miles to the west. 

Indraprastha. 

In my memory of the epic, Indraprastha was supposed to be the glorious, magical kingdom built by the Pandavas *after* a complex land dispute with the Kauravas. But the timeline was clearly fragmenting. Because right now, Indraprastha was actively occupied. It was the central hub of power for the Naga Kingdom, and it was ruled by a man I had only heard terrifying rumors about. 

King Takshaka. 

I found Sahadeva sitting on the porch one evening, staring blankly out at the dusty expanse of Sector 15, and I asked him about the political landscape. 

"Indraprastha is the most happening place in Bharata right now," Sahadeva sighed, his eyes distant. "It is the economic center. The cultural apex. Hastinapur might have old money and traditional martial strength, but Indraprastha is where the actual innovation happens. And just next to it lies the fabled land of GuruGramaha."

"GuruGramaha?" I blinked, the name instantly triggering a horrifying modern reflex. "You mean... Gurugram?"

"An absolute haven of excessive wealth and terrible drainage systems," Sahadeva nodded sagely. "You see, we cannot possibly afford to live there. The property prices in GuruGramaha are so exponentially off the roof that even King Dhritarashtra of Hastinapur couldn't afford a two-bedroom hut there without liquidating the Royal Treasury. Only the absolute richest elites of Indraprastha, like Takshaka, own villas in GuruGramaha. I foresee that in a few millennia, it will be populated entirely by people wearing Bluetooth earpieces complaining about corporate valuations while drinking very expensive, highly caffeinated fermented bean water."

I shuddered. "So instead of the Millennium City, we get stuck in ancient Sector 15."

"Exactly. Anyway, Takshaka... Takshaka is a force of nature."

I frowned, trying to remember what limited mythological knowledge I possessed. "Isn't Takshaka a giant, terrifying snake-king who bites people and burns down forests?"

Sahadeva laughed, a short, dry, depressing sound. "That is the propaganda spread by old conservatives like Bhishma to scare the children. Takshaka is not a giant magical snake. Takshaka is... well, Takshaka is essentially a brilliantly ruthless politician." 

"How ruthless?" I asked nervously. 

"He is the ultimate advisor to all the surrounding kingdoms," Sahadeva explained, drawing a map in the dust with a stick. "He controls the trade routes. He negotiates the treaties. But more importantly... he controls the media narrative. The man is completely obsessed with his public relations image. He never misses a photo opportunity."

"A photo opportunity in 3200 BCE?" 

"Yes," Sahadeva nodded. "He employs a squad of specialized artists who aggressively follow him around and rapidly paint portraits of him interacting with poor farmers, opening new grain silos, and hugging cows. He ensures these portraits are sent to every tavern in the subcontinent within twenty-four hours. He is everywhere. He acts like the supreme benevolent emperor, constantly waving to crowds and delivering passionate speeches about infrastructure."

"He sounds like an ancient PM Modi," I muttered, deeply impressed and profoundly terrified by the concept of an ancient Prime Minister possessing absolute PR dominance. 

"Exactly," Sahadeva agreed, not blinking at the 2026 reference. "But Takshaka's central action stays entirely within Indraprastha. He hasn't visited Nayada in three decades. We are safe here. But we are also completely irrelevant." 

The sheer irrelevance of our current situation was grinding the Pandavas down to dust. They were men built for destiny, currently relegated to watching Bhima try to barbecue scorpions. 

So, when the news arrived two months later of a massive public event happening in the heart of Indraprastha, it was the spark that ignited an absolute logistical nightmare.


It was an exceptionally dusty afternoon when a lone courier riding a heavily exhausted horse appeared on the edge of the Nayada compound. He nailed a large, colorful parchment flyer onto the front gate before riding away. 

I walked out with the Pandavas to inspect it. Nakula immediately recoiled from the poor font choice. Arjuna started aggressively analyzing the aerodynamic properties of the nail used to secure it. Yudhishthira pulled out his reading glasses and squinted at the text. 

"Ah," Yudhishthira read aloud, his voice taking on a formal tone. "It is a public proclamation from King Takshaka. He is hosting the inaugural 'DaahLee Daud'. An athletic exhibition of endurance and civic unity."

"What is a DaahLee Daud?" Arjuna asked, tilting his head. 

"Daud means run," I translated slowly, staring at the flyer. "He is hosting a run. A marathon. In Delhi. He is hosting the first-ever Delhi Marathon."

"It states here that participation is mandatory for all capable citizens of the surrounding regions," Yudhishthira continued reading. "It is a 21-kilometer track mapped exactly through the center of Indraprastha. All proceeds and... 'sponsorship grain' will be donated to King Takshaka’s personal NGO charity for the beautification of his own statues."

"Charity for his own statues?" I muttered. "The PR machinery on this guy is terrifyingly efficient."

Before I could point out the absolute absurdity of a corrupt political marathon, Bhima aggressively slapped the compound gate, nearly shattering the wood. 

"Main daudunga!" Bhima roared enthusiastically in Punjabi, his eyes gleaming with the desperate fire of a man who hadn't engaged in competitive physical activity in weeks. "Main sabnu piche chhad danga! Ithe baithe baithe meri mitti kharab ho rahi hai! Main DaahLee Daud jit ke aaunga!" *(I will run! I will leave everyone behind! Sitting here my potential is getting ruined! I will win the DaahLee Daud and return!)*

Arjuna's competitive reflexes instantly engaged. "You? Win a race of endurance? Please, Bhima, your caloric intake would actively hinder your cardiovascular output. If anyone is going to secure the 21-kilometer victory, it is me. My stride-mechanics are structurally flawless."

"I shall also participate," Nakula declared, flipping his hair. "I foresee an incredible opportunity to sweat out my toxins. And the glow at the finish line will be visually devastating to my competitors." 

Even Yudhishthira, ever fearful of disobeying a bureaucratic mandate, agreed to attend, citing a technical imperative to verify the route mapping. 

The next dawn, our entire disguised, incognito group—wrapped in heavy cloaks to hide their highly identifiable royal features—made the fifty-mile commute from Nayada into the bustling, overwhelming metropolis of Indraprastha. 

The city was breathtaking. Unlike the stony, grim militant architecture of Hastinapur or the quiet misery of Nayada, Indraprastha was a masterpiece of thriving Bronze Age urban planning. Wide, paved streets, massive open-air markets, towering ziggurats, and thousands of people buzzing with energetic commerce. 

The starting line for the DaahLee Daud was packed with thousands of citizens. At the center of a massive stage stood King Takshaka. 

Takshaka was a tall, imposing man wearing a glittering emerald-green silk tunic, surrounded by an absolute army of portrait painters. He was aggressively smiling, waving to the crowd, and handing out free water flasks that had his own face painted on them. 

"Look at him," Sahadeva whispered from beneath his hooded cloak, gesturing to the stage. "He is currently ensuring that at least five different painters capture him kissing a commoner's baby. Truly, a master of deception."

A massive brass gong sounded. The race began. 

The DaahLee Daud was exactly as grueling as you would expect a 21-kilometer ancient footrace to be. I, possessing the cardiovascular health of a heavy smoker who worked in IT, tapped out exactly three hundred meters past the starting line, claiming a severe hamstring injury. Queen Kunti patted my shoulder sympathetically and handed me a bowl of unauthorized kheer. 

But the Pandavas were built differently. They ripped off their cloaks—relying on the sheer speed of their run to blur their identities—and shot to the front of the pack. 

By the 15-kilometer mark, Arjuna had easily pulled ahead. His perfect stride, his flawless breathing mechanics, and his sheer arrogance carried him effortlessly past the struggling masses of Indraprastha. He looked absolutely bored, barely breaking a sweat, pacing himself purely to enjoy the inevitable victory. 

But as Arjuna rounded the final corner towards the finish line, tragedy struck. Or rather, a heavily armored, aggressively dramatic tragedy struck. 

From completely outside the designated running path, sprinting at an absolute breakneck pace, burst a new runner. He wasn't wearing athletic linen. He was wearing full, polished gold plate armor, and his earrings physically glowed in the sunlight. 

It was Karna. 

Karna had apparently heard about the marathon, traveled across the subcontinent, and literally jumped into the race at the final kilometer for the exclusive, terrifyingly petty purpose of beating Arjuna. 

Arjuna's eyes went wide. "How did you get here?!" Arjuna yelled, breaking his perfect stride to sprint desperately. 

"I heard there was a competition!" Karna yelled back, actively adjusting his golden arm-guards while sprinting at a record-breaking pace. "I am morally obligated to challenge you at every conceivable physical milestone! I will not be surpassed in cardio, Arjuna!"

"You're wearing full armor!" Arjuna shrieked. "That's completely un-aerodynamic!"

"My tragic backstory provides sufficient lift!" Karna shouted back. 

The two greatest warriors of the age sprinted neck-and-neck down the final stretch, utterly ignoring the screaming crowds. They crossed the finish line at the exact same millisecond, crashing through the ceremonial silk ribbon, completely ruining King Takshaka’s planned photo finish with the local champion. 

Takshaka’s PR painters frantically tried to paint the chaotic, sweaty tie, but the moment the race ended, Arjuna physically turned his back on Karna. 

"I am uninterested," Arjuna sneered, looking incredibly flustered and furiously trying to fix his ruined hair. "You joined late. A technical foul. The Guinness records would never accept this. I am going to find a cold bath."

Arjuna stalked away, radiating pure, furious annoyance, leaving Karna standing at the finish line looking bizarrely disappointed. Karna hadn't come just to race. He had a mission. 

Ten minutes later, in a quiet, heavily shadowed corner of a local Indraprastha open-air restaurant, Karna finally managed to corner our incognito group. 

We were huddled around a large wooden table behind a tapestry, aggressively consuming massive bowls of hot lentil stew. 

"I finally found you," Karna gasped, pushing past the tapestry and dramatically leaning on his golden mace. "Do you have any idea how hard it is to track a group of 'dead' princes? Hastinapur thinks you were incinerated at Varnavata."

"We are currently legally classified as ash, yes," Yudhishthira nodded, taking a prim bite of his stew. "Why were you tracking us?"

"Because," Karna explained, grabbing a chair and sitting heavily, looking completely exhausted from sprinting in metal pants. "The entire geopolitical structure of Hastinapur is collapsing. Everyone assumes that Suyodhana ordered the arson."

I nearly dropped my spoon. My eyes widened. "They blame Suyodhana?"

"Yes!" Karna groaned, rubbing his temples in utter frustration. "And his PR campaign is a total disaster! He keeps aggressively telling the court that he had absolutely nothing to do with it, but because he spent the week beforehand loudly telling everyone he was going to a 'superior luxury spa' while you were sent to a cheap cabin in the woods, the focus groups think he is a callous murderer!"

"Oho," Bhima laughed darkly from across the table, finishing his fifth bowl of stew. "Karma bada kutta hunda hai!" *(Karma is a big dog!)*

"It's a complete crisis," Karna continued, looking desperately at Yudhishthira. "Suyodhana’s poll numbers have dropped to zero. He is universally hated. Even Dhritarashtra couldn't think of a pun to lighten the mood. The kingdom is furious. I came to find you so you could return and publicly exonerate him! Tell them the truth, that you survived!"

Yudhishthira carefully wiped his mouth with a linen napkin. "We cannot return, Karna. Our strategic exile is mandated by the universe. Or rather, by Mother Kunti."

Karna turned his desperate, intense eyes toward Queen Kunti, who was sitting at the head of the table. 

"My Queen," Karna pleaded, his heroic voice softening. "Please. If you do not return, Suyodhana's reputation will be permanently shattered. The kingdom will tear itself apart over this fake assassination."

Kunti stared at Karna. She looked at his glowing earrings. She looked at his tragic, beautiful face. The internal emotional struggle happening behind her eyes was physically painful to watch. She was staring directly at her secret first-born son, the boy she had abandoned in a river, actively begging her for help. 

Suddenly, Kunti stood up. She grabbed Karna by the wrist. With her other hand, she grabbed my wrist. 

"Both of you," Kunti whispered fiercely, her voice trembling with barely controlled maternal hysteria. "Come with me to the next table."

She dragged both Karna and me exactly four feet away to a smaller, empty table. She aggressively shoved us down into two chairs and began furiously serving us extra bowls of stew, completely ignoring the deeply confused looks from the Pandavas. 

"Eat," Kunti commanded, aggressively pushing a bowl toward Karna. "You are too thin. That heavy armor is terrible for your posture."

Karna blinked, utterly bewildered by the sudden maternal aggression from the mother of his bitter rivals. "My Queen... I do not need stew. I need a political exoneration."

"And you," Kunti turned to me, aggressively pushing a second bowl of kheer into my hands. "Do not slump, Rahul. Eat the kheer. Both of my... both of you... need to build your strength for the harsh reality of the world."

Karna looked at me, profound confusion written across his face. 

"She thinks I'm her son," I whispered to Karna, desperately hoping to defuse the situation. "It’s a long story involving a Maruti 800 and bad ergonomics. Just eat the stew, man. Do not argue with her when the maternal instinct activates."

Karna cautiously took a bite of the stew, staring at Kunti with an expression that shifted from confusion to a deep, terrible sadness he couldn't quite articulate. 

"We will not return to Hastinapur, Karna," Kunti said softly, watching him eat with tear-filled eyes. "Tell Suyodhana he must survive his own PR disaster. The Pandavas remain dead to the world until the time is right."

Karna slowly put his spoon down. He looked at Kunti, then at the table of disguised Pandavas sitting behind the tapestry. He could have exposed us. He could have run back to Dhritarashtra and ruined our incognito sanctuary. 

Instead, he stood up, gave a deep, genuinely respectful bow to Kunti, and nodded. 

"Your secret is safe with me, My Queen," Karna said quietly. "I will tell Suyodhana I searched the ashes and found nothing but silence. I swear it on my honor."

As Karna turned to leave the restaurant, Bhima suddenly shoved his chair back and lumbered over. 

"Oye Sunehri murgi!" Bhima boomed, wiping stew off his thick beard. *(Hey Golden Chicken!)* 

Karna tensed, immediately grabbing the hilt of his sword, assuming Bhima was about to initiate combat. 

Instead, Bhima threw his massive arms around Karna, completely engulfing the armored warrior in a terrifying, bone-crushing hug that lifted Karna entirely off his feet. 

"Tu banda ghaint hai!" Bhima laughed loudly in Punjabi, slapping Karna’s metal back so hard a dent formed. "Bhavein tera style thoda over-dramatic hai, par tera waada pakka hai! Agar Navada aana hove, taan mainu zaroor daseen, main tainu apni special lassi pilawanga!" *(You are a good guy! Even though your style is a little over-dramatic, but your promise is solid! If you ever come to Navada, tell me definitely, I will make you drink my special lassi!)*

Karna, unable to breathe, just nodded weakly. 

When Bhima finally dropped him, Karna staggered backward, gave one last deeply confused, slightly concussed look to our table, and sprinted out of the restaurant toward Hastinapur. 

We had successfully navigated our first major crisis in Indraprastha. We had survived an encounter with our greatest rival and secured our secret identity. 

But as we turned to leave the restaurant, a small squadron of incredibly polished, heavily armed Naga palace guards blocked the exit. 

In the center of the squadron stood King Takshaka. He was no longer smiling for the PR painters. He looked directly at our group, his eyes cold, calculating, and intensely sharp. 

"Well," Takshaka drawled, tapping a jeweled cane against the stone floor. "This is certainly an interesting plot twist. The dead princes of Hastinapur, hiding out in my city, eating my stew, and casually securing promises from the enemy commander. That is a brilliant piece of geopolitical theater."

The Pandavas instantly froze. Hands went to hidden weapons under their cloaks. 

"I suggest you do not draw steel in my city, boys," Takshaka smiled, a thin, deeply unpleasant expression. "Because if you do, I will ensure every newspaper, town crier, and royal messenger in Bharata prints your exact coordinates by dawn. And then, your quiet little vacation is over permanently."

We were trapped. King Takshaka had us exactly where he wanted us.


Twenty minutes later, we were entirely disarmed and standing in the heavily guarded, immensely opulent private chambers of King Takshaka. 

Takshaka poured himself a glass of expensive wine, casually sipping it while staring at the greatest warriors of Hastinapur as if they were slightly irritating interns who had broken the office printer. 

"I have known for weeks," Takshaka stated simply, setting the glass down. "Vidura’s encrypted carrier pigeons might have been accidentally consumed by your voracious brother, but my own intelligence network relies on snakes. And snakes are incredibly hard to digest. My serpents tracked your entire chaotic migration from the ashes of Varnavata straight to the dusty boredom of Nayada."

"If you knew we were here," Yudhishthira said, nervously adjusting his glasses, "why did you not inform Hastinapur? The regulatory compliance guidelines regarding missing royalty—"

"Because I am a politician, Yudhishthira," Takshaka interrupted smoothly. "Knowledge is leverage. Hastinapur is currently suffering a massive economic downturn due to the PR crisis surrounding your 'deaths'. The stock market in Gandhara is heavily shorting their grain exports. If I reveal you are alive, the crisis ends. Hastinapur stabilizes. And I lose my tactical advantage."

"So you are going to hold us hostage?" Arjuna demanded fiercely. 

"Hostage is such a harsh, outdated term," Takshaka clicked his tongue disappointedly. "I prefer the term 'mandatory contractual employment'. You see, you have a problem: you need to remain completely hidden from the world until you formulate a strategy to deal with your psychopathic author Vyasa. And I have a problem: I am running the largest, most aggressive geopolitical empire in Bharata, and my staff is wildly incompetent."

Takshaka smiled. It was a terrifyingly brilliant, capitalistic smile. 

"I am going to blackmail you into working for me," Takshaka announced happily. 

The Pandavas stared at him in profound disbelief. They had expected torture, execution, or magical imprisonment. They had not expected forced corporate administration. 

"I have already drafted the job descriptions," Takshaka continued, walking down the line of completely bewildered princes. 

He stopped in front of Bhima. "You. Bhima. My royal chefs are terrible. You are going to take complete control of the central palace kitchen. And if you so much as mention the word 'Tandoori-Momo-Fusion' to my guests, I will personally throw you into the Ganges. I expect five-star Michelin quality banquets."

Bhima’s eyes lit up. "Oye! Pakki gal hai! Main tere kitchen nu swarg bana danga!" *(Hey! It's a deal! I will make your kitchen a heaven!)* 

Takshaka ignored the Punjabi enthusiasm and moved to Arjuna. "Arjuna. The clandestine special forces of Indraprastha are highly underperforming in their archery metrics. You will be their secret, completely unauthorized trainer. You will fix their aerodynamic deficiencies."

Arjuna huffed, crossing his arms, but I could clearly see a glint of excitement in his eyes. He loved correcting people's form. 

"Yudhishthira," Takshaka said next. "You are functionally obsessed with rules and compliance. You will be my Chief Strategic Advisor regarding loophole management and bureaucratic obstruction. When I want to delay a treaty with Magadha, you will draft a fifty-page compliance report that legally paralyzes their entire senate."

Yudhishthira practically vibrated with excitement. "A fifty-page compliance report? I can give you eighty pages! I will cite precedents from the Satapatha Brahmana!"

"Nakula," Takshaka sighed, looking at the glowing, flawless face of the fourth prince. "You are completely useless in a modern economy. However, my personal barber was recently fired. You possess an aggressive obsession with aesthetic perfection. You will manage my hair, my beard, and my seasonal manicures."

"Finally!" Nakula gasped, touching his chest in pure relief. "A position that fully utilizes my divine potential! You have excellent bone structure, Your Majesty, but your split ends are screaming for a deep herbal condition!"

Takshaka quickly moved past Nakula, terrified by the enthusiasm. He stopped in front of Sahadeva. 

"The Prophet," Takshaka murmured. "I don't need you to tell me the future. The future is depressing. But I do need you to monitor the psychic frequencies of the surrounding kingdoms. You are my new Director of Intelligence. Just sit in a dark room and tell me if anyone is plotting to assassinate me."

"I accept," Sahadeva said smoothly. "If my dark room is equipped with comfortable pillows and no one interrupts my... trances."

"Done," Takshaka agreed. Finally, he turned to me. 

The most powerful politician in the ancient world stared at a middle management tech employee from 2026. 

"And you," Takshaka said softly, his eyes narrowing slightly. "The anomaly. My spies reported your absolutely catastrophic escape from the Chakravyuh. You didn't fight. You successfully diagnosed a structural geometric loop and exploited the system latency. That behavior is not ancient. That behavior is... uniquely modern."

I swallowed hard. "I'm just a guy who knows how to spot bad coding."

"Exactly," Takshaka smiled. "I don't know who you really are, Rahul. I don't know where you came from. But my intelligence network also reported the highly lucrative, aggressive 5,000-year non-compete clause Shakuni tricked you into signing for MBHT Media. A man who gets targeted by Shakuni for economic monopolies is a man who knows things regarding market trends."

Takshaka walked over and put a heavy hand on my shoulder. 

"You are going to be my Senior Investment Analyst," Takshaka declared. "You are going to look at the regional markets, and you are going to tell me exactly where to place my treasury funds. You will optimize my portfolio. And in exchange, I will ensure Hastinapur never learns that their dead princes are currently operating my kitchen and cutting my hair."

"I... I can do that," I nodded, terrified but relieved. "I can build you an ancient Excel sheet."

"Excellent," Takshaka clapped his hands together warmly, the ruthless politician instantly replaced by the aggressively friendly PR persona. "Welcome to the team! HR will process your onboarding documents in the morning!"


To truly grasp the absolute absurdity of our mandatory corporate employment under King Takshaka, one must understand how aggressively the Pandavas leaned into their assigned roles over the next three weeks. 

Despite being literally blackmailed, the princes of Hastinapur possessed an intense, almost pathological need to overachieve at whatever task they were given. 

Bhima, for instance, did not just take over the royal kitchens. He established a culinary dictatorship. 

On his third day as Chief Culinary Officer, Bhima called a massive mandatory staff meeting in the central courtyard for all seventy-five of Takshaka’s highly trained Naga chefs. Bhima stood on a large sack of grain, wearing a silk apron that was visibly straining against his massive chest. 

"Oye suno saare!" Bhima roared, pacing back and forth angrily. "Main kal raat di inventory check kiti hai! Tuhade paas na te properly aged basmati hai, aur na hi decent quality da desi ghee! Ae ki mazaak hai?! Takshaka da kitchen hai ya koi sasta dhaba?!" *(Listen everyone! I checked the inventory last night! You neither have properly aged basmati, nor decent quality desi ghee! What is this joke?! Is this Takshaka's kitchen or some cheap roadside stall?!)*

A terrified, trembling sous-chef raised his hand. "Lord Bhima... the King prefers a very light, herb-based diet. He is very conscious of his PR image and wishes to remain, quote, 'serpentine and slender'."

Bhima stared at the chef as if the man had just insulted his mother. 

"Slender?!" Bhima bellowed, aggressively grabbing a nearby massive bronze cauldron with one hand and lifting it above his head to demonstrate sheer protein-based power. "Slender is for weaklings who sit in air-conditioned offices! A warrior runs on heavily buttered parathas! From today, we are changing the entire menu! I am introducing the 'Hastinapur Bulk-Up Protocol'! The King will eat paneer! The King will eat laddoos! By the end of the month, Takshaka will be so thick he won't be able to fit through his own PR portraits!"

True to his word, Bhima began secretly injecting massive amounts of premium fats into Takshaka’s "light salads". Within two weeks, the most ruthless politician in Bharata actually had to ask his tailors to let out the seams on his emerald-green tunics, complaining loudly about sudden water retention. 

Arjuna's tenure as the clandestine Special Forces trainer was equally disastrous, mostly because Arjuna physically could not comprehend the concept of moderate athletic ability. 

I snuck out of my investment office one afternoon and walked down to the hidden underground archery range to check on him. 

Arjuna was standing in front of a squad of twenty elite Naga assassins. These were men trained to kill silently in the dead of night. Arjuna, however, was treating them like remedial kindergarteners who had failed basic geometry. 

"No, no, no!" Arjuna practically screamed, snatching a bow out of a terrifyingly scarred assassin's hands. "Your draw-curvature is entirely misaligned! You are leaking at least fifteen percent of your kinetic potential into the lateral wind-shear! Draupadi would be disgusted! Look at your elbow! It's practically pointing at the ground like a withered branch!"

"Sir," the assassin grunted, his eyes flashing dangerously. "I have killed forty men with this exact posture."

"And you could have killed sixty if you understood basic aerodynamics!" Arjuna shot back fiercely, violently correcting the assassin's grip. "Forty is a C-minus performance! I am surrounded by amateurs! Tomorrow, we are doing a full twelve-hour theoretical workshop on structural fletching!"

The assassins looked absolutely miserable. They were hardened killers currently being subjected to the ancient equivalent of an agonizingly boring corporate PowerPoint presentation. 

Nakula, however, was thriving. 

Entrusted with Takshaka’s grooming, Nakula immediately initiated a hostile takeover of the entire aesthetic department. He fired three royal advisors simply because their robes clashed with the throne room drapes.

"King Takshaka," Nakula announced one morning, aggressively walking circles around the seated monarch, holding a massive pumice stone. "Your PR painters are incompetent. They are capturing you from the eastern angle. The eastern angle aggressively highlights your jaw-tension. We need to pivot to the southwestern lighting parameter immediately, or your approval ratings will drop amongst the key demographic of visually literate aristocrats."

Takshaka, the terrifying blackmailing overlord, actually looked slightly intimidated by Nakula’s aggressive beauty-consulting. 

"Southwestern lighting?" Takshaka asked nervously, instinctively touching his own jaw. "Are you sure? My PR team said the east showcases my benevolence."

"Your PR team are peasants wearing unbleached linens," Nakula scoffed, aggressively applying a thick layer of crushed pearl-paste to Takshaka’s forehead. "Trust me. By next week, you will look so incredibly luminous the citizens will willingly pay higher taxes just to maintain your skincare routine."

Ironically, I was the only one genuinely struggling with my job. 

While the Pandavas essentially bullied the most powerful ruler in Bharata into submission through overwhelming competence, I was stuck trying to invent an ancient Excel spreadsheet using a piece of chalk and a massive slate board. 

"Rahul," Takshaka had said on my fourth day, pacing in my windowless office. "The Magadha trade delegates are arriving tomorrow. I need an optimized fiscal projection on the proposed silk tariffs. Can you model the amortization schedule?"

"Amortization schedule," I repeated, sweat dripping down the back of my neck. I was a backend software engineer. I barely understood my own tax returns in 2026, let alone the macro-economic policies of a prehistoric empire. "Yes. Absolutely. Let me just... run the numbers through the grid."

I spent fourteen consecutive hours drawing agonizingly complex grids on the slate board, ultimately producing a financial model that was essentially just a series of random, highly confident guesses loosely based on a YouTube video I watched in 2023 about crypto-currency. 

Miraculously, my "Silicon Valley Confidence" actually worked. When I presented my entirely fictionalized market projections to Takshaka, loudly using phrases like "bullish momentum" and "diversified portfolio leveraging," the ancient King was so baffled by the corporate jargon that he simply assumed I was a financial genius. 

"Astonishing," Takshaka whispered, staring at my completely meaningless chalk graph. "You have visualized the economic ether. We will implement the 'Rahul Protocol' immediately."

It was exhausting. Maintaining the facade of a financial prodigy while actively terrified of being executed for fraud was mentally draining. Which is exactly why the commute back to Nayada every night was so profoundly soul-crushing.


And so began the most profoundly depressing, deeply realistic phase of my entire mythological career. 

I was officially living in ancient Noida and commuting every single day into the urban sprawl of ancient Delhi to work a corporate desk job for a ruthless overlord. 

Every morning at 5:30 AM, our heavily disguised convoy would leave the quiet, dusty suburbs of Nayada in Sector 15. The commute to the central palace of Indraprastha took exactly two hours, largely because the main trading route was aggressively choked with slow-moving merchant caravans, herds of confused goats, and the ancient equivalent of mid-level managers aggressively yelling at charioteers to move faster. 

 It was the literal Bronze Age origin story of the Noida-Delhi peak traffic hour. 

"I cannot handle this commute!" Nakula complained one Tuesday morning, aggressively covering his face with a silk scarf to avoid the dust as our chariot sat motionless behind a massive caravan of timber. "My pores are absorbing the anxiety of the lower classes!"

"Use the time productively, Nakula," Yudhishthira advised, holding a large clay tablet and a stylus. "I have drafted fourteen new HR addendums regarding the proper distribution of Takshaka's water flasks during public events."

I sat in the back of the chariot, staring miserably at the endless line of traffic, feeling an extreme, crushing wave of déjà vu. I hadn't traveled back in time to change the course of human destiny. I had traveled back in time to reinvent the soul-crushing misery of the NCR daily commute. 

But I refused to be a corporate drone forever. I spent my long days in a small, windowless office near Takshaka’s treasury, building "ancient algorithms" (mostly just basic multiplication tables written on palm leaves) to optimize his tax collections. And while I did that, I was silently, carefully planning our escape. 

I was conceptualizing an independent startup. A venture that the Pandavas and I could launch independently of Takshaka’s oppressive monopoly and Hastinapur’s violent legacy. We just needed capital, a solid localized base of operations, and a fiercely competent CEO to run the day-to-day administration.

The answer to all our problems arrived six months later via the morning gossip in Takshaka’s royal kitchens. 

We were huddled in the pantry during our designated fifteen-minute lunch break, aggressively eating Bhima’s newly perfected "Indraprastha-Style Chicken Tikka." 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima mumbled around a mouthful of chicken, tossing a crumpled parchment at me. "Ae dekh! Sabji wale bhaaji kende ne ki Panchaal de raje ne apni kudi da Swayamvar rakhya hai! Draupadi da!" *(Hey Rahul! Look! The vegetable-seller brother says the King of Panchaal has hosted his daughter's Swayamvar! Draupadi's!)*

I unrolled the parchment. It was a massive, highly produced royal flyer. 

King Drupad, having realized that his designated son-in-law had "tragically perished" in a highly suspected arson attack, was officially reopening the market for his daughter’s hand. He was hosting a grand Swayamvar. The challenge was notoriously impossible: the suitor had to string a massive, unyielding bow and shoot a rapidly moving target while looking only at its reflection in a pool of oil. 

Arjuna, who was sitting on a sack of basmati rice, nearly dropped his lunch. 

"A moving target?" Arjuna whispered, his eyes widening in pure, unadulterated aerodynamic obsession. "Shot entirely via refractive aqueous targeting? That is... that is the most technically complex challenge I have ever heard of. I must go. I must demonstrate my structural supremacy."

"Absolutely not!" Yudhishthira immediately shouted, nearly dropping his compliance report into the tikka sauce. "It is an incredibly high-profile event! All the kings of Bharata will be there! If we go, we violate our incognito status, breach Takshaka’s mandatory employment contract, and completely jeopardize our stable suburban lifestyle in Nayada!"

"Stable lifestyle?" I snapped, throwing the parchment onto the table. "Yudhishthira, you are currently drafting fake tax regulations for a man who loves his own statues! We are corporate slaves! We cannot live in Nayada forever!"

"Nayada is safe!" Yudhishthira argued fiercely. "The commute is brutal, yes, but the property values are slowly rising! I feel a deep, comforting sense of middle-class security!"

At that moment, Sahadeva slipped into the pantry. He must have tuned into the conversation telepathically. 

"He is entrenched," Sahadeva whispered to me, nodding toward his eldest brother. "The corporate matrix has absorbed his spirit. You must break his programming."

I took a deep breath. I channeled the intense, highly toxic, incredibly persuasive energy of every single tech-bro entrepreneur I had ever met in my life. I channeled the spirit of late-night podcast hustle-culture. 

I stood up, pointing aggressively at Yudhishthira. 

"Yudhishthira," I said, my voice dropping an octave into a low, intense rhythm. "Security is the ultimate myth. You are clinging to the safety of suburban Nayada because you are afraid of market disruption. But true growth? True holistic development? It only happens outside your comfort zone! Tell me something, Brother. Do you really want to spend the rest of your life commuting from a dusty, rusty mud-brick house in Nayada? Or do you want to secure the ultimate corporate dream? Don't you want to own a massive, glass-fronted luxury mansion in GuruGramaha?!"

Yudhishthira blinked, slightly confused but instantly hooked by the real-estate angle. "My... comfort zone? A mansion in GuruGramaha? They have private swimming pools..."

"You are thinking like an employee!" I yelled, slapping the table. "You need to think like a Founder! What is our ultimate goal? A unified, peaceful, highly optimized kingdom! Can we build that while working mid-level admin jobs for Takshaka? No!"

"He's right, Brother," Sahadeva chimed in, sounding exactly like a synchronized hype-man. "We are lacking visionary equity."

"We need a localized startup!" I continued, aggressively leaning into Yudhishthira's personal space. "We need to construct our own empire! But we are missing a critical component! We are all backend developers and operational heavy-lifters! We lack a Chief Executive Officer! A brilliant, politically savvy, relentlessly aggressive female founder who can manage the front-end PR and secure external funding!"

"Draupadi," Arjuna whispered reverently from his sack of rice. "Her organizational margins were flawless."

"Exactly!" I pointed at Arjuna. "Draupadi is the ultimate co-founder! She is the missing piece of our synergistic puzzle! If Arjuna wins the Swayamvar, we don't just win a wife, we secure a top-tier geopolitical consultant and automatic Series A funding from Panchaal!"

Yudhishthira’s bureaucratic resistance visibly cracked. The pure, aggressive buzzwords were overwhelming his cautious logic. 

"A top-tier geopolitical consultant?" Yudhishthira muttered, his eyes darting back and forth. "With... with established infrastructural funding?" 

"And a proven track record of reforming archaic HR models!" I pushed harder. "If we stay here, we die as middle-management. If we go to the Swayamvar, we disrupt the global market and establish our own monopoly! What would Elon Musk do, Yudhishthira?! Would he stay in Nayada?!"

"I do not know who Elon Musk is," Yudhishthira gasped, clutching his head, "but I assume he would violently aggressively pursue the market acquisition!"

"YES!" Bhima roared, slamming his fists on the table, sending the chicken tikka flying. "Asi apna dhandha shuru karange! Panchaal challo!" *(We will start our own business! Let's go to Panchaal!)*

"It is settled," Nakula nodded, wiping a tear from his eye. "I will pack my exfoliating scrubs. A Swayamvar requires optimal hydration."

The Pandavas were completely unified. The corporate brainwashing of Nayada had been successfully shattered by the raw, aggressive power of tech-bro hustle culture. We were going back to Panchaal. We were going to hijack the greatest matrimonial event in ancient history purely for the sake of securing a highly competent startup CEO. 

Sahadeva looked at me, a highly rare, terrifyingly excited smile breaking across his depressed face. 

"Rahul," Sahadeva whispered happily. "The next plot arc is going to be spectacularly chaotic."
