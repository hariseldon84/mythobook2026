# Chapter 4: The Start-Up Pitch

It took exactly fourteen days for the universe’s most lethal timeline anomaly to become Hastinapur’s favorite middle management executive.

I was officially introduced to the Royal Court by Sahadeva as the new "Chief Product Officer of the Hastinapur Distributed Relay Network." To my utter astonishment, nobody questioned it. When you confidently throw around terms like "synergized deliverables" and "cross-platform integration" in 3200 BCE, bronze-age royals assume it’s high-level Vedic sorcery and leave you completely alone.

The only mandate came from Yudhishthira, who filed a formal sixteen-page grievance regarding my "distracting, leg-suffocating blue garments." Consequently, I was now walking around the palace in a flowing white silk dhoti and an embroidered upper cloth, trying very hard not to trip over my own hems while looking like an extra in an aggressively budgeted mythological soap opera.

And honestly? I was thriving.

Once the immediate threat of universal paradox erasure was safely contained within Sahadeva’s 40-kilometer radius, the ancient royal court felt bizarrely like a high-end corporate offsite. The bitter rivalry between the Pandavas and Kauravas hadn’t fully fermented into genocidal warfare yet. Right now, they were just hyper-competitive frat boys with unlimited access to the royal treasury. And they *loved* me.

Mostly because I introduced them to junk food.

"Oye, Raool!" 

A hand the size of a microwave oven slammed onto my shoulder, nearly dislocating it for the second time that month. I turned to find Bhima, covered in flour and grinning like a maniac. 

"Raool puttar!" Bhima bellowed in thunderous Punjabi. "Tu ithe aa! Tainu main nayi invention dikhawan! Tera oh gol-gol bread wala idea? Kamaal kar ditta tu!" *(Rahul son! Come here! Let me show you a new invention! That round bread idea of yours? You've done wonders!)*

Bhima dragged me into the suffocating heat of the royal kitchens. There, resting on a silver platter, was a massive, slightly misshapen circle of flatbread. It was smothered in freshly churned buffalo cheese, roasted venison, spiced tomatoes, and terrifying amounts of green chilies. 

"Look at this!" Bhima roared, hefting the world’s first pizza. "I call it the 'Bhima-Chakra'! Tu kha ke dekh, swaad aa jayega! Tere uss Italy-Vitely de lokaan nu bhool jayega tu!" *(I call it the Bhima-Chakra! Taste it, you'll love it! You'll forget your Italy people!)*

I took a bite. It was, objectively, a cardiac arrest on a plate, but it tasted incredible. 

"It’s a masterpiece, Bhrata Bhima," I choked out, my eyes watering from the spice. 

"Hai na?!" Bhima laughed, grabbing me in a bear hug that cracked two of my ribs. "Te naal tera oh kaala paani vi banaya main! Ki kehnda si tu usnu... Coca-Cola? Main usda naam badal ditta. Aaj ton eh 'Bhim Cola' hai!" *(Right?! And I also made that black water of yours! What did you call it... Coca-Cola? I changed its name. From today, it's 'Bhim Cola'!)*

He shoved a clay goblet into my hands. It was filled with a dark, aggressively bubbling liquid made from jaggery, soda ash, and some highly questionable unfermented spices. I took a sip. It burned exactly like a cheap local cola, and hit my bloodstream with the force of a sugar-coated sledgehammer.

"Bhim Cola," I wheezed. "It’s going to be a global phenomenon."

"Oye shera!" Bhima slapped my back. "Ajj raat nu Dushasana de naal party hai! Tu aana hai zaroor! Tera oh nawa dance sikhaana hai ajj!" *(O Lion! Tonight there's a party with Dushasana! You must come! We have to learn your new dance today!)*

"You mean Bhangra?"

"Aho! Te oh duja vi... ki si... Rock and Roll!" 

Yes. I had somehow managed to bridge the deepest mythological divide in human history by teaching the Kaurava and Pandava princes how to do the Bhangra to heavy rock music. 

Later that evening, the royal courtyard looked like a frat house before a massive football game. Vikarna, my designated best friend in the Kaurava camp, handed me a chalice of somras while we watched the sheer absurdity unfold.

"Your cultural contributions are terrifying, peasant," Vikarna drawled, packing his clay pipe. 

In the center of the courtyard, Dushasana was currently trying to execute a moonwalk while wearing his traditional silver ghungroos. 

"E ka Raool Bhaiya!" Dushasana yelled dynamically in his thick Bihari accent, pointing at his feet. "Hamar charanwa piche jaat baa, par hum aage dekh rahal bani! Ee toh bilkul jaadu baa! Tani aur fast wala musicwa bajaiye!" *(What Rahul brother! My feet are going backward, but I am looking forward! This is absolute magic! Play some faster music!)* 

"Just drop the beat, Dushasana!" I yelled back, feeling the somras kicking in. "Give them the Bhangra-Rock fusion!"

Dushasana immediately threw his hands in the air and started doing a wildly aggressive, perfectly synchronized Bhangra step, mixing his classical Kathak footwork with what looked like an ancient version of headbanging. 

Bhima jumped into the circle, waving a massive chicken leg like a glowstick, and started doing the same step. Soon, half the Kuru princes were aggressively dancing to a drumbeat I had taught the royal musicians, screaming "HOY! HOY! HOY!" into the ancient night sky.

Vikarna sighed. "I give it two weeks before Yudhishthira files an injunction against unauthorized joy." 

***

It wasn't just the princes who loved me. King Dhritarashtra had formally appointed me to a role that I suspect he invented solely to have someone to bounce his terrible jokes off. 

At least three times a week, I was summoned to the grand throne room for my duties as the "Senior Tech Advisor to the Crown."

"Rahul!" Dhritarashtra boomed as I entered, his blind eyes staring slightly to the left of where I was actually standing. "My favorite Product Manager! Come here, come here! I have an incredible update for the kingdom’s infrastructure network!"

"Yes, My King?" I said, bowing deeply and adjusting my dhoti.

"Why..." Dhritarashtra wheezed, leaning forward on his golden throne, "did the palace servant get fired from the royal mint?!"

I closed my eyes. "I don't know, My King. Why?"

"Because he lacked *common cents*!" Dhritarashtra slapped his knee, roaring with laughter.

The guards leveled their spears. "LAUGH AND REJOICE!"

"HAHAHAHA! BRIGHTEST MIND OF OUR GENERATION!" the ministers screamed in absolute terror. 

I fake-laughed so hard my lungs hurt. "Brilliant, Maharaja. An unparalleled metaphor for fiscal responsibility."

"I know!" Dhritarashtra beamed. "You understand my genius, Rahul! You’re not like Bhishma. That man hasn't laughed since the Bronze Age started. Now, about this 'Bhim Cola' you've invented... can we tax it?"

"We can put a 28% GST on it, My King," I replied seamlessly, sliding back into my corporate persona. "Goods and Somras Tax."

"Write that down!" he yelled to a terrified scribe. "Genius!"

***

There was only one relationship that was consistently, profoundly awkward.

Queen Mother Kunti.

Ever since the archery tournament, Kunti had been looking at me with an expression of deep, tragic, maternal confusion. Because of my 21st-century anxiety and overall stressed demeanor, she was overwhelmingly convinced that I was the accidental byproduct of her magical boon—another secret son, much like Karna, just terribly dressed and possessing zero combat skills.

I was walking down the corridor one afternoon when she suddenly stepped out from behind a carved stone pillar, clutching a silver bowl.

"Rahul," she whispered, her voice trembling with emotion.

"My Queen," I bowed awkwardly.

She stepped closer, tears shining in her eyes. She raised a trembling hand and gently patted my cheek. "You look so thin, beta. So burdened by the world. Does your... does your lower back hurt from sitting in front of those 'laptop' machines you talk about in your sleep?"

"Uh, sometimes? But it's mostly bad posture—"

"Shhh," she interrupted, pressing the silver bowl into my hands. "Do not speak of your pain. I have made special kheer for you. Made from the milk of our finest cows. Eat it, my child. And remember..." A single tear rolled down her cheek. "I am always watching over you. Even if we cannot announce it to the world. A mother knows."

"My Queen, I really am just an IT guy from East Delhi, my parents are actu—"

"So brave," she sobbed, completely ignoring me, and hurried down the corridor, aggressively dabbing her eyes with her silk pallu. 

I stood there holding the kheer. I later told Sahadeva about it. He stared at a wall for ten minutes and then quietly requested I stop telling him things that actively aged his soul.

***

Not everyone was happy with the new status quo. In fact, the two most powerful men in Hastinapur were absolutely livid that the royal palace had devolved into a frat house featuring Bhim Cola and late-night pizza parties.

I was walking back from the kitchens late one night when I heard harsh, low voices echoing from the grand library. I ducked behind a massive bronze statue of Lord Indra and peered around the edge. 

It was Bhishma. And pacing furiously in front of him was Sage Vyasa.

"I cannot work under these conditions!" Vyasa hissed, waving a bundle of palm leaves in the air. "I am trying to write a sweeping, multi-generational epic of tragedy, duty, and catastrophic warfare! I need tension! I need blood! I need a cosmic struggle between right and wrong!"

"And what do you have?" Bhishma grumbled, looking like he wanted to punch a wall.

"I have Dushasana organizing a 'battle of the bands' in the main courtyard!" Vyasa screamed. "I have Bhima refusing to practice with his mace because his—what did he call it?—his 'pizza dough needs to proof'! This Rahul boy is ruining my narrative arc! The cousins aren't plotting against each other! They are forming a startup!"

Bhishma rubbed his temples. "I tried to explain the concept of Kshatriya Dharma to them this morning. Duryodhana asked me if Dharma was a scalable business model. I almost threw myself into the Ganges."

"We need to get rid of him," Vyasa muttered darkly, aggressively scratching out a line of text on his leaf. "I had this beautiful scene planned where Duryodhana tries to poison Bhima's food. Classic, elegant betrayal."

"And now?"

"Now, Bhima has Rahul taste-testing everything for 'flavor profiles'! The poison plot is completely ruined! I'm going to have to resort to a complicated real estate dispute to start the war. A real estate dispute, Bhishma! The Iliad got a war over the most beautiful woman in the world! I am going to have to start an epic war over a forest clearing in Khandavprastha!"

I clapped my hands over my mouth to stop from laughing out loud. 

"Patience, Vyasa," Bhishma growled. "The boy is a distraction. The core tensions remain. We just need to wait for the right trigger. Perhaps a game of dice. Something simple."

"It better work," Vyasa grumbled. "If this 'Bhim Cola' goes public, I'm retiring to the Himalayas."

***

Months passed. The weather turned cooler, the timeline remained horrifyingly stable, and I slowly began to accept that my life now consisted of dodging Vyasa’s angry stares, eating Kunti's guilt-kheer, and helping Sahadeva run the backend operations of the Bronze Age internet.

And then, everything went sharply, terrifyingly off the rails.

I was summoned to the guest quarters of the Gandharan delegation. Shakuni’s chambers. 

I walked into a room that was perpetually draped in shadows. The air was thick with incense and the clatter of bone dice. Shakuni was sitting cross-legged on a velvet cushion, meticulously rolling his famous dice on a small wooden table. 

"Ah. The Chief Product Officer," Shakuni sneered, not looking up. "Come in, Rahul. Shut the door."

I shut the door, my stomach tying itself into a knot. "You asked for me, Uncle Shakuni?"

"Sit."

I sat across from him. He finally looked up. His eyes were sharp, calculating, and completely lacking the frantic, frat-boy innocence of the younger princes. He smiled, a thin, deeply unpleasant expression.

"I have a business proposition for you," Shakuni said smoothly.

"I'm honored, Uncle. But I only handle product strategy for the relay network—"

"Oh, save the corporate jargon for the blind king," Shakuni interrupted, waving a dismissive hand. "I am a man of vision, Rahul. I see trends. I see gaps in the market. Look at my YouTube channel, for instance. *Mama Ke Nuskhey*. Fifty thousand subscribers."

I blinked. "I... I have seen it. Your video on 'Top 5 Ways to Gaslight Your Nephews' has excellent audience retention."

"Exactly. Finance. Politics. Mental manipulation. That is my brand," Shakuni leaned forward, steepling his fingers. "But I have realized that my vision is limited by a linear understanding of time. I can only manipulate what is currently happening. But you... you can manipulate what *hasn't* happened."

My blood ran cold. "I don't know what you're talking about."

Shakuni chuckled, a dry, rattling sound. "Please, Rahul. I am the master of secrets. A few months ago, during the archery tournament's after-party... I was taking a quiet stroll near the kitchens. And I happened to overhear a highly fascinating, deeply confusing conversation between you and my psychic nephew Sahadeva."

I stopped breathing. He knew.

"I didn't understand all the words," Shakuni continued, his voice dropping to a dangerous whisper, his eyes locking onto mine. "But I understood enough. Something about a 'mechanical vehicle' in a haystack. Something about 2026. A timeline rupture. You, Rahul Sharma... are not from here. You know the future. You know *everything* that happens."

I opened my mouth to deny it, but my vocal cords had completely paralyzed themselves. 

"Don't worry, my boy. I haven't told Bhishma. I haven't told Vyasa. Why would I give away such a lucrative competitive advantage?" Shakuni smiled broadly now, revealing slightly stained teeth. "I want to launch an agency. A premier stock-market-tips empire and political PR agency. We will call it *MBHT Media*."

"MBHT?" I squeaked. 

"'Mera Bachcha Hai Tu'," Shakuni said proudly. "A brand name signaling trust, family loyalty, and mild emotional manipulation. With your knowledge of future market crashes, real estate booms, and political assassinations, we will make billions. We will short the Hastinapur treasury before the war even starts."

"Uncle Shakuni," I stammered, panic flooding my system. "It doesn't work like that. The timeline—"

"I have already drafted the paperwork," Shakuni pulled out an immensely long, terrifyingly dense palm-leaf contract from his robes. "I just need your thumbprint. It is an exclusive, iron-clad lock-in deal. Standard terms. A 10,000-year non-compete clause."

"Ten... ten thousand years?!" 

"I like to think long-term," Shakuni grinned. "Sign it, Rahul. Or I might accidentally let it slip to Vyasa that you are the reason his beautiful epic is currently sponsored by Bhim Cola."

"I... I need to consult my legal counsel," I blurted out, scrambling backward so fast I tripped over the carpet. 

"Take your time," Shakuni chuckled, tossing the bone dice onto the table. They landed on double sixes. "But not too much time, my boy. The future waits for no one."

I bolted out of his chambers, my heart hammering against my ribs so hard I thought they would crack. I didn't stop running until I reached Sahadeva's room, bursting through the door like a hijacked train. 

"Sahadeva!" I screamed, gasping for air. 

Sahadeva looked up from his scrolls. He didn't look surprised. He just looked deeply, profoundly exhausted.

"Do not tell me," Sahadeva sighed, reaching for his temples. "MBHT Media?"

"HE WANTS A TEN THOUSAND YEAR LOCK-IN PERIOD!" I yelled. 

Sahadeva closed his eyes. "Ask me how we fix this."
