# Chapter 10: The Secret CEO and The Rise of Royal Café

The arrival at our dusty, mud-brick compound in ancient Nayada was perhaps the most crucial atmospheric turning point in the entire epic. 

I stood by the front door, rigid with absolute terror, practically holding my breath as Arjuna and the newly acquired corporate CEO, Princess Draupadi, stepped through the threshold. 

Queen Kunti was standing by the hearth, stirring a pot of lentils. 

Yudhishthira, completely oblivious to the existential timeline threat, opened his mouth. I immediately aggressively elbowed him in the ribs so hard that his glasses nearly flew off his face. 

"Oof!" Yudhishthira gasped, doubling over. "Rahul! What is the meaning of this unprovoked kinetic assault?!"

Kunti turned around. She looked at Arjuna. She looked at Draupadi. She looked at my violently out-of-place cargo shorts. 

"Ah," Kunti smiled warmly, stepping forward and completely ignoring the Yudhishthira anomaly. "My son has returned. And he has brought a bride. Welcome, child, to our deeply unassuming, structurally questionable suburban home."

I exhaled a breath so massive I almost passed out. We had done it. We had bypassed the legendary five-husband matrimonial catastrophe. Draupadi was exclusively Arjuna's wife, the narrative was stable, and Lord Krishna’s warning had been perfectly executed. 

Draupadi, however, wasn't looking at the family. She was critically evaluating the property. She ran a perfectly manicured hand along the mud-brick wall, leaving a trail of disapproval. 

"Thank you, Mother," Draupadi said politely, before immediately pivoting to business. "However, the structural integrity of this vestibule is abhorrent. And the lighting? Terrible. Are we currently surviving on a single oil lamp per quadrant? This is fundamentally inefficient for optimal productivity."

"We are currently pursuing an ascetic lifestyle, Princess," Yudhishthira offered defensively, recovering from my elbow strike. "We are deeply connected to the minimalist mud."

"Minimalism is an excuse for poor supply chain management," Draupadi countered instantly, crossing her arms. "I understand from my husband that we are currently operating in full incognito mode to avoid geopolitical assassination. That is fine. But hiding does not mandate squalor. We are going to completely optimize this household. Starting with the removal of whatever terrible, deeply offensive fragrance is coming from Bhima's room."

"Oye! Oh mere laddoos di khushboo hai!" Bhima yelled defensively from the kitchen. *(Hey! That is the smell of my laddoos!)* 

"It smells like weaponized cholesterol!" Draupadi yelled back. She turned to me. "Rahul. You are the designated operational director for this 'startup' idea you pitched to my husband. Give me the current status report."

"Well," I rubbed the back of my neck. "The primary issue is Dhrishtadyumna."

"My brother?" Draupadi raised an eyebrow. 

"Yes. He secretly followed our chariot from Panchaal. He is currently standing behind a cactus about three hundred yards outside. He knows the 'foreigners' from Americanapur live here. But he doesn't know who is actually under the blonde hair dye."

Draupadi nodded thoughtfully. "Classic Dhrishtadyumna. He possesses the situational awareness of a highly trained general, but the emotional patience of a toddler. He will undoubtedly try to infiltrate the perimeter. What is the counter-measure?"

"The counter-measure," I announced to the entire room, "is absolute, relentless commitment to the disguise. We cannot afford to blow our cover. Takshaka already blackmails us inside Indraprastha, but outside, we must remain the obnoxious Hippies of Americanapur."

A collective groan echoed through the mud-brick house. 

"Rahul, please," Nakula begged, dramatically collapsing onto a wooden chair. "My scalp is physically rejecting the crushed-berry blonde dye. It lacks natural oils! My hair feels like cheap, imported straw! Can we not just reveal ourselves to the spy outside? He is family now!"

"No!" I snapped. "If Dhrishtadyumna knows you are the Pandavas, he will immediately run back and tell King Drupad. Drupad will tell the world, and Suyodhana will immediately launch a tactical strike on Sector 15 to get us back home and reclaim his honour!"

"But the cargo shorts chafe!" Arjuna complained, looking down at his exposed knees. "The linen possesses optimal breathability, I will admit, but it completely compromises my authoritative aesthetic. How can I command respect when I look like a wayward merchant from a tropical island?"

"You will command respect by shutting up and wearing the shorts, Arjuna," Draupadi ordered sternly. "Rahul is correct. The corporate strategy dictates complete stealth until we establish a dominant market share. Embrace the shorts."

Arjuna instantly fell silent. The CEO had spoken. 

And so began the most ridiculous logistical routine of our entire ancient existence.

Every morning at 5:00 AM, the greatest warriors of Bharata would wake up in Sector 15 Nayada, aggressively re-apply their terrible yellow turmeric makeup, secure their scratchy blonde wigs, and don their tie-dye t-shirts and cargo shorts.

We would then commute via chariot into the sprawling metropolis of Indraprastha, looking exactly like the most confusing tourist delegation in history.

But we couldn't walk into Takshaka's royal palace looking like that. Takshaka demanded his staff look aggressively professional. 

So, I had secured a membership at a local Bronze Age gymnasium—essentially a large, open-air dirt pit where intensely muscular Naga men lifted massive circular stones and aggressively grunted at each other. The gym was conveniently located just three blocks from the royal palace.

Every morning, our chariot would pull into the alleyway behind the gym. 

"Quickly, quickly!" Yudhishthira would hiss, acting terribly anxious. "The HR compliance check is in twenty minutes!"

The Pandavas would pile into a small, heavily shadowed changing room behind the stone-lifting racks. It was a chaotic scramble of elbows, groans, and aggressively thick Punjabi complaints. 

"Arjuna, mera pair chhod de!" Bhima would yell from inside the tiny wooden stall. "Eh meri dhoti hai! Tera tie-dye shirt uthe digg gaya hai!" *(Arjuna, leave my foot! This is my dhoti! Your tie-dye shirt has fallen there!)*

"Your spatial awareness is catastrophic, Bhima!" Arjuna would snap back. "Nakula, stop applying the rosewater and help me scrub this terrible blonde dye out of my eyebrows!"

"I cannot rush perfection!" Nakula would whine. "My natural highlights are being suppressed by this rural mud-water!"

Within five minutes, the chaotic tourists of Americanapur would vanish, heavily scrubbed and changed, replaced immediately by the highly efficient, formally dressed administrative staff of King Takshaka. They would casually walk out the front of the gym, nodding professionally to the grunting stone-lifters, and march calmly toward the palace to begin their day of corporate slavery. 

In the evening, the routine would reverse. They would leave the palace, slip into the gym, smear themselves in terrible makeup, put the cargo shorts back on, and ride back to Nayada. 

It was an exhausting, deeply undignified double life. 

But our intense caution was completely justified. Because General Dhrishtadyumna was far more committed to espionage than we had anticipated. 

A week after Draupadi’s arrival in Nayada, Dhrishtadyumna formally committed to the stalker lifestyle. He didn't just report back to Panchaal. He quietly rented a small, deeply unimpressive mud-hut exactly two streets down from our compound in Sector 15. 

We watched through the slats of our wooden windows as the Crown Prince of Panchaal, a man used to immense luxury and a massive military entourage, tried to discreetly carry groceries into his new suburban rental property. 

"Look at him," Sahadeva muttered, peeking through the window. "His operational security is terrible. He is wearing a cheap burlap sack over his royal silk trousers. I foresee him accidentally exposing his true identity within seventy-two hours."

Sahadeva was wrong. It didn't take seventy-two hours. It took exactly four days for Dhrishtadyumna to realize that simply watching our house wasn't yielding any results. He needed to infiltrate our daily commute. He needed to get a job near us. 

And specifically, he needed to get a job in the one place where he knew the "foreigners" frequently visited: Gymnasium near King Takshaka’s royal kitchens, where he knew the massive tattooed guy in cargo shorts apparently had some massive culinary connection. 

This led to the most profoundly stupid, deeply chaotic job interview in ancient history.


I was sitting in my windowless basement office, aggressively trying to construct a quarterly revenue chart on a slab of slate, when the heavy door banged open. 

Bhima stood there, wearing his pristine, bright white Chief Culinary Officer uniform, which he wore with intense pride. But today, he wasn't carrying a spatula. He was carrying a massive grin. 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima laughed, shutting the door tightly behind him. "Tera plan flop ho gaya! Sadda picha karne wala spy kitchen vich pohoch gaya!" *(Hey Rahul! Your plan flopped! The spy following us has reached the kitchen!)*

"What?" I dropped my chalk. "Dhrishtadyumna is in the royal kitchen?"

"Aaho!" Bhima chuckled darkly, his massive frame shaking. "Ohne apne aap nu cook dsya hai! Kehnda hai mera naam 'Deepak' hai, aur main bada hi swadisht khana bananda haan! Uski mooch nakli hai, bro! Mainu odi aawaz pehchan leee haigi hai!" *(Yes! He introduced himself as a cook! Says his name is 'Deepak' and he makes very delicious food! His mustache is fake, bro! I recognized his voice immediately!)*

"You recognized him?" I panicked. "Bhima, you have to fire him! If he realizes you're the head chef of Takshaka's palace, our entire cover is blown!"

"Fire him?!" Bhima looked offended. "Main kyu fire karan?! Eh mera rishtedaar hai! Meri bhabhi da bhraa! Aur sab ton vadi gal, eh banda meri kitchen vich bartan dho reha hai! Tu soch, Panchaal da Crown Prince mere tandoor de pan dho reha hai! Main isnu puri zindagi ithe hi rakhunga!" *(Why should I fire him?! He is my relative! My sister-in-law's brother! And the biggest thing, this guy is washing dishes in my kitchen! Just imagine, the Crown Prince of Panchaal is washing my tandoor pans! I will keep him here my whole life!)*

I stared at Bhima. The sheer petty, vindictive joy radiating from him was terrifying. 

"Bhima," I asked slowly. "What exactly have you been making 'Deepak' do?"

"Oh, kal main uhnu pachas kilo pyaaz katan laye ditte si," Bhima sneered happily. *(Oh, yesterday I made him cut fifty kilos of onions.)* "Ajj main uhnu kehna hai ki poore rasoi di zameen floor saaf kare, apne hathan naal! Mainu bada maza aa reha hai!" *(Today I will tell him to clean the entire kitchen floor with his bare hands! I am having so much fun!)*

Against my better judgment, I snuck out of my office and followed Bhima down to the vast, steaming royal kitchens of Indraprastha. 

I peeked through the small wooden service window. 

There, standing over a massive, incredibly greasy bronze cauldron, aggressively scrubbing it with a piece of dried coconut husk, was General Dhrishtadyumna. He was wearing an absolutely horrific fake mustache that was slightly peeling off his upper lip due to the intense steam, and a cheap, stained apron. 

Bhima confidently strode into the kitchen, immediately assuming the persona of a terrifying head chef. 

"Oye Deepak!" Bhima roared perfectly in his intimidating Punjabi accent. "Eh ki haal banaya hai kitchen da?! Tainu bartan dhone vi nahi aunde?! Tere hath kinne nazuk ne, jive kisi raajkumar de hon! Panchaal de rajkumar warge hath laggde ne tere!" *(Hey Deepak! What is this state of the kitchen?! Don't you even know how to wash dishes?! Your hands are so delicate, like some prince's! They look like the hands of the Prince of Panchaal!)*

Dhrishtadyumna froze. The coconut husk slipped from his hand, splashing greasy water directly onto his apron. He stared at Bhima, his eyes wide with sudden panic. 

"I... I am just a simple cook, Chief!" Dhrishtadyumna stammered urgently, hastily pressing his fake mustache back onto his face. "I am from a small village! I moisturize heavily!"

"Moisturize!" Bhima bellowed, walking over and aggressively slapping Dhrishtadyumna on the back so hard the Prince practically folded in half. "Tu mazaakiya banda hai, Deepak! Jaise koi bada royal general bheja hove meri kitchen saf karan layi! Hahahaha! Chal, hun jake thoda lasan chil, maharaj-ji!" *(You are a funny guy, Deepak! As if some big royal general has been sent to clean my kitchen! Hahahaha! Go on, now go peel some garlic, Your Highness!)*

Dhrishtadyumna let out a shaky breath of relief, entirely unable to realize that he was being aggressively mocked. He scurried over to a massive pile of unpeeled garlic, his princely dignity completely shattered. 

But Dhrishtadyumna hadn't taken the job just to wash dishes. He had an agenda. He was trying to track the strange foreigners. 

Later that afternoon, when the kitchen slowed down, Dhrishtadyumna nervously approached Bhima, who was casually resting on a sack of flour. 

"Chief," Dhrishtadyumna asked, lowering his voice conspiratorially. "I... I have noticed that you sometimes associate with a group of very strange men. Men with... blonde hair and odd tattoos. From... Americanapur?"

Bhima raised exactly one heavily muscled eyebrow. "Aaho. Oh mere yaar ne. Pind wale ne. Tainu ki problem hai Americanapur naal, Deepak?" *(Yes. They are my friends. From my village. What problem do you have with Americanapur, Deepak?)*

"No problem!" Dhrishtadyumna backpedaled instantly. "Actually, I admire them! I... I am looking to pursue higher education! Yes! I want to get foreign admission into a top agricultural university in Americanapur!"

I snorted loudly from my hiding spot outside the service window. 

"Foreign admission?" Bhima practically vibrated with suppressed laughter. "Tainu Americanapur jaana hai?" *(You want to go to Americanapur?)*

"Desperately, Chief," Dhrishtadyumna nodded extremely seriously, his fake mustache hanging by a thread. "I was hoping you could arrange a meeting with them. I need guidance on their cultural exchange protocols."

"Achha," Bhima rubbed his chin thoughtfully, acting as if he was contemplating a highly complex academic request. "Vekh, Americanapur da visa process bada hi tough hunda hai. Oh log sirf unna nu lende ne jo physical labor vich mahir hon. Tainu apna 'GPA' badhana pauga." *(Alright. Look, Americanapur's visa process is very tough. They only take those who are experts in physical labor. You will have to increase your 'GPA'.)*

"GPA?" 

"Garlic Peeling Average!" Bhima roared, tossing an entirely new, unpeeled sack of garlic directly at Dhrishtadyumna's chest. "Ja jake pura sack chil ke aa, phir main sochunga tere visa baare!" *(Go peel the whole sack, then I will think about your visa!)*

For exactly nine days, this absolute farce continued. Dhrishtadyumna, the fierce commander of the mighty Panchaal armies, spent his entire existence aggressively peeling garlic, scrubbing pots, and asking Bhima for "LORs" (Letters of Recommendation) to Americanapur. 

Bhima milked it for every possible drop of humor, occasionally calling Dhrishtadyumna "Your Highness" entirely by accident, then quickly claiming it was an ironic kitchen nickname. 

But Dhrishtadyumna’s princely patience was inherently limited. The garlic was breaking him. He knew the foreigners commuted every day. He knew they originated from the Sector 15 compound. He just needed to catch them in the act of transition. 

And unfortunately, his espionage skills were slightly better than his dishwashing skills. 

It happened on a overly humid Tuesday evening. 

We had finished our shift at Takshaka’s palace. The Pandavas, exhausted from their corporate slavery, had marched straight into the dingy, mud-walled changing room located behind the local gym. 

I stood outside the wooden door, keeping watch while constantly checking the ancient slate watch I had carved for myself. 

Inside the changing room, the standard chaotic scramble of swapping royal administrative tunics to cargo shorts and applying fake yellow turmeric makeup was in full swing. 

"Nakula, watch where you throw your pumice stone!" Yudhishthira's muffled voice complained aggressively. "You nearly cracked my compliance spectacles!"

"My aesthetic tools are a hazard zone, Yudhishthira!" Nakula yelled back. "Respect the perimeter!"

"Oye mainu mera wig nahi labh reha!" Bhima shouted loudly. "Kise ne mera blonde wala wig gaya?!" *(Hey, I can't find my wig! Did someone take my blonde wig?!)*

Suddenly, a shadow fell over the alleyway. 

I spun around. 

Standing exactly five feet from me, holding a massive wooden ladle like a tactical weapon, was Dhrishtadyumna. He wasn't wearing his fake mustache anymore. He looked infuriated, covered in a light dusting of flour, and completely laser-focused. 

"Ah," I squeaked, aggressively leaning against the wooden changing room door. "Deepak! The cook! Hey man! How's the... how's the soup?"

Dhrishtadyumna didn't reply. He stared at me, his eyes narrowing to incredibly dangerous slits. 

"Move," Dhrishtadyumna ordered, his voice cold and terrifyingly princely. 

"I can't!" I babbled desperately. "The guys are in there! It's an Americanapur cultural ritual! Naked... sacred... meditation!"

Dhrishtadyumna didn't even hesitate. He stepped forward, grabbed me by my t-shirt collar, and effortlessly tossed me into a nearby pile of hay. He raised a foot and violently kicked the wooden door of the changing room open. 

The door crashed back against the stone wall. 

Inside the tiny room, the five greatest heroes of the epoch froze like deer caught in headlights. 

Yudhishthira was standing there in his formal chief advisor robes, desperately trying to pull a tie-dye t-shirt over his head. Nakula was half-naked, aggressively applying bright yellow turmeric paste to his flawless cheekbones. Sahadeva was calmly tying his cargo shorts. 

And Bhima... Bhima was standing perfectly still, holding his massive blonde wig in one hand and exactly half of an incredibly rich, royal paneer tikka in the other. 

Silence descended over the alleyway. It was absolute, crushing, apocalyptic silence. 

Dhrishtadyumna stared at the men in the room. He stared at Yudhishthira's recognizable face. He stared at Arjuna’s tragic, heroic jawline, currently half-covered in terrible blonde dye. 

The disguise was entirely blown. The corporate sabotage was exposed. We were going to be executed. 

But then, an expression I completely did not expect washed over Dhrishtadyumna’s flour-covered face. 

It wasn't fury. It wasn't betrayal. 

It was absolute, overwhelming, delirious joy.


"Pandavas!" Dhrishtadyumna practically screamed, dropping the wooden ladle. He threw his arms up in the air toward the sky. "The Pandavas! You're alive! You're all alive!"

"Shh!" Yudhishthira panicked, practically diving forward and clamping a hand over Dhrishtadyumna’s mouth. "Lower your decibels immediately! Our non-disclosure agreements are currently active!"

"Oye aaram naal raula na pa!" Bhima hissed, aggressively shoving his blonde wig out the small window. *(Hey patiently don't make noise!)*

Dhrishtadyumna gently grabbed Yudhishthira's hand and pulled it away from his mouth. There were literal tears of joy shining in the general's eyes. 

"I knew it," Dhrishtadyumna whispered fiercely, looking at Arjuna with pure awe. "The moment I saw you lift Shiva's bow, I knew only a Prince of Hastinapur could possess that kind of structural kinetic potential! Americanapur wasn't a real place! It was an elaborate counter-espionage cover identity!"

"Exactly," I said quickly, standing up from the hay. "Very high-level espionage. Now stop yelling."

"This is the greatest day in the history of Panchaal!" Dhrishtadyumna continued, aggressively gripping Arjuna's shoulders. "My sister isn't married to some peasant tourist! She is married to the greatest warrior on the continent! Our political alliances are permanently secured! Oh, wait until my father hears this! King Drupad will throw a massive ticker-tape parade!"

"No!" the Pandavas shouted in absolute unison. 

Dhrishtadyumna blinked, confused. "What? Why not? You survived Varnavata! You must return to the world! You must reclaim your rightful place as the vanguard of the Kuru dynasty!"

"General Dhrishtadyumna," Arjuna said smoothly, stepping forward and wiping the remaining yellow dye off his jaw. "While we appreciate the enthusiastic political support, we simply cannot return to Hastinapur right now. The market conditions are entirely unfavorable."

"Market conditions?" Dhrishtadyumna repeated, bewildered. 

"Suyodhana currently holds absolute PR dominance," Yudhishthira explained nervously, adjusting his glasses. "If we suddenly reappear without a massive, fully funded army, his public relations team will easily spin our survival into an aggressive act of treason. We need time. We need structural infrastructure."

"And most importantly," Bhima added, crossing his massive arms proudly, "saddi bhabhi te Raool mil ke ek ghaint business plan bana rahe ne!" *(And most importantly, our sister-in-law and Rahul are making an awesome business plan together!)*

"A business plan?" Dhrishtadyumna looked at me blankly. "What is he talking about? You are Kshatriya princes! You conquer kingdoms! You don't make business plans!"

"Listen, Deepak," I said gently, stepping into the cramped room. "The modern era requires vertical integration. We have currently identified a massive gap in the Indraprastha market regarding high-quality, caffeinated operational efficiency. Draupadi is currently crunching the operational numbers. We are launching a startup. Right here. Under Takshaka's nose."

Dhrishtadyumna looked rapidly between the faces of the greatest warriors in Bharata. They were entirely serious. They actively preferred plotting a beverage monopoly over immediately claiming a kingdom. 

"My sister," Dhrishtadyumna sighed heavily, rubbing his forehead. "She always wanted to act as a CEO. She complained about it constantly in Panchaal. I assume she is leading this... startup?"

"Draupadi has currently optimized our daily workflows by 14%," Yudhishthira nodded proudly. "Her supply chain vision is incredibly clear."

Dhrishtadyumna stood in silence for a long moment. He looked at the ridiculous cargo shorts, the half-applied turmeric makeup, and the complete, unyielding loyalty these men had for whatever chaotic plan I had somehow dragged them into. 

"Fine," Dhrishtadyumna finally said, looking at me with a distinctly calculating expression. "I understand the concept of a tactical delay. But you cannot launch a successful economic infiltration without initial liquidity. You need capital."

He reached beneath his flour-dusted apron and pulled out a small, incredibly heavy velvet pouch. He tossed it to Arjuna, who caught it effortlessly. 

The pouch clinked with the distinct, heavy sound of pure Panchaal gold coins. 

"Consider this my Seed Capital," Dhrishtadyumna declared with absolute princely authority. "I am investing in this startup. Panchaal expects a massive return on investment when you finally go public and conquer Hastinapur. Do not fail my sister."

"We will structure your equity according to standard Series A evaluations," Yudhishthira promised seriously, already mentally drafting the financial paperwork. 

"Now," Dhrishtadyumna sighed, turning to the door. "If you will excuse me. My garlic-peeling average is apparently quite low, and I must return to Panchaal before I develop permanent calluses."

He paused at the door, looking back at Bhima. 

"And Chief?" Dhrishtadyumna smiled slightly. "The paneer quality in your kitchen is highly subpar. I suggest you upgrade your vendors."

Before Bhima could aggressively defend his cheese supply chain, Dhrishtadyumna vanished into the alleyway, moving with the silent grace of a deeply relieved, highly satisfied spy.

The immediate threat of discovery was gone. We had successfully managed to retain our incognito status, and we had just secured our initial venture funding from a completely unhinged royal angel investor.

It was time to build an empire.

The startup phase of our operations was an absolute logistical nightmare, entirely driven by the terrifyingly competent administrative aggression of Draupadi. 

For the next three weeks, our dusty mud-brick home in Nayada became the corporate headquarters for the greatest disruption the ancient world had ever seen.

"We are not launching a military offensive," Draupadi had declared on her first official day as acting CEO. She stood in front of a massive slate board in our living room, tapping it aggressively with a piece of chalk. "Military offensives require high casualty rates and expensive supply lines. We are going to conquer the Delhi NCR region through aggressive caffeine addiction."

I suggested Draupadi to use the word Delhi NCR region rather than calling kingdoms of Navada, Indraprastha and Gurugramaha. Surprisingly no one even asked why?

"Caffeine?" Arjuna asked curiously, sitting on the floor cross-legged. "Is that a new structural kinetic weapon?"

"It is an agricultural extract," I explained from the corner, currently acting as her Chief Technology Officer. "It aggressively stimulates the central nervous system. It makes people work faster, talk louder, and become deeply reliant on the supplier."

"Fascinating," Sahadeva murmured, taking notes on a palm leaf. "A chemical dependency model. Very scalable."

"Exactly," Draupadi nodded sharply. "We will establish a premium experiential beverage location in the heart of Indraprastha. We will not sell 'tea' or 'herbal infusions'. We will sell highly customized, aggressively overpriced caffeinated concoctions wrapped in an aura of exclusive elitism."

"But bhabhi," Bhima raised his hand slowly. "Ithe log sirf doodh lassi pinde ne. Eh caffeine wali cheez lokaan nu kiwe pasand aayegi?" *(But sister-in-law, people here only drink milk lassi. How will people like this caffeine thing?)*

"By convincing them that if they don't drink it, they aren't functioning at optimal capacity," Draupadi replied with purely terrifying corporate logic. "We create the demand by marketing the solution. But Dhrishtadyumna's gold will only cover the initial operational lease. We need a secondary investor. A local partner who understands the Indraprastha real estate market and possesses serious expansion capabilities."

"I know just the man," Yudhishthira spoke up softly from his desk. "During my compliance audits under Takshaka's administration, I noticed a very wealthy, aggressively ambitious local merchant operating on the outskirts of the central market. He practically controls the agricultural supply lines into the city."

"What is his name?" Draupadi asked, chalk at the ready. 

"Virata," Yudhishthira replied. "He is cunning, excessively rich, and harbors a deep, secret desire to eventually establish his own kingdom. He wants royal adjacency."

"Perfect," Draupadi smiled. It was the same terrifyingly brilliant, capitalistic smile that Takshaka used. "Rahul, set up a pitch meeting. We are going to secure Virata as our primary local distributor."

Securing Virata was shockingly easy, mostly because when a highly aggressive, brilliantly articulate CEO like Draupadi walks into a room flanked by five massive, intensely intimidating heavily armed "consultants", merchants tend to listen.

"I don't understand the product," Virata had admitted nervously during our pitch meeting in his opulent warehouse, staring at the small, dark cup of the prototype coffee Bhima had brewed. "It looks like heavily polluted river water."

"It is liquid productivity, Virata," Draupadi countered smoothly, leaning across his massive desk. "It targets the tired mid-level administrators of Takshaka’s regime. We provide the product, the branding, and the operational talent. You provide the real estate in the central tech-… I mean, the central merchant park. In return, when we inevitably expand and monopolize the market, your political influence scales directly with our profit margins."

Virata took a cautious sip of the prototype. The pure, aggressive blast of caffeine hit his ancient, unadjusted nervous system like a lightning bolt. His eyes went wide. His pupils dilated. 

"I feel..." Virata gasped, his hands trembling slightly in pure kinetic energy, "I feel like I could personally inventory my entire warehouse in twenty minutes! By the gods, what is this magic? What are you calling your business as?"

"We call it," Draupadi announced proudly, "The Rajsooyi Karyafe."

"Or Royal Cafe, to the commoners," I translated helpfully. 

Virata aggressively slammed his hand on the table. "I provide the storefront immediately! We launch on the next auspicious moon!"

Before we could even approach Virata, we had to ensure our product was completely market-ready. 

Dhrishtadyumna’s seed capital allowed us to enter the highly intense Research & Development phase of the startup. Draupadi, possessing the relentless aggression of a top-tier corporate visionary, immediately instituted mandatory daily tasting sessions in our mud-brick compound. 

"The modern consumer does not want simplicity," Draupadi lectured us one Tuesday morning, pacing in front of a heavily exhausted Bhima who was covered in roasted bean dust. "They want complexity. They want to feel intellectually superior because their beverage takes seven minutes to prepare. Bhima, what is the status of Prototype 4?"

"Bhabhi, main is vich dalchini, thodi jaiphal, aur bakri da doodh paya hai!" Bhima reported, aggressively wiping sweat from his forehead. *(Sister-in-law, I have added cinnamon, a little nutmeg, and goat milk in this!)* "Par mainu lagda hai caffeine da level thoda jyada ho gaya hai!" *(But I think the caffeine level has become a little too high!)*

"Show me," Draupadi demanded. 

Bhima carefully picked up a small clay cup filled with a dark, terrifyingly thick liquid and handed it to Nakula, who had been designated our Chief Tasting Officer primarily because he complained the loudest about anything subpar. 

Nakula took a tiny sip. He closed his eyes. 

For exactly three seconds, nothing happened. Then, Nakula’s eyes snapped violently open. They were entirely bloodshot. His hands began to visibly vibrate. 

"I can hear the future!" Nakula gasped, gripping the edges of the wooden table with terrifying strength. "I can visually track the individual dust particles in the air! The aesthetic symmetry of the universe is utterly flawed! I must reorganize the entire kitchen pantry immediately!"

Nakula sprinted out of the room so fast he physically blurred, leaving behind a trail of intense herbal scent. 

"Excellent," Draupadi nodded with profound corporate satisfaction, aggressively writing on her slate board. "Prototype 4 achieves absolute central nervous system domination. Now we must solve the supply chain logistics. Arjuna!"

Arjuna snapped to attention, dropping the bow he was casually polishing. "CEO Draupadi. Ready for deployment."

"What is our bean procurement interval?" Draupadi asked sharply. "We cannot rely on sporadic merchant caravans. We need a dedicated agricultural pipeline from the southern slopes."

"I have already calculated the optimal trajectory," Arjuna replied, pulling out a deeply complicated map he had drawn in the dirt. "If we utilize three rotating squads of fast-moving chariots, bypassing the central taxation routes by hugging the eastern riverbanks, we can reduce our delivery times by 40%. It requires highly advanced chariot drifting techniques."

"Perfect," Draupadi smiled her terrifyingly competent smile. "Authorize the route. Sahadeva, what is the psychic forecast for market reception?"

Sahadeva looked up from his dark meditation corner. He had completely abandoned predicting geopolitical wars and was now entirely focused on predicting consumer purchasing trends. 

"The market eagerly desires validation," Sahadeva murmured, his eyes glowing faintly. "The Naga administrators are deeply insecure about their social standing. If we price the beverage exponentially higher than standard water or milk, they will assume it infers high status. They will purchase it simply to be seen holding the clay cup."

"Pricing elasticity!" Draupadi clapped her hands together in genuine joy. "This is a flawless business model. We are creating an artificial scarcity loop!"

I watched them from the corner of the room, completely awestruck. 

The greatest heroes of ancient mythology, the warriors destined to fight the most devastating, epic war in human history, were currently using their divine, superhuman abilities purely to optimize a coffee supply chain for a suburban startup.

They were an unstoppable corporate force. The world simply wasn't ready for them. 

The launch of the first 'Rajsooyi Karyafe' was an absolute, unmitigated economic phenomenon that completely shattered the local ecosystem of Indraprastha. 

We didn't just open a shop; we introduced the Bronze Age to the concept of highly specialized, aggressively marketed consumer culture.

The physical location, graciously provided by Virata, was a large, open-air pavilion right near the central administrative hub. Nakula, utilizing his aggressive aesthetic dominance, had completely redesigned the interior. It didn't look like a tavern. It featured low, highly polished seating, ambient oil-lamp lighting, and soft, instrumental veena music playing in the background. It was designed to look incredibly expensive while serving what was essentially just boiled bean water.

Bhima naturally assumed the role of Head Barista, though he insisted on wearing his heavy battle-apron. Arjuna aggressively managed the supply logistics, ensuring our delivery times were structurally flawless. Yudhishthira handled the complex compliance regulations and taxation filings, baffling the local authorities with his airtight bureaucratic loops. 

And Draupadi and I managed the overarching brand strategy. 

Within the first week, we introduced the concept of the "Morning Rush." 

The mid-level administrators of Takshaka’s government, constantly exhausted by the King's relentless PR demands, discovered our highly caffeinated brews. 

"I don't know what this 'Caramelized Jaggery Venti Infusion' is," a heavily exhausted Naga seneschal had muttered on opening day, clutching his clay cup defensively. "But after I drank it, I successfully reorganized the entire eastern taxation grid in forty-five minutes. My hands are actively shaking, but I have never felt more alive. I need three more for my staff."

By week three, the line for Rajsooyi Karyafe aggressively stretched around the entire block. We introduced 'Customer Experience Enhancements' like inexplicably spelling everyone's name entirely wrong on their clay cups, which generated massive amounts of free, chaotic word-of-mouth marketing in the local taverns. 

*“Why did they write ‘Praful’ when my name is blatantly ‘Purohit’?!”* customers would complain loudly, immediately returning the next day to purchase another vastly overpriced cup just to see what new phonetic disaster Bhima had invented. 

By the end of the second month, the revenue was so overwhelmingly massive that our daily, soul-crushing commute from Nayada officially felt entirely redundant. 

We would run this in parallel to our day jobs at Takshaka's palace by taking turns, aggressively using our leaves, and operating practically at LWPs (Leave without pays).

"The structural integrity of this endeavor is fully verified," Arjuna declared one evening, aggressively tracking our massive daily profits on a slate board back in Sector 15. "I have mathematically proven that the localized cafe margins are outperforming our salaries at Takshaka's administration by exactly 400%."

"It is time," Draupadi nodded sharply from her CEO desk in the corner. "We are executing the termination protocols. Tomorrow, you will all officially resign from His Majesty's employment."

Quitting the most powerful ruler in Bharata was incredibly satisfying. 

We marched into Takshaka’s throne room the next morning, still wearing our terrible hippie disguises as the 'tourists from Americanapur'. 

"Resign?" Takshaka demanded, his dark eyes narrowing violently as Yudhishthira handed him a flawlessly formatted, eighty-page termination compliance report. "You cannot resign! You are under a mandatory exclusivity contract! If you push this, I will expose you! I will send my painters to every tavern in Bharata to reveal that the 'dead' Pandavas are hiding in Indraprastha! Suyodhana's assassins will be here by dawn!"

"Actually," Yudhishthira adjusted his fake blonde wig smoothly, completely unbothered by the blackmail. "Clause 14, subsection B of my own administrative rewrite clearly states that the contractors maintain at-will operational flexibility granting immediate departure upon submission of 24-hour notice. It is legally binding, Your Majesty. As for exposing our true identities... we no longer care."

Takshaka stared at Yudhishthira, completely baffled. "You don't care about a geopolitical assassination attempt?"

"They have found their true purpose," Draupadi stepped forward, adjusting her CEO linen robes. "They aren't just exiled princes anymore. They are entrepreneurs now. And as Rahul continually reminded me—for a startup to truly disrupt the ecosystem, one has to make sacrifices and eventually go public. You cannot stop our IPO, Takshaka."

Takshaka stared at them with a mixture of absolute fury and genuine, terrifying professional respect. 

"Who is managing my kitchen?!" Takshaka yelled, slamming his fist on the armrest, realizing his blackmail was utterly useless against pure startup ambition. "My PR painters are starving! The aesthetic department is in ruins!"

"We highly recommend you pursue localized talent acquisition," I offered cheerfully. "Peace out, Your Majesty! Osom!"

We strolled out of the palace as free men, leaving Takshaka reeling from the sudden administrative vacuum.

For the next two years, we didn't train for war. We didn't build armies. We built an absolute, unyielding corporate monopoly. 

The Rajsooyi Karyafe expanded rapidly. We crushed the local competition. We opened franchises in the northern trade sector, the southern agricultural hubs, and even established three localized drive-throughs for passing merchant caravans. We entirely dominated the Delhi NCR region of 3200 BCE. 

We were the Starbucks of the Bronze Age. Our revenue generated a literal mountain of gold safely stored in Virata's heavily guarded vaults. 

The Pandavas, no longer wearing their terrible disguises, operated freely in the background of Indraprastha. We were incredibly wealthy, immensely successful, and aggressively comfortable. 

Even King Takshaka eventually realized he couldn't beat the caffeinated juggernaut. 

Exactly twenty-four months after our resignation, the terrifying Naga overlord himself arrived at our flagship store in the central plaza. He was wearing his usual emerald silks, followed by his exhausted-looking PR painters, but his arrogant stride lacked its usual menace. 

He found Draupadi and me going over the quarterly projections in a private back booth. 

"Princess Draupadi," Takshaka said smoothly, heavily acknowledging her true identity, sliding into the booth perfectly opposite her. "The most ruthless CEO to ever grace my trading block. And Rahul... the architect."

"Ah, King Takshaka," Draupadi replied coolly, not looking up from her slate. "Are you here to purchase a Double Venti Goat-Milk Macchiato?"

"I am here to purchase equity," Takshaka smiled his cold, capitalistic smile. "Frankly, my administrative output has dropped 30% since your staff left. Which is irritating. But the true tragedy is that I am currently missing out on the greatest market capitalization in history. I want to become a Series A investor in the Karyafe. I will offer you unprecedented tax breaks across the entire Indraprastha region for a meager 15% stake."

Draupadi finally looked up from her slate perfectly meeting the King's terrifying gaze without flinching. 

"Your Majesty," Draupadi smiled sharply. "While we appreciate the infrastructural support... we are no longer accepting regional investors."

Takshaka frowned, clearly unused to rejection. "Why not? You are reaching market saturation in Indraprastha. Where else could you possibly expand locally without my support?"

"We are not expanding locally, Your Majesty," I interjected smoothly, leaning back in my chair. "We are preparing for a national rollout."

Takshaka's eyes widened slightly. He looked around the cafe, and then leaned in, changing his tone from corporate shark to deeply offended neighbor.

"Speaking of regional expansion," Takshaka crossed his arms. "I notice you have all completely vacated that dusty mud-hut in Nayada. My intelligence network informs me that you have purchased a massive, incredibly premium plot of land on Chitrapur Road on the outskirts of Indraprastha. You have built a palace that rivals my own. And yet, I have not received an invitation."

"It's a farmhouse, Your Majesty, not a palace," Draupadi replied smoothly, perfectly defusing the tension. "And we are still planning the housewarming party. You will be the first on the guest list."

Takshaka huffed, slightly placated, and marched out of the cafe without buying a coffee. 

Draupadi was right. We weren't living in Nayada anymore. The massive success of the Rajsooyi Karyafe had allowed us to officially move into Indraprastha. We had purchased an absolutely monumental piece of real estate on the serene, deeply forested outskirts of the city on Chitrapur Road. 

However, Bhima, completely unable to wrap his intense Punjabi accent around the ancient Sanskrit pronunciation, aggressively refused to call it by its proper name. 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima had yelled just that morning, carrying a massive stone pillar over his shoulder for the garden patio. "Main Chattarpur vich apni tandoor di bhatti laga riha haan!" *(Hey Rahul! I am setting up my tandoor oven in Chattarpur!)*

I had laughed so hard I nearly dropped my slate. *Chattarpur*. The ancient, mythological origin of modern-day Chhatarpur in Delhi NCR. I was actively watching a Punjabi giant invent the linguistic geography of my hometown in real-time. History, it seemed, was deeply impacted by heavily mispronounced accents. 

But the real comedy of the Chattarpur Farmhouse was the interior design. 

Sahadeva, utilizing our massive corporate reserves, had insisted on managing the home's aesthetics to establish absolute dominance over our wealthy neighbors. He wanted the house to exude a deeply sophisticated, "upper-class" vibe. 

To achieve this, he had hired an extremely expensive, highly dramatic interior designer whom he proudly introduced to the family as "Luigi".

"He is an Italian architect," Sahadeva had whispered to Nakula, proudly gesturing to a short, highly energetic man who was currently measuring a window with a piece of string. "From the distant, incredibly sophisticated Western lands. His vision for our marble flooring is entirely avant-garde."

The Pandavas, completely unfamiliar with "Italian" culture, treated Luigi with a mixture of deep confusion and profound respect. 

"Luigi-ji," Yudhishthira asked politely, pointing at a wildly impractical woven-grass chandelier. "Is this structural arrangement structurally compliant with Italian load-bearing principles? It looks highly flammable."

"Mamma mia!" Luigi had thrown his hands up dramatically, his accent entirely baffling. "Eez the art, Signor Yudhishthira! You must respect the *passion*!"

I had watched this interaction with extreme suspicion. Later that evening, I cornered Sahadeva in the eastern courtyard. 

"Sahadeva," I crossed my arms. "'Luigi' is wearing a dhoti under his toga. And when he hit his thumb with a hammer yesterday, he very clearly yelled *'Arre maiyya ri!'* in perfect Mathura dialect. Who is he?"

Sahadeva sighed heavily, dropping his psychic facade. 

"His name is Lallan," Sahadeva admitted quietly, looking around to ensure Nakula couldn't hear. "He is a highly competent carpenter from Mathura. But Rahul, you must understand... if I tell the neighbors we hired a local carpenter, they will think we are just wealthy peasants! If I tell them we imported an *Italian designer*, it immediately establishes immense, unapproachable vanity! I have actively groomed Lallan for three weeks to say 'Mamma mia' just so our property value skyrockets!"

I slowly shook my head. The sheer, terrifyingly modern social-climbing tactics of the ancient era were truly unmatched. 

Once the Italian... I mean, Mathura-designed modifications were complete, the entire board of directors gathered around the massive, imported oak table in the central dining hall of our Chattarpur Farmhouse for a massive strategic meeting. 

Queen Kunti sat quietly at the head of the table, her eyes gleaming with quiet, overwhelming pride as she looked at her sons, completely thriving in their massive palace despite the exile they had been forced into. 

"The two-year financial models are unequivocally clear," Yudhishthira announced, pushing his glasses up his nose. "We have established an unbeatable monopoly in Indraprastha. We have significant capital reserves. Our brand recognition is aggressively viral."

"So, what is the next targeted demographic?" Arjuna asked, casually sharpening an arrow while sipping his heavily caffeinated brew. "Do we move towards the southern kingdoms? Magadha? Mathura?"

"No," Queen Kunti spoke up, her voice surprisingly firm, carrying an underlying edge of long-suppressed royal authority. 

The entire room went completely silent. When Kunti spoke about business strategy, everyone listened. 

Kunti looked slowly around the table, her gaze lingering on each of the Pandavas, on Draupadi, and finally on me. 

"We have hidden in the shadows long enough," Kunti stated softly, but with terrifying intensity. "We have built our strength. We have secured our resources. It is time the world learns exactly what happened to the 'dead' princes of Hastinapur."

Yudhishthira nodded slowly, a dangerous, deeply strategic smile breaking across his face. 

"Mother is correct," Yudhishthira agreed firmly. "The localized expansion phase is officially over. We are taking the franchise national. We need to officially declare that we are alive. And we are going to go back to Hastinapur... to personally invite our Kuru cousins to our farmhouse housewarming party."

Bhima slammed his large fist onto the table, his eyes practically burning with excitement. "Aaho! Main Duryodhana de muh te apna naya ghar show off karunga! Oh jal ke raakh ho jayega!" *(Yes! I will show off my new house right to Duryodhana's face! He will burn to ashes out of jealousy!)*

"Bhima," I interjected quickly, holding up a hand. "Remember the core narrative rewrite. Suyodhana is not an evil villain here. He is a deeply insecure, PR-obsessed tech-bro. He is our contemporary rival, not our mortal enemy. Do not punch him simply because he has terrible corporate synergy."

Bhima pouted slightly but nodded. 

As the meeting wrapped up and the family began celebrating the impending return home, I caught Sahadeva’s eye. We stepped out onto the expansive, Luigi-designed balcony overlooking the ancient Delhi NCR skyline. 

"We are going back," I murmured, watching the oil lamps flicker in the distance. 

"Yes," Sahadeva nodded, his psychic eyes tracing invisible lines in the sky. "But going back means re-entering the primary narrative timeline. We are going to have to actively manage Vyasa's dramatic plotlines. And more importantly... we have to face Grand-sire Bheeshma. He is the ultimate traditionalist. He will not understand our startup models or our corporate disruption."

"We will handle Bheeshma," I promised, though I wasn't entirely sure how one explains IPOs to a celibate mythological patriarch. 

Inside the farmhouse, Queen Kunti was meticulously preparing the guest list on a large, high-quality palm leaf. 

She wrote down the names of the Hastinapur elders. She wrote down King Drupad and Dhrishtadyumna from Panchaal. And then, right at the top of the list, holding the most prestigious position for the housewarming party... Kunti smiled warmly and added one final name. 

*Lord Krishna.*

The exile was officially over. The Pandavas were going home. And they were bringing a highly caffeinated startup empire—and an Italian-designed Chattarpur farmhouse—with them to utterly shock the Kauravas.
