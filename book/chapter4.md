# Chapter 4: The Start-Up Pitch

It took exactly fourteen days for the universe’s most lethal timeline anomaly to become Hastinapur’s favorite middle management executive.

If someone had told me a month ago that my ability to nod thoughtfully, say verbs like "synergize" without laughing, and draw Venn diagrams on whiteboards would save my life in the Bronze Age, I would have asked for whatever uncle Jaggi was smoking in his garage. 

But apparently, corporate jargon is the universal language of unearned authority. When you confidently throw around terms like "cross-platform integration" and "leveraging asynchronous workflows" in 3200 BCE, heavily armed royals assume it’s high-level Vedic sorcery and leave you completely alone.

My official title, bestowed upon me by Sahadeva in an emergency meeting with King Dhritarashtra, was "Chief Product Officer of the Hastinapur Distributed Relay Network." To my utter astonishment, nobody raised an eyebrow. The ministers just nodded grimly, assuming "Product Officer" was some lethal new class of Kshatriya warrior who fought with ledgers instead of maces. 

Of course, integration wasn't entirely seamless. 

The first major hurdle was the HR department. Or, as he was known in this timeline, Prince Yudhishthira. 

On my fourth day, a runner arrived at Sahadeva’s chamber holding a tightly rolled clay tablet. It was a formal, sixteen-page grievance filed by the eldest Pandava regarding my "distracting, morally compromising, and leg-suffocating blue garments."

"He has cited three different ancient scriptures detailing why denim constitutes an insult to the gods of knee mobility," Sahadeva had sighed, tossing the tablet onto a pile. "Go to the royal tailor, Rahul. If you wear those jeans into the throne room again, I fear Yudhishthira might actually combust from sheer righteous indignation."

Which was how I found myself standing in front of a mirror in the royal armory, trying very hard not to trip over my own hems while looking like an extra in an aggressively budgeted mythological soap opera. 

Tying a traditional silk dhoti, I quickly realized, is an architectural crisis. It requires the geometric precision of origami and the blind faith that a carefully tucked fold of fabric will somehow prevent you from flashing the entire Kuru dynasty. I was currently wearing a flowing white silk dhoti, an embroidered golden upper cloth draped over my shoulders, and leather sandals that clacked obnoxiously against the stone floors. 

I looked ridiculous. I felt ridiculous. But more importantly, I finally looked like I belonged here. 

And honestly? Once the immediate threat of universal paradox erasure was safely contained within Sahadeva’s 40-kilometer radius, the ancient royal court felt bizarrely like a high-end corporate offsite. The bitter rivalry between the Pandavas and Kauravas hadn’t fully fermented into genocidal warfare yet. Right now, they were just hyper-competitive frat boys with unlimited access to the royal treasury, massive egos, and a desperate, underlying need for entertainment. 

And they *loved* me. 

Mostly because I introduced them to junk food. 

***

"Oye, Raool!" 

A hand the size of an industrial microwave slammed onto my shoulder, nearly dislocating my collarbone for the second time that month. I coughed, spinning around to find Bhima. He was covered chest-to-toe in refined flour, sweating profusely, and grinning like a maniac. 

"Raool puttar!" Bhima bellowed in thunderous Punjabi, his voice echoing off the vaulted ceilings of the palace corridors. "Tu ithe aa! Tainu main nayi invention dikhawan! Tera oh gol-gol bread wala idea? Kamaal kar ditta tu!" *(Rahul son! Come here! Let me show you a new invention! That round bread idea of yours? You've done wonders!)*

Without waiting for an answer, Bhima grabbed me by my embroidered upper cloth and practically dragged me into the suffocating, intensely aromatic heat of the royal kitchens. 

The royal kitchen was the size of a professional soccer field. Hundreds of chefs and scullions were running around with massive vats of boiling ghee, roasting entire boars on spits, and carrying baskets of fresh produce. But in the center of the kitchen, Bhima had cleared a massive stone table and commandeered the primary clay tandoor oven. 

"Look at this!" Bhima roared proudly. 

Resting on a massive silver platter was a colossal, slightly misshapen circle of baked flatbread. I had spent three days desperately trying to explain the concept of pizza to the royal bakers to cure my homesickness. I had described yeast, the proofing process, and the specific ratios of water to flour. I had directed them to crush ripe tomatoes with a mortar and pestle, adding crushed garlic, sea salt, and a handful of local herbs. For mozzarella, we had aggressively strained and pressed fresh buffalo milk cheese until it reached a semi-solid, meltable consistency. 

But I hadn't expected Bhima to take personal ownership of the project. 

The pizza sitting in front of me was not a delicate Italian margherita. It was a brutal, warlike interpretation of the dish. It was two inches thick, smothered in a bubbling lake of golden cheese, and topped with thick cuts of roasted venison, wild boar, heavily spiced tomatoes, and terrifying, fist-sized green chilies. 

"I call it the 'Bhima-Chakra'!" Bhima announced, slapping the table so hard a nearby pile of lentils jumped. "Tu kha ke dekh, swaad aa jayega! Tere uss Italy-Vitely de lokaan nu bhool jayega tu!" *(I call it the Bhima-Chakra! Taste it, you'll love it! You'll forget your Italy people!)*

He grabbed a hunting dagger and effortlessly sliced a wedge the size of a small laptop. He shoved it toward me. 

I took a bite. The cheese pulled perfectly. The crust was crisp from the clay oven, but the inside was soft and pillowy. And the toppings... it was, objectively, a guaranteed cardiovascular disaster, but the sheer explosion of spice, umami, and fat was transcendental. It was the greatest thing I had eaten in five thousand years. 

"It’s a masterpiece, Bhrata Bhima," I choked out, tears of actual joy immediately streaming down my face as the green chilies began aggressively attacking my palate. 

"Hai na?!" Bhima laughed, grabbing me in a terrifying bear hug. "Aur ruk! Sirf khana nahi! Te naal tera oh kaala paani vi banaya main! Ki kehnda si tu usnu... Coca-Cola? Main usda naam badal ditta. Aaj ton eh 'Bhim Cola' hai!" *(Right?! And wait! Not just food! I also made that black water of yours! What did you call it... Coca-Cola? I changed its name. From today, it's 'Bhim Cola'!)*

He whistled sharply. Two terrified kitchen boys rushed forward carrying a massive clay amphora. Bhima poured the liquid into a silver goblet and shoved it into my hands. 

It was dark, almost black, and aggressively bubbling. I had spent hours trying to recreate a carbonated beverage, resorting to boiling dark jaggery, utilizing naturally carbonated spring water from the deeper mountain wells, and adding a frankly insane mix of tamarind paste, black salt, and roasted cumin to replicate the acid bite. 

I took a sip. 

It burned exactly like a cheap local cola, attacking the back of my throat with violent carbonation before hitting my bloodstream with the force of a sugar-coated sledgehammer. It wasn't exactly Coke, but it was dangerously addictive. I immediately let out a massive, unprompted burp that echoed loudly across the kitchen. 

"Bhim Cola," I wheezed, wiping my mouth with my silk sleeve. "It’s going to be a global phenomenon. We are going to IPO this before the winter solstice."

"Oye shera!" Bhima slapped my back, nearly sending me face-first into the pizza. "Ajj raat nu Dushasana de naal party hai courtyard mein! Tu aana hai zaroor! Tera oh nawa dance sikhaana hai ajj sab nu!" *(O Lion! Tonight there's a party with Dushasana in the courtyard! You must come! We have to teach everyone your new dance today!)*

"You mean Bhangra?"

"Aho! Te oh duja vi... ki si... Rock and Roll!" 

***

Yes. I had somehow managed to bridge the deepest mythological divide in human history by teaching the Kaurava and Pandava princes the basics of North Indian wedding dances set to a thumping 4/4 rock beat. 

Later that evening, the grand royal courtyard looked remarkably less like a gathering of noble ancient warriors and far more like a chaotic college fraternity mixer. Scores of floating oil lamps cast a warm, flickering light over the carved stone pillars. Massive bronze braziers roared with fire, keeping the encroaching autumn chill at bay. The smell of roasted meats, the newly minted "Bhima-Chakra" pizzas, and copious amounts of highly fermented somras filled the air. 

I was sitting on a cushioned marble bench near the primary wine barrels, officially designated as the event's cultural director. 

Vikarna, my designated best friend in the Kaurava camp, handed me a freshly filled chalice of somras while we watched the sheer absurdity unfold in the center of the courtyard. 

"Your cultural contributions to our royal house are terrifying, peasant," Vikarna drawled, packing his clay pipe with precision. "You are structurally dismantling thousands of years of dignified warrior tradition using baked cheese and erratic foot movements."

"Innovation requires disruption, Vikarna," I said, taking a sip of the wine. "Corporate synergies."

In the center of the courtyard, Dushasana was currently sweating profusely, trying to execute a textbook Michael Jackson moonwalk while wearing his traditional silver Kathak ghungroos. The heavy anklets jingled rhythmically as he frantically slid his linen-wrapped feet backward across the polished stone. 

"E ka Raool Bhaiya!" Dushasana yelled dynamically, slipping into his thick Bihari accent in sheer frustration. He pointed wildly at his feet. "Hamar charanwa piche jaat baa, par hum aage dekh rahal bani! Ee toh bilkul jaadu baa! Tani aur fast wala musicwa bajaiye! Ee beatuwa pe balance naahi banat!" *(What Rahul brother! My feet are going backward, but I am looking forward! This is absolute magic! Play some faster music! I can't balance on this beat!)* 

I turned to the royal musicians sitting nervously in the corner holding ancient nagada drums and stringed lutes. I had spent the last two hours trying to teach them the basic drum beat of Queen's 'We Will Rock You' combined with a standard Punjabi dhol rhythm. They looked deeply concerned for my sanity but were too well-paid to complain. 

"Just drop the beat, Dushasana!" I yelled back, feeling the somras kicking in and deciding to fully embrace the madness. "Forget the moonwalk! Give them the full Bhangra-Rock fusion! Musicians! Hit the *dhin-tak-dhin* on the fast tempo!"

The drummer smashed the nagada. A heavy, thumping, aggressive rock beat echoed across the courtyard. 

Dushasana immediately threw his hands in the air. Leaving behind four generations of classical Kathak training, he started doing a wildly aggressive, perfectly synchronized Bhangra step, mixing his elegant pirouettes with what looked like an ancient version of heavy metal headbanging. His ghungroos jingled furiously against the heavy drumbeat. 

Seeing this open display of rhythm, Bhima couldn't handle the FOMO. He jumped into the circle, waving a massive chicken leg like a glowstick. "Burrrraaaahhh!" Bhima roared, popping his shoulders and instantly executing a flawless Punjabi shoulder shrug. 

Within minutes, half the Kuru princes—both Kauravas and Pandavas—were aggressively dancing in a massive, sweaty circle to a drumbeat I had essentially plagiarized from late 90s Bollywood item numbers. Duryodhana was trying to do a complicated spin and failing spectacularly. Nakula was managing to do the exact same dance while mysteriously preserving his entirely perfect hairstyle. Only thirty feet away, a massive clay vat of Bhim Cola was being aggressively depleted. 

They were screaming "HOY! HOY! HOY!" into the ancient night sky, entirely united by the power of carbohydrates and good vibes. 

Vikarna sighed next to me, taking a long, slow drag of his pipe. "I give it exactly two weeks before Yudhishthira files a legal injunction against 'unauthorized joy and secular noise propagation'." 

***

It wasn't just the younger princes who were fascinated by my arrival. King Dhritarashtra had formally appointed me to a role that I strongly suspect he invented solely to have a new captive audience member for his terrible comedy routine. 

At least three times a week, I was summoned to the grand throne room for my official duties as the "Senior Tech Advisor to the Crown."

The meetings were always exactly the same. I would enter the massive, echoing stone chamber. The blind king would be sitting on his golden throne, looking thoroughly bored while various ministers droned on about agricultural yields in the outer provinces. 

"Rahul!" Dhritarashtra boomed the second I was announced, his unseeing eyes staring slightly to the left of where I was actually bowing. "My favorite Product Manager! Come here, come here! The agriculture briefing is putting me to sleep. I have an incredible update for the kingdom’s infrastructure network!"

The ministers immediately went dead silent, stepping back nervously. 

"Yes, My King?" I said smoothly, adjusting my dhoti and stepping forward. 

"Rahul... tell me..." Dhritarashtra wheezed, leaning forward on his golden throne, a massive grin splitting his face. "Why did the palace servant get fired from the royal mint?!"

I closed my eyes. It was happening again. I took a deep breath. "I do not know, My King. Why did the servant get fired?"

"Because," Dhritarashtra yelled, slapping his knee with a sound like a thunderclap, "he lacked *common cents*!"

He roared with laughter. It was a terrible, wheezing, entirely genuine sound of a man who thought he was the funniest person alive. 

The captain of the guard raised his spear and glared murderously at the assembled ministers. "LAUGH AND REJOICE!"

"HAHAHAHA! BRIGHTEST MIND OF OUR GENERATION! WHAT CRITICAL FISCAL WIT!" the ministers screamed in absolute, high-pitched terror. 

I fake-laughed so hard my ribs from Bhima's earlier hug started actively throbbing. "Brilliant, Maharaja. An unparalleled, razor-sharp metaphor for our current monetary policy."

"I know!" Dhritarashtra beamed, adjusting his massive gold crown. "You understand my genius, Rahul! You’re not like Bhishma. That man hasn't cracked a smile since the Bronze Age formally began. Honestly, if Bhishma laughed, the tectonic plates would shift. Now, back to business... about this highly addictive 'Bhim Cola' you've invented... can we tax it?"

I didn't even blink. I slid immediately back into my corporate persona, the one that used to confidently lie to investors during Q3 projections. "Absolutely, My King. Since it contains imported jaggery and highly processed spices, we can categorize it as a luxury beverage. I propose we put a flat 28% GST on it moving forward."

Dhritarashtra frowned. "GST?"

"Goods and Somras Tax," I replied seamlessly. 

"Goods and Somras Tax!" Dhritarashtra yelled, pointing happily at his lead scribe, who was sweating profusely over a palm leaf. "Write that down! The boy is a fiscal genius! 28%! Do you hear that, ministers? I am funding the next war entirely on carbonated jaggery water! Oh, before you go, Rahul! One more!"

I braced myself. 

"Why did the archer break up with his favorite bow?!" 

"Why, My King?"

"Because it was too stringy and entirely too controlling!" 

"REJOICE!" the guards bellowed. 

I left the throne room forty-five minutes later, my jaw aching from forced smiling, utterly convinced that if I ever made it back to 2026, I would immediately transition into a highly successful career in late-night television sidekicking. 

***

While the King and the Princes embraced me with terrifying enthusiasm, there was one relationship that was consistently, profoundly, and overwhelmingly awkward. 

Queen Mother Kunti. 

Ever since the archery tournament, Kunti had been looking at me with an expression of deep, tragic, maternal confusion. Because of my 21st-century anxiety, my poor posture, and my overall stressed demeanor, she was overwhelmingly convinced that I was the accidental byproduct of her magical boon—another secret son, much like Karna, just terribly dressed and possessing zero martial combat skills. 

I could literally never predict when she was going to strike. 

I was walking down the eastern corridor one afternoon, minding my own business and mentally drafting a sprint roadmap for Sahadeva's relay runners, when she suddenly stepped out from behind a massive carved stone pillar. She was clutching a large silver bowl to her chest. 

"Rahul," she whispered, her voice trembling with the weight of unspeakable emotion. 

I instantly froze, bowing awkwardly, almost tripping over my dhoti. "My Queen."

She stepped closer, glancing nervously around the empty corridor to ensure no one was watching. Tears immediately sprang to her eyes. She raised a trembling hand, hesitated for a second, and then gently patted my cheek. 

"You look so thin, beta," Kunti whispered, her lip quivering. "So horribly, tragically burdened by the world. Does your... does your lower back hurt from sitting all day in front of those glowing 'laptop' machines you loudly mumble about in your sleep?"

"Uh, sometimes? But it's mostly due to bad ergonomic office chairs, really—"

"Shhh," she interrupted, forcefully pressing the heavy silver bowl into my hands. "Do not speak of your pain. You do not need to be a stoic warrior here. I have made special kheer for you. Made from the milk of our finest cows and saffron from the high mountains. Eat it, my child. Build your strength." 

"Queen Kunti, this is incredibly generous, but I really am very full. Bhima just force-fed me an entire pizza—"

"Eat!" she commanded, a brief flash of queenly authority breaking through the maternal tears. And then, her voice broke again. "And remember... I am always watching over you. Even if we cannot announce it to the world. Even if society would reject you for your tight blue trousers. A mother knows. A mother's heart always knows."

"My Queen, I really am just an IT guy from East Delhi. My parents are retired school teachers, and my father takes a very aggressive interest in his mutual funds! I am not a demigod—"

"So brave," Kunti sobbed, completely ignoring my frantic defense. "So terribly brave not to demand your birthright." 

Before I could correct her again, she turned and hurried down the corridor, aggressively dabbing her streaming eyes with the edge of her silk pallu, leaving me standing alone in the hallway holding a massive bowl of elite ceremonial pudding. 

I took the kheer back to Sahadeva's room and explained the interaction to him in excruciating detail. 

Sahadeva stopped his calculations, stared blankly at a wall for an unbroken ten minutes, and then quietly requested that I stop telling him things that actively aged his soul. 

***

Not everyone was happy with the new status quo. In fact, the two most powerful men in Hastinapur were absolutely livid that the royal palace had devolved into a frat house featuring Bhim Cola, late-night pizza parties, and surprisingly functional inter-departmental HR harmony.

I was walking back from the kitchens late one night, attempting to stealthily secure some leftover pizza slices, when I heard harsh, low voices echoing from the grand library. The library was normally deserted at this hour. I instinctively ducked behind a massive bronze statue of Lord Indra, holding my breath, and carefully peered around the decorative shield. 

It was Bhishma, the terrifying patriarch of the dynasty. And pacing furiously in front of him, looking like a man rapidly losing his grip on his own sanity, was Sage Vyasa. 

"I cannot work under these conditions!" Vyasa hissed, waving a bundle of palm leaves frantically in the air. "I am trying to write a sweeping, multi-generational epic of tragedy, duty, and catastrophic warfare! I need tension, Bhishma! I need blood! I need a deep, cosmic struggle between right and wrong that will echo through eternity!"

"And what do you have?" Bhishma grumbled, leaning heavily against a bookshelf, looking like he wanted to punch through a solid granite wall. 

"I have Dushasana organizing a damn 'battle of the bands' in the main courtyard!" Vyasa screamed, pulling at his thick beard. "I have Bhima refusing to practice with his mace because his—what did he even call it?—his 'pizza dough needs to proof'! This Rahul boy is ruining my entire narrative arc! The cousins aren't plotting against each other! They aren't staring at each other with murderous intent! They are forming a startup company!"

Bhishma rubbed his temples, letting out a heavy, tragic sigh. "I tried to explain the foundational concept of Kshatriya Dharma to them this morning. I gave a passionate two-hour lecture on duty, honor, and the righteous path of the warrior."

"And?" Vyasa prompted. 

"Duryodhana raised his hand at the end," Bhishma ground out, his jaw clenching so hard it made a distinct popping noise. "He asked me if Dharma was a scalable business model. He asked if we could IPO the concept of righteousness to external investors. I almost threw myself directly into the Ganges."

"We need to get rid of him," Vyasa muttered darkly, aggressively scratching out a line of text on his palm leaf with a sharp stylus. "He is destroying the dramatic pacing. I had this beautiful, meticulously plotted scene planned for next month where Duryodhana attempts to poison Bhima's food. Classic, elegant, mythological betrayal. It was going to establish Duryodhana as the definitive villain."

"And now?"

"Now, Bhima has Rahul taste-testing literally everything for 'flavor profiles and modern plating aesthetics'! The poison plot is completely ruined! If Duryodhana poisons the food, Rahul will probably just tell him the arsenic needs more sea salt to balance the acidity!" Vyasa threw the palm leaf onto the floor in disgust. "I'm going to have to resort to a complicated real estate dispute to start the great war. A real estate dispute, Bhishma! The Iliad got a ten-year war over the most beautiful woman in the world! I am going to have to start the greatest epic in Indian history over a barren forest clearing in Khandavprastha!"

I clamped both hands over my mouth, biting my own fingers to violently suppress the laughter threatening to explode out of my chest. 

"Patience, Vyasa," Bhishma growled, closing his eyes to summon his legendary restraint. "The boy is merely a localized distraction. The core tensions of the timeline remain intact. The greed, the pride, the arrogance—it is all still there, bubbling under the surface. We just need to wait for the right trigger. Perhaps a game of dice. Something simple that spirals out of control."

"It better work," Vyasa grumbled, bending down to collect his scattered scrolls. "If this 'Bhim Cola' goes public and they start trading equity shares, I'm retiring to the Himalayas and writing romance novels."

I silently backed away, slipping back down the corridor, terrifyingly aware that the author of my own reality was currently suffering from severe writer's block directly because of my existence. 

***

Months passed. The weather turned noticeably cooler, transitioning into the crisp, biting winter of ancient northern India. The timeline remained horrifyingly stable. The war remained stalled. Gradually, I began to accept that my life now consisted of dodging Vyasa’s increasingly angry, murderous stares across the dining hall, aggressively eating Kunti's daily guilt-kheer to maintain my cover, and helping Sahadeva continuously debug the backend operations of the Bronze Age internet runners. 

And then, on a quiet Tuesday afternoon, everything went sharply, terrifyingly off the rails. 

I was officially summoned to the guest quarters of the Gandharan delegation. 

Shakuni’s chambers. 

I had largely avoided Shakuni. The man radiated the kind of slick, corporate toxicity of a middle manager who routinely CCed your boss on email chains just to highlight your minor typos. 

I walked cautiously into a room that was perpetually draped in heavy silk shadows. The air was thick with aggressively fragrant incense and the distinct, alarming clatter of bone dice hitting wood. Shakuni was sitting cross-legged on a velvet cushion, meticulously rolling his famous dice on a small, ornate table. 

"Ah. The Chief Product Officer," Shakuni sneered, his nasal voice echoing in the quiet room. He didn’t look up from the dice. "Come in, Rahul. Shut the heavy door behind you."

I shut the door. The immediate drop in acoustics made my stomach tie itself into a cold, hard knot. "You asked for me, Uncle Shakuni?"

"Sit."

I lowered myself onto a cushion across from him. He finally looked up. His eyes were sharp, cold, fiercely calculating, and completely lacking the frantic, frat-boy innocence that shaded the younger princes. He smiled, and it was a thin, deeply unpleasant expression that didn't reach his eyes. 

"I have a highly lucrative business proposition for you," Shakuni said smoothly, gathering the dice into his bony hands. 

"I'm honored, Uncle," I started, trying for my best professional deflection voice. "But I only handle product strategy for Sahadeva’s relay network. Any commercial ventures really need to go through the royal treasury—"

"Oh, save the tedious corporate jargon for the blind king," Shakuni interrupted sharply, waving a dismissive hand as if swatting an annoying fly. "I am a man of vision, Rahul. I see the underlying trends. I see the massive gaps in the current market. I understand audience retention. Look at my YouTube channel, for instance. *Mama Ke Nuskhey*. Fifty thousand subscribers. Growing week over week."

I blinked, momentarily thrown off by hearing the ancient master of deceit drop analytics terminology. "I... I have seen it. Your most recent video on 'Top 5 Psychological Ways to Gaslight Your Nephews into Giving Up Their Kingdom' has excellent audience retention."

"Exactly. Finance. Geopolitics. Deep mental manipulation. That is my personal brand," Shakuni leaned forward, steepling his long fingers. "But during my meditations, I have realized that my vision is inherently limited by a linear understanding of time. I can only manipulate what is currently happening on the board. I can only play the pieces I see. But you... you are different."

The temperature in the room seemed to drop ten degrees. My blood ran cold. "I don't know what you're talking about."

Shakuni chuckled, a dry, rattling sound that sounded exactly like shaking bones in a cup. "Please, Rahul. Let us not insult each other's intelligence. I am the grand master of secrets in this castle. A few months ago, during the archery tournament's famously rowdy after-party... I was taking a quiet, contemplative stroll near the kitchens."

I stopped breathing. 

"And I happened to overhear a highly fascinating, deeply confusing, and exceptionally loud conversation taking place behind a tapestry between you and my notoriously psychic, tight-lipped nephew Sahadeva."

The walls of the room slowly started to spin. He knew. Shakuni knew. 

"I admit, I didn't understand all the specific terminology," Shakuni continued, his voice dropping to a dangerous, conspiratorial whisper. His eyes locked onto mine like a hawk spotting a field mouse. "But I am excellent at context clues. I understood enough. Something about a 'mechanical vehicle' buried in a haystack. Something about a year called '2026'. A localized timeline rupture. You, Rahul Sharma... are not from East Delhi. At least, not *current* East Delhi. You know the future. You know *everything* that happens to us."

I opened my mouth to deny it. I tried to formulate a lie about being a traveling scholar. But my vocal cords had completely paralyzed themselves. I just stared at him, my eyes wide with sheer, unadulterated panic. 

"Don't worry, my boy. Breathe. You're turning pale," Shakuni smiled broadly now, revealing slightly stained teeth. "I haven't told Bhishma. I haven't told the perpetually angry Sage Vyasa. Heavens forbid, why would I give away such a massively lucrative competitive advantage to my rivals?"

"What... what do you want?" I finally managed to squeak out. 

"I want to launch a premier consultancy agency. A specialized stock-market-tips empire and a highly curated political PR firm. We will call it *MBHT Media*."

"MBHT?" 

"'Mera Bachcha Hai Tu'," Shakuni said proudly, gesturing broadly to an invisible billboard. "A brilliant brand name signaling absolute trust, deep family loyalty, and mild, comforting emotional manipulation! With your exact knowledge of future market crashes, impending droughts, real estate booms, and political assassinations, we will make billions. We will short the Hastinapur treasury before the impending war even technically starts! We will buy all the land in Khandavprastha for pennies before Duryodhana demands it! We will own the entire subcontinent's economic future before the dice are even rolled!"

"Uncle Shakuni," I stammered, panic aggressively flooding my nervous system. "It really doesn't work like that! The timeline is extremely fragile! If we start massive speculative trading based on future knowledge, the cosmic immune system will—"

"I have already had my legal team draft the preliminary paperwork," Shakuni interrupted, reaching into the folds of his dark robes. He pulled out an immensely long, terrifyingly dense palm-leaf contract tied with red silk thread. He dropped it onto the table with a heavy thud. "I just need your thumbprint to notarize the incorporation. It is an exclusive, iron-clad lock-in deal. Standard founder terms. Oh, there is a small non-compete clause at the bottom."

I stared at the thick stack of leaves in horror. "A non-compete?"

"Yes. A standard 10,000-year non-compete clause," Shakuni grinned, an expression of pure, capitalistic malice. "To ensure our market dominance."

"Ten... ten thousand years?!" I shrieked. "That's not exactly standard!"

"I like to think long-term," Shakuni chuckled softly. "Sign it, Rahul. Or... I might accidentally let it slip to Sage Vyasa during dinner tonight that *you* are the primary reason his beautiful, tragic epic is currently being aggressively sponsored by Bhim Cola."

"I... I need to consult my legal counsel," I blurted out, scrambling backward so fast my sandals slipped on the velvet carpet. "My lawyers are very strict about signing un-reviewed palm leaves."

"Take your time," Shakuni chuckled, tossing the bone dice onto the table with a sharp clatter. They landed, inevitably, on double sixes. "I am a reasonable man. But do not take too much time, my boy. The future waits for no one."

I bolted out of his chambers. I didn't look back. My heart was hammering against my ribs so hard I thought they would shatter. I dodged three servants, a stray royal peacock, and almost bowled over Yudhishthira, ignoring his immediate protests about my corridor speed limit violations. 

I didn't stop running until I reached Sahadeva's room, bursting through the heavy wooden door like a hijacked, out-of-control steam train. 

"Sahadeva!" I screamed, gasping for air, clutching my knees. 

Sahadeva looked up from his endless scrolls of calculations. He didn't look surprised by my dramatic entrance. He didn't look panicked. He just looked deeply, profoundly, structurally exhausted by the reality he was forced to inhabit. 

"Do not tell me," Sahadeva sighed heavily, reaching up to aggressively massage his temples. "Shakuni finally caught you. MBHT Media?"

"HE WANTS A TEN THOUSAND YEAR LOCK-IN PERIOD ON THE STOCK MARKET OPTIONS!" I yelled, hysteria finally breaking my voice. 

Sahadeva closed his eyes slowly, resigning himself to the chaos. 

"Ask me how we fix this," the prophet said.
