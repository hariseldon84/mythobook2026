# Chapter 6: The Accidental Groom and the Grand Escape

My tenure as the official future son-in-law of Panchaal began exactly the way one might expect for a man whose combat experience was limited to aggressive keyboard typing: with overwhelming, unearned hospitality and profound internal panic. 

King Drupad, having fully convinced himself that my erratic, terrified sprint through his invincible Chakravyuh was actually the highest form of tactical genius, refused to let me return to the war camp. Instead, he forcibly relocated me into the grandest guest chambers of the Panchaal palace. 

The chambers were the size of a modern shopping mall. The walls were draped in heavy silks, the floors were imported marble so highly polished I kept almost slipping and dying, and there were literally four different fountains bubbling near the bed. 

"Nothing but the best for my future general!" Drupad had bellowed happily, actively ignoring the fact that I was currently wearing sweatpants I had managed to salvage from my Maruti 800 and a t-shirt that said *‘I Survived Mondays’*. 

For three days, I was treated like royalty. Servants fed me grapes. Bards sang heroic songs about my "Method of Tactical Absense." And every afternoon, Drupad would corner me and ask insanely complex military questions like, " राहुल (Rahul), how would you optimize an arrowhead against an oiled shield matrix?" and I would blindly reply, "I would suggest shifting to a software-as-a-service model, Your Majesty, to lower overhead costs," which Drupad would write down furiously as if I was dropping golden nuggets of Vedic truth. 

But despite the luxury, a crushing weight rested on my chest. I was engaged to Draupadi. 

In every iteration of the Mahabharata, Draupadi was the fire-born heroine whose marriage to all five Pandavas served as the foundational pillar for the entire geopolitical landscape. By accidentally proposing to her—or rather, having her father propose to me while I was essentially having a panic attack—I had derailed the entire timeline. If she married an IT guy from Noida instead of Arjuna, the war wouldn't happen, Vyasa would probably literally murder me in my sleep, and the universe might completely collapse in on itself due to lack of dramatic tension. 

On my fourth night in the palace, my problems came directly to my door. 

I was tossing and turning on a bed entirely too soft for a man whose spine was heavily conditioned by cheap ergonomic office chairs, when I heard a sharp, aggressive *click* at my balcony door. 

I sat bolt upright, grabbing the nearest weapon, which was unfortunately just a solid gold decorative fruit bowl. "Who's there?! I am armed with citrus! And I'm not afraid to make a terrible, slightly sticky mess!" 

The heavy silk curtains parted. A figure stepped into the moonlight, draped in a dark cloak. The hood was pulled back to reveal the most dramatically furious, incredibly intimidating woman I had ever met. 

Draupadi. 

She stood there, radiating the exact energy of a Chief Financial Officer who has discovered someone embezzled from the company account to buy ping-pong tables. She crossed her arms, fixing me with a glare that could have melted bronze. 

"Put the bowl down, you absolute buffoon," Draupadi hissed, slipping silently into the room and immediately checking the hallway doors to ensure she wasn't followed. 

"Princess Panchali," I stammered, awkwardly lowering the fruit bowl. My knees instinctively knocked together. "I... I can explain. The victory dance was entirely an involuntary nervous tic. I don't want to marry you. The very thought terrifies me on a cellular level."

Draupadi paused. The murderous glare flickered, briefly replaced by profound confusion. "Wait. You don't want to marry me?"

"I don't have the emotional capacity for a royal marriage," I blurted out. "And I don't speak a word of classical Sanskrit. We have zero synergy. Plus, I don't want to die, and I'm pretty sure Arjuna spends at least four hours a day thinking about how fast he can shoot an arrow through my neck right now."

Draupadi's tense shoulders dropped instantly. The furious CFO facade vanished, replaced by an expression of pure, unadulterated relief. She let out a long, heavy sigh that ruffled the edges of her cloak. She walked over to one of the velvet chairs, collapsing into it and aggressively rubbing her temples. 

"Thank the gods," Draupadi muttered, dropping the classical articulation and sounding entirely like an exhausted modern college student. "Do you have any idea how horrible this week has been for me? My father has been marching around the palace for three days talking about your 'lateral thinking paradigms.' He seriously thinks you're going to revolutionize our infantry."

"I told him we should outsource our spear production to third-party vendors," I admitted, slowly sitting on the edge of the bed. "He loved it. I think he's drafting the contracts right now." 

Draupadi groaned, burying her face in her hands. "He is so gullible when it comes to weird corporate terminology. Look, Rahul. I cannot go against my father's mandate publicly. It would cause a massive political scandal. But privately? I despise you."

"That is extremely validating to hear," I said genuinely. "I am unlikable on several levels."

"I'm glad we agree," Draupadi said sharply, looking up. "I don't want you. My heart... is already set on someone else."

"Arjuna," I interrupted smoothly. 

Draupadi froze. A slight blush crept onto her cheeks, softening her fierce expression for the first time. "How did you—?"

"I am a tech analyst from the future," I reminded her. "And also, he is the most dramatically handsome, highly skilled warrior on the subcontinent who spends most of his free time posing in front of reflective water features. Who else would it be?"

"He's magnificent," Draupadi whispered, a dreamy look entering her eyes. "Did you see the way he held his bow? The absolute arrogance in his jawline? The tragic intensity in his eyes? He looks like a man who carries the weight of the world but would still remember to buy me flowers."

"Actually, he's violently insecure and heavily reliant on external validation," I corrected her. "But sure. He's very aesthetic. So, what’s the plan? How do we get you married to Arjuna so your father stops trying to make me the Chief Operating Officer of Panchaal?"

Draupadi sat up straight, instantly switching back into business mode. "My father won't retract his offer unless Arjuna actively steps up, proves his superiority, and makes an official claim. But Arjuna is so obsessed with his 'Kshatriya honor' right now because he lost to the Chakravyuh, he refuses to even look at the palace."

"Ah. Classic bruised male ego. That's a fundamental error."

"So," Draupadi concluded, leaning forward. "I need to woo him. I need to entice him to come back to the palace and officially challenge you for my hand."

"And how are you going to do that?" I asked, raising an eyebrow. "Going to write him a romantic poem about his archery?"

"No," Draupadi snorted dismissively. "I am going to use intellectual manipulation. I have studied him from the balcony for three days. You said it yourself—he is reliant on validation. If I just act pretty, I'm just another princess. To catch his attention, I need to act like the smartest person in the room. I need to aggressively critique his archery posture and offer strategic upgrades to his bow tension."

I stared at her. My respect for Draupadi instantly tripled. She wasn't just a fiery mythological icon. She was an absolute savage. 

"You want to negg him?" I asked, profoundly amused. 

"I don't know what 'negg' means, but I am going to emotionally dismantle him until he is entirely obsessed with my approval," Draupadi nodded with terrifying conviction. "And you, my accidental fiancé, are going to help me."

"I am? How?"

"You are going to tell me exactly what specific, overly complicated technical terms he likes. And then, once I have him properly intrigued, you are going to host a very public, very relaxed 'diplomatic dinner' date for the three of us to seal the deal." Draupadi gave me a calculating smile. "If you do this for me, Rahul... I promise I will make sure my father lets you leave Panchaal alive."

I stood up instantly. "I am going to be the greatest wingman in the history of the ancient world."

For the next seven days, the royal courtyard of Panchaal became the staging ground for the most deeply sarcastic, highly tactical courtship I had ever witnessed. 

As per Drupad's orders, the Kuru princes had been allowed to camp in the outer gardens. Every afternoon, Arjuna would angrily practice shooting arrows into wooden targets, violently over-compensating for his recent defeat in the Chakravyuh. 

And every afternoon, Draupadi would 'casually' stroll past the archery range, accompanied by me, playing the role of her doting, clueless fiancé. 

"Oh, look," Draupadi drawled loudly on the first day, pausing near the targets. "The great Arjuna of Hastinapur. Tell me, Prince, do you always over-torque your draw string like that, or do you actively enjoy losing ten percent of your aerodynamic velocity?"

Arjuna stopped mid-draw. His jaw practically unhinged. He spun around, staring at the princess in absolute shock. 

"My... my aerodynamic what?" Arjuna stammered. 

"Velocity," Draupadi sighed heavily, giving him a deeply disappointed look. "The sheer wind resistance on that arrow is offensive. Your elbow is too low. You're bleeding potential kinetic energy. Come on, Rahul. I cannot bear to watch such inefficient biomechanics." 

She grabbed my arm and dragged me away, leaving the greatest archer of the age staring blankly at his own elbow, experiencing a profound existential crisis. 

I had given her the vocabulary. She was wielding it like a scalpel. 

By day three, Arjuna was eagerly waiting for her to walk by. The moment Draupadi appeared, he tried to casually demonstrate a complex ricochet shot off a stone wall. 

"Nice effort," Draupadi yawned, inspecting her perfectly manicured nails. "But you failed to account for the micro-fractures in the stone. A true intellectual would have analyzed the structural integrity of the target surface before attempting a deflection shot. Really, Arjuna, your math is sloppy. A solid B-minus performance."

"B-minus?" Arjuna asked, his voice cracking slightly. For a demigod prince, academic grading systems apparently triggered deep trauma. "I can adjust the math! Wait! Let me show you my wind-shear calculations!" 

But Draupadi was already walking away, leaving him desperately shouting physics equations into the courtyard. 

By day seven, the manipulation had achieved maximum effectiveness. Arjuna was essentially a highly lethal puppy, completely fixated on earning Draupadi's academic and strategic approval. It was equal parts hilarious and horrifying to watch the mythological equivalent of Superman nervously ask a woman if his bow-grip achieved proper ergonomic flow. 

"He’s ready," Draupadi whispered to me as we watched Arjuna aggressively polishing his arrows from a balcony. "Set up the date."

I immediately went to the kitchens. If I learned anything from my corporate days, it was that a successful networking event lived or died entirely on catering. I didn't trust the Panchaal chefs to deliver the requisite casual vibes, so I invoked my emergency culinary asset. 

I found Bhima in the royal pantry, violently aggressively eating a roasted lamb shank while arguing with a sack of flour. 

"Bhima," I announced, stepping into the pantry. "I need you to cook the greatest meal of your life. I am hosting a diplomatic mixer tonight, and the entire future of the epic depends on the appetizers."

Bhima’s eyes lit up like a supernova. The sheer prospect of taking over an international kitchen instantly banished any lingering resentment he held regarding my engagement. 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima boomed, dropping the lamb shank and wrapping me in a suffocating, flour-covered hug. "Tune mainu yaad kita! Tera bhai aithe hai! Menu das, ki banawaan? Tandoori chicken? Butter paneer? Ya phir tera oh 'pasta' jida main desi version banaya si?" *(You remembered me! Your brother is here! Tell me, what should I make? Tandoori chicken? Butter paneer? Or that 'pasta' for which I made a desi version?)*

"Go entirely wild, Bhima," I instructed. "But make it classy. First dates require elegance."

"Classy!" Bhima roared, rolling up his sleeves. "Main enni classy roti banawanga ki Drupad de piche de khet vi nachenge!" *(I will make such a classy roti that even Drupad's backyard fields will dance!)*


That evening, the private royal terrace overlooking the eastern gardens was transformed into a bizarre, ancient fusion of a romantic Michelin-star restaurant and a highly stressful corporate mixer. 

I had set up a low table with silk cushions, flickering oil lamps, and a massive jug of premium, heavily diluted somras. Arjuna arrived early, looking uncharacteristically nervous. He spent five minutes trying to find the best angular lighting to showcase his jawline before carefully sitting down. 

Draupadi arrived exactly ten minutes late—a power move she claimed established absolute dominance. 

She glided onto the terrace wearing a stunning crimson sari, her expression entirely neutral. She sat down, completely ignoring Arjuna's attempts to pull out her cushion. She looked at me. 

"Rahul," she demanded. "Where is the tactical review of the southern border?"

"I am currently compiling the data, Princess," I replied smoothly, pouring her a drink. "But tonight, let us focus on networking."

Arjuna cleared his throat, trying to insert himself into the conversation. "Princess Draupadi. You look... exceptionally aerodynamic tonight. Very streamlined." 

I almost choked on my somras. Draupadi didn't blink. 

"Aerodynamic," Draupadi repeated slowly, tapping her chin. "A fascinating compliment. Though technically, my sari contains entirely too many loose folds for optimal wind resistance. If I were truly aerodynamic, I would be wearing boiled leather. Are you suggesting I should wear boiled leather to dinner, Arjuna?"

Arjuna immediately panicked. "No! I didn't mean—I meant you look highly efficient! Your beauty has excellent... structural integrity?"

"Keep digging, Prince," Draupadi sighed heavily, taking a slow sip of her drink. "Your conversational skills are currently mirroring your archery draw-weight: fundamentally unbalanced."

"I can balance it!" Arjuna practically pleaded, leaning across the table. "Test me! Ask me anything about trajectory!"

The date proceeded exactly like a stressful job interview where the interviewer actively despises the candidate, and the candidate is desperately in love with the interviewer. It was glorious. Draupadi ruthlessly interrogated Arjuna on everything from his political philosophies to his preferred ratio of feather-fletching on heavy arrows. Arjuna answered every question with the desperate, sweaty enthusiasm of a straight-A student taking a surprise pop quiz. 

And then, Bhima’s catering arrived. 

The heavy terrace doors slammed open. Bhima strode out, wearing a massive linen chef’s apron, carrying a solid bronze tray the size of a shield. 

"Oye mere shera!" Bhima bellowed into the romantic ambiance, completely shattering the mood. He slammed the tray down onto the delicate table. The wood groaned under the weight. 

"Appetizers!" Bhima announced proudly in thick Punjabi. "Main banaya hai 'Tandoori-Momo-Fusion'! Ehde vich main poora bakra bharya hai! Kha ke dekho, tuhanu jannat de darshan ho jange!" *(Appetizers! I made 'Tandoori-Momo-Fusion'! I stuffed a whole goat inside it! Eat it, you will see glimpses of heaven!)*

Draupadi stared at the massive, aggressively spiced dumplings sitting on the table. Each dumpling was the size of a human fist and radiated intense heat and the profound smell of garlic. 

"Fascinating," Draupadi murmured, picking up a small silver fork. She took a delicate bite. Her eyes widened slightly. "This... this is incredibly good. Complex flavor profile. High caloric efficiency."

Bhima beamed, slapping Arjuna on the back so hard the great archer almost face-planted into his own plate. "Dekhya? Bhabhi ji nu pasand aaya! Oh sorry, I mean, Princess ji!" *(See? Sister-in-law liked it!)*

The dinner progressed splendidly. Arjuna successfully answered a heavily complex question regarding chariot wheel lubrication, Draupadi smiled genuinely at him for the first time, and I realized my wingman duties were rapidly concluding. 

But as I reached for another piece of Tandoori goat-momo, a slight rustle in the heavy silk curtains at the edge of the terrace caught my eye. 

I paused, chewing slowly. 

Through a tiny gap in the fabric, I saw the distinct gleam of a bronze helmet. Someone was watching us. And judging by the incredibly arrogant, heavily spiked shape of the helmet... it was Dhrishtadyumna. 

My blood ran cold. Dhrishtadyumna was Drupad’s son, the general who had mercilessly roasted my friends at the Chakravyuh. He was highly protective of his sister and fiercely loyal to his father's crazy engagement plan. 

I watched as the shadow behind the curtain suddenly vanished. He had seen enough. He had seen his sister openly flirting with Arjuna while her "official fiancé" sat there feeding them goat-dumplings. 

"Excuse me," I mumbled, standing up abruptly. "I need to check the... backend supply chain of the kitchen."

I bolted off the terrace and slipped into the dark, echoing corridors of the palace. I didn’t go to the kitchen. I aggressively power-walked toward King Drupad’s private chambers, sticking closely to the shadows like a highly anxious, un-stealthy ninja. 

I crept up to the heavy wooden doors of Drupad's office and slipped behind a large, decorative tapestry depicting a very confusing battle involving giant swans. 

Inside, voices were already raised. 

"Father!" Dhrishtadyumna's voice echoed through the thick wood. "The engagement is a sham! I just saw them on the eastern terrace! Panchali is actively manipulating the Hastinapur prince, and that fake-genius Rahul is just sitting there eating dumplings! They are making a mockery of your royal decree!"

There was a heavy silence. And then, the terrible, booming sound of King Drupad's voice, shaking with absolute fury. 

"A sham?" King Drupad roared. "He thinks he can play bureaucratic games with the King of Panchaal?! He thinks his 'optimization strategies' can insult my family?! He is a fraud!"

"What shall I do, Father?" Dhrishtadyumna asked eagerly. 

"Arrest him!" Drupad yelled, slamming his fists on his desk. "Arrest the boy in the blue trousers! Throw him in the deepest, dampest dungeon we have! Cut off his access to the royal Wi-Fi! I want him executed at dawn for treason against the state and emotional manipulation of his future father-in-law!"

Behind the swan tapestry, my entire life flashed before my eyes. Mostly, it was just images of incomplete code commits and unfinished Netflix series, but it was still terrifying. 

I didn't wait to hear the rest of the execution plan. I practically flew down the corridor, my sandals slapping wildly against the marble. 

I needed an extraction team. I needed the Kurus. 

I sprinted out of the main palace complex and into the royal gardens where the Pandavas and Kauravas had established their temporary camp. It was late at night, but the camp was surprisingly active. 

I burst into the central pavilion, wheezing for breath. 

Suyodhana was sitting at a large table, furiously writing onto palm leaves. Dushasana was practicing a deep plié in the corner. Yudhishthira was meticulously organizing a stack of compliance reports, and Bhima had just returned from closing down his kitchen pop-up. 

"Guys!" I gasped, clutching my chest and collapsing against the central tent pole. "Code red! System failure! Drupad knows everything! He issued an arrest warrant! General Dhrishtadyumna is coming to execute me!"

The camp instantly went deadly silent. 

"Execute you?" Suyodhana demanded, dropping his stylus. A look of deep, political irritation crossed his face. "This is entirely unacceptable! I have spent the last six days actively trying to bribe the Panchaal council elders to vote for me in the next Hastinapur generalized elections! If I am harboring a fugitive, my entire PR campaign will be ruined! Rahul, you have structurally jeopardized my focus groups!"

"Bro, I am literal minutes away from being locked in a damp dungeon!" I yelled, panicked. "Forget your focus groups! We have to flee!"

"Ehh Rahul Bhaiya," Dushasana sighed, dropping his plié and shaking his head in his thick Bihari accent. "Ee toh bada gadbad ho gail. Hum toh sochat rahli pachaas-saath naya dance step seekhengi idhar ke logan sange... ab bhaagne ke pari?" *(Oh Rahul brother, this has gone completely wrong. I was thinking I would learn fifty-sixty new dance steps with the local people here... now we have to run?)*

Before Suyodhana could officially refuse to help me, a dark figure slid into the pavilion. 

It was Draupadi, having miraculously snuck out of the palace without triggering the guards. 

"He is telling the truth," Draupadi said sharply, her eyes flashing. "My brother is marching the royal guard down here right now. You must all leave. Immediately."

Suyodhana crossed his arms, raising his chin arrogantly. "Why should we flee, Princess? We are the princes of Hastinapur. We have diplomatic immunity. And frankly, this peasant's engagement glitch is not my problem. My poll numbers in the outer sectors are highly dependent on this peace mission."

Draupadi looked at Suyodhana, aggressively analyzing him for two seconds before completely destroying him. 

"Prince Suyodhana," Draupadi said, her voice dripping with cold authority. "If you help Rahul escape tonight securely, I promise you my personal endorsement. I will utilize the Panchaal royal network to secure you a guaranteed 30% surge in your Hastinapur voting analytics. And... I will publicly state that your brand imaging is vastly superior to Yudhishthira's."

Suyodhana’s eyes widened to the size of saucers. An endorsement from Panchaal? Superior brand imaging? 

"Pack the chariots!" Suyodhana roared, spinning around and grabbing his mace. "Dushasana, grab the analytics files! Leave the dancing gear! We are initiating an emergency extraction algorithm! The Kaurava Consulting Group protects its assets!"

"Wait!" Yudhishthira suddenly stood up, holding a tightly rolled compliance scroll. "This is a violation of international law! As the eldest prince, I must insist on righteousness! We cannot simply flee in the night! We must stay and face the judicial system of Panchaal! It is our Dharma!"

Bhima cracked his knuckles, stepping toward his eldest brother. 

"Oye wadda aya righteousness wala!" Bhima yelled in aggressive Punjabi, aggressively pointing a thick finger at Yudhishthira. "Jadon Chakravyuh gheri si, tu kahan si?! Tu ped te chad ke baitha si aur sadi galtiyan likh reha si! 'Tu compliance tod reha hai Bhima!' Chup kar, aur rath te beh ja chup karke!" *(Oh Mr. Righteousness! Where were you when we were trapped in the Chakravyuh?! You were sitting on a tree documenting our mistakes! 'You are breaking compliance Bhima!' Shut up and sit quietly on the chariot!)*

Yudhishthira looked deeply offended. "I was conducting an objective third-party audit of our combat inefficiencies!"

"Aur hum toh ehiyo bolab ki eke audit nahi, eke darr kahal jaala Bhaiya!" Dushasana chimed in, supporting Bhima entirely. "Tani chup chap bhagi line se!" *(And I would say this isn't an audit, this is called fear, Brother! Just run quietly in line!)*

"You have no jurisdiction here, Yudhishthira!" Suyodhana yelled triumphantly. "Get on the chariot! We are leaving!"

Within three chaotic minutes, the entire Hastinapur delegation had violently packed their belongings. I jumped onto the back of Arjuna's chariot, who had just returned from the terrace looking incredibly pleased with himself. 

"Don't worry, Rahul," Arjuna said confidently, grabbing the reins. "My structural aerodynamic calculations are flawless. They will never catch us."

As the chariot lurched forward, crashing through the outer garden walls, I looked back. Draupadi stood on a small hill, the moonlight catching her sari. She flashed me a quick, sharp salute, a perfect corporate "job well done."

"Goodbye, my beautiful, frightening CEO," I whispered, clutching the chariot rail as we sped aggressively into the night, officially concluding my four-day tenure as the most terrified fiancé in mythological history.


While I was dealing with my own immediate survival in Panchaal, I was completely unaware that Suyodhana was actively utilizing our diplomatic immunity to run a highly aggressive, deeply flawed political campaign in the local markets. 

I discovered this much later when going through the Kaurava Consulting Group's archived records. Apparently, while Arjuna was practicing archery and Draupadi was studying his aerodynamics from the balcony, Suyodhana had set up a literal focus group booth in the center of the Panchaal bazaar. 

He had commandeered a large silk tent, hired local criers, and was offering free, slightly stale Hastinapur sweetmeats to any local merchant who would sit down and fill out a palm-leaf survey about his "future leadership potential."

"Now, listen to me, commoners," Suyodhana had announced, standing in front of a completely baffled group of Panchaal blacksmiths and potters. "I am currently optimizing my platform for the impending transition of power in Hastinapur. I need solid analytics. Scale of one to ten, with ten being ‘absolute divine manifestation’ and one being ‘Yudhishthira’... how would you rate my brand appeal in this specific region?"

An elderly potter raised a shaky hand. "My lord... we do not know who you are. Or what a brand is. Can we just have the sweetmeat?"

"My brand is ruthless synergistic dominance!" Suyodhana yelled, slamming his mace onto the table so hard a bowl of sweets shattered. "Write it down! Actually, let me pivot. Dushasana!"

Dushasana had stepped out from the back of the tent, clad in vibrant yellow silks, holding a set of small hand cymbals. 

"Bhaiya! Bolo ka zaroorat baa?" Dushasana asked brightly in his Bhojpuri accent. *(Brother! Tell me what's needed?)*

"Perform an interpretive dance demonstrating my policy on lowering import tariffs!" Suyodhana commanded. 

"Ji bhaiya!" 

For twenty agonizing minutes, the merchants of Panchaal were forced to watch the second eldest Kaurava prince frantically spin and leap around a tent, aggressively clashing cymbals together, while Suyodhana yelled keywords like 'TAX REDUCTION' and 'SYNERGY' over the performance. 

When the survey results came back, 100% of the respondents had written that they were actively praying to Indra for rain to wash away the memory of the consultation. 

This deep, profound commitment to the corporate hustle was exactly why Suyodhana was so furious when we finally fled in the middle of the night. He hadn't finished parsing the data. He hadn't managed to establish a local subsidiary. 

But as we fled in the chariots, enduring Yudhishthira's endless compliance audit, another significant conversational tangent occurred that I hadn't initially absorbed due to my own sheer panic. 

Bhima, frustrated by Yudhishthira's nagging, had turned to me. 

"Oye Raool!" Bhima yelled over the wind. "Tu itna tension kyun le reha hai? Draupadi bhabhi ne kaha hai na, oh tera endorsement karegi? Tere paas backup hai!" *(Hey Rahul! Why are you taking so much tension? Sister-in-law Draupadi said she will endorse you right? You have backup!)*

"Bhima, she endorsed Suyodhana!" I yelled back. "Not me! I am her fake fiancé who is currently fleeing a massive state-mandated arrest warrant!"

"Technically," Arjuna interrupted, leaning back from the reins. "She merely promised to utilize her administrative capacity to alter poll results. That is a highly functional political maneuver. I must say, her strategic understanding of electoral fraud is... intensely attractive to me."

"You are attracted to electoral fraud?" I asked, completely bewildered. 

"I am attracted to efficiency!" Arjuna shot back defensively. "Have you seen her handwriting? It is perfectly spaced! Her margins are immaculate! A woman who respects the margins of a palm leaf is a woman who understands order in the universe!"

It was deeply strange listening to Arjuna, the supreme warrior of the era, completely ignore Draupadi’s physical beauty in favor of aggressively romanticizing her bureaucratic competence and handwriting spacing. 

"Well, I am just glad we are going on holiday soon," Yudhishthira sighed, rolling up his compliance report as the walls of Hastinapur finally appeared in the distance. "I need a heavily structured itinerary, a relaxing itinerary full of scheduled rest periods."



The chariot ride away from Panchaal was perhaps the most aggressively dysfunctional road trip I had ever experienced in any timeline. 

While Arjuna desperately tried to navigate the chariot out of the kingdom's borders utilizing the stars—muttering constantly to himself about 'Draupadi-approved celestial trajectories'—Yudhishthira decided this perfectly tense, life-or-death escape was the ideal time to host a mandatory post-event project retrospective. 

Yudhishthira pulled out a massive stack of crinkled palm leaves. The Hastinapur Eldest Prince cleared his throat loudly, ignoring the sound of distant Panchaal wardrums.

"Attention, Kaurava Consulting Group and Pandava Associates!" Yudhishthira called out, precariously balancing on the back of Arjuna's speeding chariot while Bhima and Suyodhana rode alongside us. "Since we have successfully evaded the immediate threat of execution, I would like to review my third-party audit of the Chakravyuh engagement. I observed several severe violations of standard operating procedure."

"Yudhishthira, we are literally running for our lives!" Suyodhana yelled from his chariot, his brand-imaging clearly suffering from wind-shear. "Put the compliance report away!"

"As the project lead, it is my Dharma to address our actionable feedback!" Yudhishthira insisted, aggressively unfurling the first leaf. "Violation number one: Communication Breakdown. When General Dhrishtadyumna utilized targeted emotional warfare regarding parathas, Bhima failed to log a formal grievance with the HR department before engaging the enemy. This shows a complete lack of chain-of-command respect."

"Oye tu bakwas band kar!" Bhima roared from across the dirt path. "Ohne meri maa de hath de parathe di bezzati kitti si! Main HR nu ki labda? Main sidha ohda muh todna si!" *(Hey you stop your nonsense! He insulted my mother's hand-made parathas! What would I look for HR? I just wanted to break his face!)*

"It doesn't matter," Yudhishthira sighed, making a massive checkmark with a piece of charcoal. "You lacked synergistic restraint. Violation number two: Arjuna. When presented with a fictional Guinness World Record involving a mole on a bird's eye, you abandoned the left flank entirely to pursue unverified international clout."

"It was a legitimate target!" Arjuna argued defensively, nearly swerving the chariot into a ditch. "You saw the wind variables! I could have hit it!"

"Violation number three," Yudhishthira plowed on enthusiastically, visibly alive with the thrill of bureaucratic pedantry. "Dushasana engaging in unauthorized classical dance routines in a combat zone. Highly unsafe. No protective footwear was worn during the pirouettes."

"Ee hamar passion hai bhaiya!" Dushasana yelled defensively from his chariot. "Kala aur yuddh ka sangam! Thoda aesthetic bhi toh hona chahiye!" *(This is my passion brother! The union of art and war! There should be some aesthetic too!)*

Yudhishthira kept reading his endless list of tactical audit failures for another forty-five miles. By the time he reached an incredibly niche complaint regarding the improper storage protocol of Suyodhana's mace during a fast turn, the entire Hastinapur rescue team was practically begging the Panchaal army to catch up and arrest us just to make the compliance review stop. 

"If he doesn't stop talking about 'best practices'," Suyodhana ground out, his face pale, "I am going to intentionally crash my own chariot."


The journey back to Hastinapur was significantly less majestic than the journey out. There were no banners waving, no songs of anticipated victory, and the princes were largely exhausted from essentially running away from their first major military deployment. 

When our chariots finally clattered into the grand courtyard of Hastinapur, we were not greeted by a hero's welcome. We were greeted by Sahadeva and Vikarna. 

They were sitting casually on some marble steps, sharing a large bowl of what looked suspiciously like ancient popcorn, watching our battered, dirt-covered procession arrive with expressions of intense, unbothered amusement. 

"Ah," Sahadeva drawled, dramatically checking an invisible watch on his wrist. "The great conquerors return. Exactly three days, fourteen hours, and twenty-two minutes earlier than predicted. Please, tell me, how did the hostile takeover of Panchaal go? Did King Drupad surrender immediately upon seeing your impressive synergistic alignment?"

Suyodhana aggressively dismounted his chariot, throwing his helmet to the ground. "Do not speak to me, Prophet! The market conditions were unfavorable! We faced an asymmetric defensive structure that violated several international treaties regarding fair play!"

"You mean the Chakravyuh?" Vikarna asked lazily, popping a kernel into his mouth. "The massive spinning circle of death? Yeah, Drona told us about it in the syllabus. None of you actually did the reading, did you?"

Bhima stepped out of his chariot, looking entirely exhausted but still aggressively defensive. "Oye chup kar! Oh ghanchakkar gol-gol ghumda si! Mainu chakkar aa gaya, nahi taan main sabnu tod denda!" *(Hey shut up! That stupid thing was spinning round and round! I got dizzy, otherwise I would have broken everything!)*

"You yelled threats at an elephant, Bhima," Arjuna sighed, leaning against his chariot wheel. "It was embarrassing."

Before the fighting could fully escalate in the courtyard, a massive trumpet blast echoed from the grand balcony above. Everyone stopped and looked up. 

King Dhritarashtra was standing on the balcony, flanked by his guards, wearing an incredibly elaborate purple robe and a gold crown that was slightly tilted to the left. 

"MY BOYS HAVE RETURNED!" Dhritarashtra boomed happily, waving wildly in the general direction of the stables instead of the courtyard. "I sense the glorious smell of victory! And sweat! Mostly sweat! Welcome back to Hastinapur!"

The cousins awkwardly bowed. 

"Tell me, my brave warriors!" Dhritarashtra continued, leaning over the railing. "Before we discuss the looting of Panchaal... why did the incompetent scribe get fired from his job?"

A collective groan echoed across the courtyard. All the princes physically sagged, the adrenaline of the escape instantly replaced by the crushing dread of bad comedy. 

"We do not know, Father," Suyodhana answered through gritted teeth, rubbing his temples in pain. "Why did the incompetent scribe get fired?"

"Because he couldn't... DRAW A CONCLUSION!" Dhritarashtra roared with laughter, slapping the railing so hard a decorative brick dislodged and fell into the bushes. 

This time, the guards were not taking any chances with the rebellious audience. The Captain of the Guard stepped forward, aggressively dropping his spear so it clattered loudly on the stone. He glared down at the exhausted, defeated royal army. 

"LAUGH, YOU MISERABLE FOOLS!" the Captain screamed. "REJOICE IN THE FISCAL HUMOR OF THE KING! HAHAHA!"

Terrified of being arrested immediately after escaping an arrest warrant, the entire Hastinapur army—including myself, the Pandavas, and the Kauravas—began to aggressively, painfully fake-laugh. 

"HAHAHA! BRILLIANT, FATHER! HAHAHA!" Suyodhana yelled, forcing a smile that made him look like he was actively passing a kidney stone. 

"Oh, the wordplay is staggering!" I shouted, clapping my hands. 

"I love it!" Dhritarashtra beamed blindly. "You see, Pitamaha?! They love it! The youth respect a good pun!" 

Speaking of Bhishma, the terrifying patriarch was standing near the training grounds, deep in conversation with Guru Dronacharya. 

As we dispersed from the courtyard, I crept closer to the training grounds, hiding behind a large stone pillar to eavesdrop. From the harsh set of his jaw and the furious aura radiating from him, Bhishma was absolutely furious about the failed mission. 

"I do not understand, Drona," Bhishma growled, pacing in front of the Guru. "They did not capture Drupad. They were lured into a Chakravyuh and forced to flee in the night like common thieves. Your honor has not been avenged. The Guru Dakshina has failed! We must march our entire combined forces back to Panchaal and burn it to the ground! We must establish martial dominance!"

I winced, waiting for the equally furious explosion from Dronacharya. 

But Drona just smiled. It wasn't his usual, highly severe warrior grimace. It was a soft, glowing, deeply relaxed smile of a man who was operating on an entirely different plane of spiritual tranquility. 

"Relax, Bhishma," Drona said smoothly, casually leaning on his wooden staff. "Why so aggressive? Why so fundamentally tied to the material concept of revenge? Drupad is a man. We are all men. The universe is a vast, floating river of energy, my friend."

Bhishma stopped pacing. He stared at Drona as if the Guru had suddenly sprouted a second head. 

"Have you eaten a poisonous mushroom?" Bhishma demanded. "He insulted you! He denied your friendship! You ordered the boys to drag him here so you could spit on his sandals!"

"That was yesterday's Drona," Drona sighed happily, pulling a beautifully carved, slightly empty clay jug from his robes and taking a small sip. "Today's Drona realizes that holding a grudge is just drinking poison and expecting the other person to die. Or in this case, drinking premium, twelve-year oak-barrel Somras and expecting *nobody* to die. It really takes the edge off."

"You... you are withdrawing the military mandate?" Bhishma asked, completely bewildered. 

"I am officially retiring from geopolitical vengeance," Drona nodded sagely. "I am going to start a podcast. Perhaps write some poetry about trees. Tell the boys they passed the class. Except Yudhishthira. He gets a C-minus for hiding in a tree during the attack." 

Drona wandered away, humming a cheerful ancient tune, leaving the greatest warrior of Hastinapur staring into the distance, completely unable to process the total destruction of his militaristic worldview. 

Unfortunately for me, Bhishma’s anger did not dissipate. It merely found a new target. 

Later that evening, I was walking down a secluded corridor near the royal archives when I heard furious whispering. I ducked into a small shadowy alcove, my pulse immediately spiking. Whenever there was whispering in this palace, it usually meant someone was trying to dramatically alter my timeline. 

I peeked out. It was Bhishma, and standing opposite him, looking like a manic, desperate wildman... was Sage Vyasa. 

"The narrative is collapsing!" Vyasa hissed, waving a massive bundle of un-written palm leaves violently in Bhishma’s face. "Drona has refused to declare war! The cousins are bonding over shared trauma! Suyodhana is literally doing brand consultations with Draupadi via proxy messengers! I need blood, Bhishma! I need extreme, horrific betrayal!"

"They are too comfortable," Bhishma growled, crossing his massive arms. "They have grown soft in the palace. Everything is being treated as a 'team building exercise.'"

"Exactly!" Vyasa completely agreed, his eyes glowing with maniacal literary intent. "So, we need to separate them. We need a targeted assassination attempt! What do people do when they are too comfortable, Bhishma? They go on summer holidays!"

Bhishma frowned. "Summer holidays? Like migrating to the hills?"

"Yes! I have written a sub-plot!" Vyasa unspooled a long palm leaf. "We send the Pandavas on a mandatory royal holiday. To a beautiful, peaceful resort home in the forest. A house made entirely of highly flammable materials! Lacquer! Gheee! Dry bamboo! We wait until they are asleep, and we burn the house to the ground!"

My jaw dropped. The Lakshagriha. The House of Wax. The defining assassination attempt that formally triggers the apocalyptic hatred between the cousins. 

"And who do we blame?" Bhishma asked, rubbing his chin. 

"The Kauravas, obviously!" Vyasa grinned maliciously. "Suyodhana is planning to take his brothers on a luxury off-site to a retreat in the southern mountains next week. It’s the perfect alibi framing! The Pandavas will survive the fire—because I need them for the Sequel—but they will forever believe Suyodhana tried to incinerate them! The startup synergy will be destroyed forever! Chaos! Pure, marketable chaos!"

I silently backed deeper into the alcove, absolutely horrified. Vyasa was actively architecting terrorism to save his book sales. 

I needed to warn the Pandavas. I had to stop them from going on the holiday. 

Before I could move, a hand suddenly grabbed my shoulder. 

I practically jumped out of my skin, spinning around to scream, but a hand clamped firmly over my mouth. 

I was looking directly into the wide, intensely focused eyes of a man I had never seen before. He was dressed in the simple robes of a chief minister, but he possessed an aura of deep, profound wisdom.

"Do not scream, time-traveler," the man whispered, releasing his hand.

"Who... who are you?" I gasped, clutching my chest.

"I am Vidura," the man stated proudly.

When we finally returned, the relief of ending the road trip briefly overshadowed the humiliation of our retreat. That is, until the absolute incompetence of the Hastinapur intelligence network was laid bare to me by the Chief Minister himself. 

Before I had encountered Vidura in the hallway—the man claiming to be the deeply wise, omniscient spy master—I had harbored some hope that there were competent adults running this timeline. 

But as Vidura had grabbed me in that corridor, revealing his complete inability to remember his own master escape plan for the Pandavas, that hope evaporated. 

Let me expand on that terrifying conversation with Vidura. Standard mythology presents Vidura as a bastion of intellect and foresight. In reality, he was an incredibly well-meaning man who was entirely overwhelmed by the sheer volume of information and chaotic idiocy surrounding him that rendered his 200 GB memory space in brain incapable of storing new facts or sometimes deleting extremely important old facts.

"Okay, wait," Vidura had said, pacing in the small alcove. "I remember the second part of the plan. It's brilliant. We encode a secret message onto a series of exceptionally trained carrier pigeons. Only Sahadeva will know how to decode the cypher. It involves Vedic math and a very specific sequence of seed consumption."

"That sounds amazing!" I had said, deeply relieved. "Have you sent the pigeons?"

"Well," Vidura frowned, adjusting his quartz glasses again. "Here's the issue. Bhima thought they were appetizers for a new pop-up restaurant he was testing. So... the encrypted pigeons are currently being digested."

"He ate the encrypted intelligence network?!" 

"He said they needed more cumin," Vidura sighed heavily. "This is what I am dealing with, Rahul. I try to be the grand manipulator in the shadows. I try to orchestrate deep political saves. But every time I construct a masterful 4D-chess move, someone either eats the chess pieces, sets fire to the board, or Yudhishthira tries to audit the rulebook!"

"So what do we do about the flammable summer house?!" I had pressed frantically. "They leave on Tuesday! Vyasa is going to turn them into fondue!"

"I have a backup spy!" Vidura announced brightly. "An expert miner! I will send him ahead to dig a massive, undetectable tunnel under the floorboards of the resort! The Pandavas will slip down the tunnel the moment the fire starts, totally bypassing Vyasa's sabotage!"

"Okay! Great! Do it! We take Pandavas out and make Vyasa and Bheeshma believe that they are dead. We take couple of years to figure out how to neutralize the upcoming threat."

"Except..." Vidura hesitated, looking deeply ashamed. 

"Except what?" I asked, feeling my blood pressure spiking to lethal levels. 

"I accidentally gave the miner the WRONG GPS coordinates," Vidura confessed. "He is currently digging a highly advanced, totally secret escape tunnel directly under the royal dairy farm. If the cows ever need to flee an assassination attempt, we are fully covered."

I stared at the Chief Minister of Hastinapur. I reached out and gently patted his shoulder. 

"Vidura," I said softly softly. "Please just go write your grocery list. And maybe consider early retirement." 

"I think I need a sabbatical," Vidura agreed miserably, dropping the dud smoke bomb and jogging away.

And that was why I had desperately sprinted to Sahadeva’s room, praying that his psychic foresight bypassed the absolute catastrophe of the royal spy network. Fortunately, Sahadeva's foresight was completely operational. But to my absolute shock, instead of viewing the impending arson as an assassination attempt, Sahadeva viewed it as an aggressively mandated, fully funded vacation. 

"Wait," I said, leaning against Sahadeva's door frame, absolutely flabbergasted as he finished packing his ancient sunglasses. "Let me get this straight. You *know* the house is made of lacquer and ghee. You *know* Vyasa and Bhishma are going to light a match and blame the Kauravas and save you majestically. You *know* Vidura's encrypted pigeons are currently inside Bhima's stomach and the escape tunnel is under a cow farm. And you're STILL GOING?"

"Rahul," Sahadeva said smoothly, pulling a large, fluffy pillow out of his closet and shoving it into his duffel bag. "We have spent the last three weeks dealing with Yudhishthira reading 'third-party alignment checks' over breakfast. We have dealt with Draupadi aggressively establishing complex corporate boundaries from hundreds of miles away. And Bhima is trying to force us to invest in his Tandoori-Momo franchise."

He paused, zipping the bag. He looked at me with an expression of pure, unadulterated yearning for peace. 

"If Vyasa wants to burn a house down so I get to spend six months hiding underground in peace, quiet, and glorious isolation, perfectly far away from any corporate synergies... I will hand Vyasa the match myself."

He smiled warmly, tapping my shoulder. "Pack some comfortable shoes, Rahul. We are going on a highly explosive holiday."

If only Yudhishthira involved highly flammable resorts, the House of Wax was going to test his compliance to standard fire safety codes. 

Another hilarious component of our return to Hastinapur—beyond Sahadeva’s popcorn taunting and the King’s bad joke—was how Bhishma attempted to re-establish combat protocols essentially minutes after our humiliated arrival. 

While Drona was busy achieving inner peace through twelve-year-old alcohol, Bhishma was trying to run emergency drills. 

"Gather in the courtyard!" Bhishma roared the morning after our return. He was wearing full plate armor at 6:00 AM, holding a massive broadsword. "We will analyze the failures of the Chakravyuh! Dushasana! Step forward!"

Dushasana stepped forward, rubbing his eyes, wearing his silk sleeping robes and carrying his morning tea. "Haa Pitama? Ee subah subah ka bawaal baa?" *(Yes Grand-uncle? What is this morning-morning chaos?)*

"Assume the combat stance of the Lotus Matrix!" Bhishma commanded. 

"Hum toh Lotus ke baare mein bas itna jaanab ki uke upar bhagwan baithat hain," Dushasana mumbled lazily, refusing to drop his tea. "Aur matrix-vatrix humre samajh se bahar baa, Pitama. Hum toh bas classical dance mein plié jaante hain." *(I only know about Lotus that gods sit on it. And matrix-vatrix is beyond my understanding, Grand-uncle. I only know plié in classical dance.)*

Bhishma’s face turned violently purple. "A warrior does not plié! A warrior strikes with the force of a thunderbolt! Show me your strike!"

Dushasana sighed, carefully set his teacup down, perfectly executed a classical Kathak spin, clicked his ankles together harmoniously resulting in a gentle jingle of his ghungroos—which he apparently slept in—and ended the maneuver with a graceful, extended arm point directed at Bhishma's nose. 

"Boop," Dushasana said happily. 

Bhishma stared at the finger pointed at his nose. His jaw worked silently for a moment. I could practically see his ancient, highly traditional brain refusing to process the sheer audacity of a boop. 

"I am entirely surrounded by idiots," Bhishma finally whispered into the early morning light. "Vyasa is right. We need to burn something down just to remind them what fear feels like."

Which directly leads us back to exactly why Vyasa and Bhishma were aggressively plotting in the archiving alcove, desperately trying to launch the Lakshagriha murder plot, and why my discovery of that plot via the incompetent Vidura was so profoundly chaotic.


The next morning, the grand courtyard of Hastinapur was a flurry of chaotic packing activity. 

The Pandavas were officially mandated by King Dhritarashtra—via the aggressive whispering of Bhishma and Sage Vyasa—to take an "indefinite recreational sabbatical" to a newly constructed holiday home in varnavata. 

"I don't understand," Yudhishthira complained, staring anxiously at the massive royal convoy being loaded with supplies. "A vacation? But I just scheduled a Q3 alignment review! We haven't even finalized the KPIs for the border patrols!"

"Brother," Nakula sighed, adjusting his perfectly styled hair in the reflection of a polished bronze shield. "For once in your life, embrace the leisure. I hear the air in Varnavata does wonders for the pores. And frankly, the lighting in the Hastinapur throne room has been washing me out completely."

Bhima, on the other hand, was not treating this as a relaxation retreat. He was treating it as a massive mobile catering operation. 

He was aggressively directing servants to load what looked like the entire Hastinapur grain reserve onto three separate chariots. 

"Oye aaram naal rakho oh paneer de dabbe!" Bhima yelled, pointing a massive finger at two terrified stable boys. "Garmi ch kharab nahi hone chahide! Aur oh mere special laddoos kithe gaye? Tainu pata hai na safar vich meri blood sugar drop ho jaandi hai? Rahul! Rahul kithe hai?!" *(Hey put those paneer boxes carefully! They shouldn't spoil in the heat! And where are my special laddoos? You know my blood sugar drops during travel! Where is Rahul?!)*

I walked over, carrying my extremely small cloth bag containing my extra t-shirt and my 2026 digital watch. "I'm right here, Bhima."

"Tu apna oh kaala paani pack kar leya?" Bhima asked urgently, looking around paranoid. "Bhim Cola? Khali nahi hona chahida safar te!" *(Did you pack your black water? Bhim Cola? It shouldn't get over on the journey!)*

"I brought three barrels of syrup," I assured him. "We just need carbonated spring water when we get there." 

"Aah," Bhima rubbed his massive belly happily. "Phool mauj karenge! Tandoori holidays!" *(We'll have full fun! Tandoori holidays!)*

If Bhima only knew that the holiday home itself was basically a giant tandoor oven made of lacquer and ghee, his excitement would probably take a much darker culinary turn. But I had promised Sahadeva I wouldn't ruin his mandated peace and quiet by inducing an early panic attack in the others. 

Across the courtyard, the Kauravas were also packing. 

Suyodhana, acting out Vyasa’s script perfectly, was aggressively making sure everyone knew how vastly superior his own vacation plans were. 

"Make sure the silk tents are packed!" Suyodhana yelled loudly, specifically making eye contact with Yudhishthira to maximize the gloating. "We are going to the Southern Mountains! It is an exclusive, invite-only luxury resort! We will be doing extreme wellness retreats and aggressive spa therapies! Not like some cheap, newly built cabin in the woods!"

"Ee pitama bhi chalat hain ka humre saath?" Dushasana asked, casually packing a massive wooden chest entirely filled with different types of dancing ghungroos. *(Is grand-uncle also coming with us?)*

"No!" Suyodhana snapped. "This is a team-building offsite for young executives only! No boomers allowed! And Dushasana, why are you packing three hundred bells?!"

"Music ka aawaz pahaado se takra ke waapas aawat hai bhaiya," Dushasana explained seriously. "Acoustics jabardast mili udhar!" *(The sound of music bounces off the mountains, brother. We will get excellent acoustics there!)*

I watched the two rival factions pack for their respective, highly dramatic vacations. The Kauravas were heading to extreme wellness spas to improve their polling numbers. The Pandavas were heading directly into an incredibly lethal arson plot secretly designed by the author of their own reality. 

And me? I was just a tired software engineer who had somehow survived an engagement to a mythological heroine, hacked a spinning maze of death, and was now voluntarily getting into a chariot to ride toward a giant flammable house because an overly depressed psychic told me the catering in the escape tunnel would be good. 

Sahadeva walked past me, carrying nothing but a small bag and his ancient sunglasses. He paused, adjusting the shades over his eyes despite the fact that it was early morning and slightly overcast. 

"Are you ready, Rahul?" Sahadeva asked smoothly. 

"I am actively participating in my own attempted murder," I sighed heavily. "But honestly, it's still better than attending another one of Dhritarashtra's comedy shows."

Sahadeva nodded Sagely. "That is the spirit. Come. I foresee a very bumpy ride, followed by a light BBQ, and a prolonged subterranean hike."

I climbed onto the chariot. I didn't look back at the palace. I didn't look at Vyasa, who was furiously scribbling notes from a high balcony, his eyes burning with dramatic anticipation. I just tied my seatbelt—which was just a strong vine I had forced the carpenters to install—and braced myself for the House of Wax. 

As the chariot lurched forward, joining the massive Pandava convoy heading out of Hastinapur, I couldn't help but laugh. It was a slightly hysterical, deeply broken laugh of a man who had entirely surrendered to the absurdity of the Bronze Age. 

We were effectively out of the frying pan. And we were driving directly into the fire.
