# Chapter 12: The Rajsooyi Pool Party and the Chedi Crisis

The state funeral for the Chief Technology Officer of the Rajsooyi Karyafe was an incredibly strange, highly surreal experience, mostly because I was currently standing in the third row of mourners, actively weeping at my own obituary. 

"He was an anomaly," Grand-sire Bheeshma intoned deeply, standing at the head of the Hastinapur royal court. The ancient patriarch didn't shed a single tear, his face entirely carved from stoic granite, entirely because he believed he had just successfully corrected a vital metaphysical error in the timeline. 

Beside him, hiding near the marble pillar, Sage Vyasa was practically vibrating with suppressed joy. The author of the epic had a large, highly self-satisfied smile plastered across his face, clearly believing his narrative was finally back on track. 

I, however, looked absolutely nothing like the CTO from Noida. 

Through the terrifyingly effective cosmetic talents of Vikarna and Shakuni, I had been completely physically rebranded. My face was heavily tanned using crushed walnut shells. I sported a massive, incredibly thick, and profoundly itchy fake mustache, coupled with an aggressive black beard that completely hid my jawline. I was wearing the heavy, dark green silk robes of a Kaurava prince, and a thick golden sequence-chain around my neck. 

I was no longer Rahul the startup founder. I was officially Prince Rahala, the 95th Kaurava brother, previously designated to the far northern taxation routes. 

Dronacharya, standing near the royal dais, looked genuinely saddened. 

"He was a confusing man," Guru Drona sighed heavily, adjusting his white robes. "He continuously asked me if I offered 'Agile Certification' instead of archery training. I never understood his methodology, but he possessed excellent organizational spreadsheets. I shall miss his quarterly reviews."

Even King Dhritarashtra looked genuinely disappointed by the empty casket, tapping his fingers against his throne. 

"I am deeply saddened by the loss of this operational anomaly," Dhritarashtra sighed formally. "His logistics were impeccable. I suppose his risk management team... simply didn't *see* the threat coming. It is a terrible loss for our quarterly projections."

Bheeshma winced at the terrible blind pun, but the King remained entirely unaware. 

"And now," Dhritarashtra commanded, pointing vaguely toward the crowd, "my chief advisor, Vidura, will read the formal communications we have received from our regional allies."

Vidura unrolled a massive slate, his eyes scanning the incredibly chaotic text. 

"Ahem," Vidura cleared his throat nervously. "These messages came via extremely fast courier. First, we have an official decree from King Drupad of Panchaal."

Vidura adjusted his glasses, squinting at the highly emotional script. "'I weep for the absolute tragedy of losing my favorite son-in-law for a second time, despite him never actually legally managing to become my son-in-law. Although he consistently rejected my daughter to protect the chronological integrity of the universe, he was truly the confused, terrified son I never had. May his terrible tie-dye shirts forever stain the heavens.'"

I nodded solemnly in the crowd. It was a beautiful tribute to a completely unrequited corporate arranged marriage. 

"We also have messages from Indraprastha," Vidura unrolled a second slate. "We begin with Prince Yudhishthira."

The court went silent as Vidura read, "’We formally acknowledge the termination of Rahul’s current biological contract. While his sudden departure from our operational ecosystem is highly tragic, we must ensure his legacy continues through relentless corporate expansion. Specifically, we will name our new seasonal iced-beverage in his honor.’"

Vyasa frowned. That didn't sound like a man ready to declare war over my death. That sounded like a man promoting a Frappuccino. 

"We also have a message from Prince Bhima," Vidura continued, looking deeply confused by the linguistic dialect on the page. He adjusted his glasses and attempted the aggressive Punjabi. "’Oye yaar! Sadda banda maar ditta kisse ne! Uski body kyu nahi labhi?! Anyways, rest in peace prah! Main tere hisse di lassi vi pi lavanga taan ki teri aatma nu shaanti miley!’" *(Oh friend! Someone killed our guy! Why didn't we find his body?! Anyways, rest in peace brother! I will drink your share of the lassi also so your soul gets peace!)*

I had to physically bite the inside of my incredibly itchy fake mustache to prevent myself from laughing out loud at Bhima's culturally accurate grieving process. 

Suyodhana stood up from the front row, looking deeply sympathetic, though mostly because he knew the court scribes were recording his reaction.

"We mourn this sudden loss," Suyodhana declared, placing a hand on his chest. "Rahul was a highly competent logistics manager. I will personally ensure our marketing budget reflects a momentary pause in aggressive regional advertising in his honor."

"Hum ka batayein, bhaiya!" Dushasana suddenly interjected, stepping forward and wiping a tear, his voice completely slipping into a heavy, highly aggressive Bihari accent out of pure emotional distress. "Eee Rahulwa toh humar dil jeet liya tha! Uski supply chain itni tagdi thi ki humka lagta tha eek din uuu Indraprastha ka PM ban jayega! Bahut bura hua, bhaiya! Hum toh litti chokha kha ke hi uski shanti ki prarthana karenge!" *(What do I say, brother! This Rahul won my heart! His supply chain was so strong I thought one day he would become the PM of Indraprastha! It's very bad, brother! I will eat litti chokha and pray for his peace!)*

The transition from deeply formal Sanskrit to aggressive Bihari regionalism completely baffled the Hastinapur elders, but it perfectly secured my official departure from the Kuru records. Rahul was dead. Rahala was officially born. 

Three days later, the massive Kaurava caravan finally departed Hastinapur, marching toward Indraprastha to formally accept the Pandavas' heavily politicized housewarming invitation. 

The journey took several days, and I seamlessly integrated into the massive, slightly chaotic traveling logistical mess that was ninety-nine brothers sharing fifty chariots. Nobody questioned who I was. 

"Brother!" a heavy hand pounded on my back as my chariot bounced over a particularly aggressive rut in the dirt road. 

I turned around. It was Ugra, the muscular Kaurava I had met in Vikarna’s chambers. He was chewing an entirely new piece of tree bark and looking extremely bewildered. 

"Hey," Ugra squinted at me beneath the hot Bronze Age sun. "Do... do I actually know you?"

"Of course you do, Ugra," I lied smoothly, aggressively adjusting my fake mustache. "We met in Vikarna's room last week? Brother Rahala? From the northern taxation routes?"

"Right! Yes!" Ugra lied instantly, highly defensive that he might have forgotten a legitimate sibling. "Absolutely! Northern routes! Lots of... snow! Say, didn't you promise me something?"

"A Caramel Macchiato," I provided helpfully. "When we reach the Royal Cafe."

"Yes! The Makki-Ato!" Ugra beamed, settling back into his seat. "The finest taxation beverage!"

Before I could continue educating Ugra on premium Italian coffee culture that didn't actually exist in Italy yet, a massive, heavily armored chariot pulled alongside ours. 

Crown Prince Suyodhana stood tall in the chariot, inspecting the logistical lines. He glanced over at our chariot, his eyes sweeping across Ugra and then landing directly on me. 

Suyodhana frowned. His gaze was far more penetrating than Ugra's. 

"Vikarna!" Suyodhana yelled across the roaring dust of the caravan to a nearby chariot. 

Vikarna instantly popped his head out, looking highly stressed. 

"Who is this man riding with Ugra?" Suyodhana demanded, pointing a jeweled finger at me. "Is he a new mercenary? His beard looks incredibly uneven, as if it was hastily glued on during a panic attack."

My blood essentially turned to ice. Suyodhana’s PR instincts were too sharp. 

"Ah! Brother Suyodhana!" Vikarna yelled back, smoothly executing the cover story without missing a single beat. "You remember Rahala, surely? He is sequentially brother number ninety-five. He just arrived back from the far northern routes. The intense mountain cold has deeply disturbed his facial follicles."

"Ninety-five?" Suyodhana muttered, rubbing his chin and squinting at me. "Wait, is ninety-five the one who aggressively hoards cheese, or the one who got lost in the Magadha forest for three years?"

"He is the one who optimizes the taxation grids!" Vikarna lied confidently. 

"Oh! The spreadsheet guy!" Suyodhana’s face cleared instantly, his corporate approval washing away all suspicion. He offered me a very brief, highly professional nod. "Excellent work on the Q3 returns in the northern valleys, Rahala. Try to fix your beard before the press interface in Indraprastha."

Suyodhana’s chariot sped ahead, completely satisfied. That was it. That was the entirety of the security check required to infiltrate the most highly guarded royal family in the ancient world. You just had to tell the Crown Prince that you optimized his spreadsheets. 

When the vast Hastinapur caravan finally crossed the borders into the massive, heavily modernized (for 3200 BCE) city of Indraprastha, our diplomatic protocol required us to make one incredibly stressful pitstop before heading out to the Chattarpur Farmhouse. 

We had to formally greet the regional landlord.

Takshaka sat on his throne, his cold, terrifyingly sharp eyes instantly locking onto the massive crowd of Kuru royals flooding into his hall. 

Suyodhana immediately seized the central floor, desperately eager to solidify his political alliance.

However, Takshaka’s gaze didn't stay on Suyodhana. It swept across the ninety-nine brothers behind him, and stopped with terrifying precision... directly on me. 

Takshaka didn't miss details. He had practically memorized the operational structure of the Pandavas. He analyzed my body language, the way I stood, the specific, exhausted corporate slouch that I constantly maintained. 

"And who," Takshaka hissed softly, his eyes narrowing to tiny slits, "is this fascinating new addition to your entourage, Crown Prince? The one with the incredibly thick, highly unconvincing mustache?"

"That is my nintey-fifth brother, Rahala!" Suyodhana declared proudly. "The greatest tax auditor in the northern hemisphere!"

"Is that so?" Takshaka leaned forward, a distinct, incredibly dangerous smile playing on his lips. "An auditor. Fascinating. He moves remarkably like a former... consultant."

"Your Majesty," I stepped forward swiftly, deepening my voice significantly and aggressively puffing out my chest. "I assure you, I am a pure Kshatriya warrior of Hastinapur! I have never consulted in my life! I only aggressively audit!"

"He is very passionate about spreadsheets," Vikarna supplied helpfully from beside me. 

Takshaka stared at me for five agonizing seconds. He knew. I was absolutely certain the Naga King knew exactly who was under the fake beard. But before Takshaka could completely detonate my cover and reveal the assassination fraud to Vyasa and the world...

"King Takshaka!" Suyodhana violently interrupted, deeply annoyed that he wasn't the absolute center of attention. He completely stepped between Takshaka and me. "We must finalize the campaign logistics! The regional Indraprastha elections are next year, and I require access to your southern advertising billboards! Our alliance practically demands synergistic messaging!"

Takshaka, instantly smelling the potential for highly lucrative real estate leasing contracts, immediately dropped his suspicion of my fake beard and pivoted his absolute attention to Suyodhana’s campaign budget. 

Vikarna and Shakuni quietly dragged me backward into the safety of the crowd. 

"That was too close," Vikarna whispered. "We need to get to the Farmhouse. Keep your head down, Rahala."

Bhima’s jaw slowly dropped. The massive tray of drinks in his hand actually began to tilt dangerously.

"Ahem!" 

A sharp, psychic jolt hit the back of my mind. Sahadeva suddenly materialized directly behind Bhima out of absolutely nowhere, aggressively grabbing his massive bicep. 

"Bhima," Sahadeva hissed perfectly through gritted teeth, maintaining a brilliant customer-service smile for the Kauravas. "Do not break the non-disclosure agreement. Do not reveal the corporate secrets. This is Prince Rahala. Serve him his dietary requirements immediately."

Bhima blinked rapidly, entirely failing to comprehend the complex chronological assassination plot, but completely terrified of Sahadeva’s psychic authority. 

"Right! Yes!" Bhima forced an incredibly aggressive, highly vibrating smile. "C-Coffee for Prince Rahala! Enjoy the beans, bro!"

He practically sprinted away before he could accidentally ruin the entire geopolitical landscape. 

The coffee was flawlessly executed, and after draining the company profits for lunch, the absolute chaos of the royal entourage finally mobilized toward the true objective: the sprawling, deeply forested estates of Chitrapur Road. 

The housewarming party was actively escalating toward mythological disaster.

The Chattarpur Farmhouse was an absolute architectural masterpiece. 

As the massive Hastinapur caravan pulled into the vast, expertly manicured courtyards of the newly acquired estate, even the deeply cynical Kauravas were genuinely stunned. It didn't look like a traditional mud-brick palace. It looked like a hyper-modern, excessively luxurious corporate retreat, complete with 'Luigi's' bizarre Italian marble flooring and highly impractical, flammable grass chandeliers. 

"Look at those structural columns!" Suyodhana gasped, completely abandoning all political subtlety. "The PR implications of this property are staggering! I could host the G20... I mean, the Regional Trading Summit here!"

As the royal delegations dismounted their chariots and began making their way toward the grand entrance hall for the inaugural Puja, a terrifying realization suddenly hit me. 

My heart hammered violently against my ribs. 

The Housewarming Party at Indraprastha. 
The incredibly expensive, highly confusing new architecture. 
The gathering of the Kauravas. 

*The Maya Mahal.*

I had casually forgotten the most crucial, devastating plot point of the entire epic. In the original script by Vyasa, this was the exact moment where the divine architect (or in our case, the Mathuran carpenter) had designed floors that looked like water, and water that looked like floors. 

Suyodhana was supposed to be completely confused by the optical illusion, mistakenly step into a massive pool of water, and completely humiliate himself. Draupadi was supposed to witness it and laugh, famously mocking him by saying, *"A blind man's son is also blind."*

It was the specific, deeply cruel insult that mathematically guaranteed the Kurukshetra war. 

And Vyasa was currently standing near the entrance, holding his manuscript, looking furious that nobody was fighting yet. The author was actively waiting for the trigger. 

"Vikarna!" I grabbed the Kaurava prince by his silk sash, aggressively yanking him backward. "The water! You have to stop Suyodhana!"

"What water?" Vikarna frowned, looking around the completely dry marble courtyard. 

"The structural illusions! The interior designer!" I looked wildly around. "Suyodhana is going to fall into a pool, and Draupadi is going to laugh at him, and it's going to destroy the entire timeline! I have to stick to him!"

Before Vikarna could stop me, I violently pushed my way through the massive crowd of brothers, actively dodging elbows and royal jewelry, until I was practically glued to Suyodhana’s side. 

"Ah! Rahala! Brother!" Suyodhana grinned, completely misinterpreting my aggressive proximity. "Are you analyzing the cost-per-square-foot metrics of this courtyard? I bet it's staggering!"

"Your Highness," I hissed, constantly scanning the floor for sudden, hidden pools. "You must step exactly where I step. The architectural geometry here is highly deceptive. It is entirely non-compliant with standard safety codes."

"I see!" Suyodhana nodded seriously, fully embracing the hazard warning. "Excellent risk mitigation, Rahala!"

For the next ten minutes, I violently micromanaged the Crown Prince of Hastinapur. Every time Suyodhana took a step toward a highly polished reflective surface, I aggressively hip-checked him entirely out of the way.

"Watch the glare!" I yelled, violently shoving him into a decorative fern. 

"I appreciate your dedication, Brother, but my ribs are bruising!" Suyodhana complained, actively attempting to navigate the complex hallways.

Draupadi was standing on the far balcony, observing the arriving guests, looking incredibly regal and terrifyingly corporate.

"Rahala, let go of me!" Suyodhana snapped, completely losing his patience. "I need to network with the Magadha delegation!"

Bhima, deeply distracted by a passing servant carrying a large tray of highly spiced samosas, was aggressively marching backward, trying to catch the servant's attention. 

"Oye! Samosa idhar la!" Bhima boomed loudly. *(Hey! Bring the samosa here!)*

Crown Prince Suyodhana.
