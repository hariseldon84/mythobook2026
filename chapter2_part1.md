# Chapter 2: The HR Department of Kurukshetra (Part 1)

My heart hammered against my ribcage like a trapped pigeon. Safely wedged in my timeline, Sahadeva’s wink would have been a quirky visual glitch. Here, in 3200 BCE, staring at a bleeding psychic who somehow clearly recognized the underpaid IT guy in the third row of the VIP box, it felt like my soul was actively trying to leave my body through my nose. 

"Did he just... wink at me?" I muttered, wiping a sudden sheen of cold sweat from my forehead. 

"Who, the little one bleeding in the dirt?" Vikarna, Third Son of Dhritarashtra and First Lord of Unearned Arrogance, took another idle pull from his pipe. He didn’t even look in Sahadeva’s direction. "Probably an involuntary muscle spasm derived from massive internal trauma. Honestly, Suyodhana doesn't know the meaning of 'proportional response.' Anyway, the dirt-sweeping intermission is over. Prepare yourself, peasant. The next bout is my personal favorite."

Down in the arena, royal healers had hastily carted Sahadeva’s bruised body away. In his place stood an absolute titan of a man, vibrating with the kind of intense, destructive energy typically reserved for runaway freight trains.  

Bhima. 

He had removed his silk upper garments to reveal a torso entirely composed of what looked like carved sandstone and raw muscle. He was pacing furiously with a massive golden mace resting casually on his shoulder. 

"Oye! Kithhe gaya oye Dushasana?!" Bhima roared, his voice thunderous enough to rattle the VIP balcony's wooden rafters. "Aaja bahaar, mitti de sher! Tainu main aisi phent lawaanga, teri maa tainu pehchaan ni paugi!" *(Where did you go, Dushasana?! Come out, you mud tiger! I'll beat you so badly your mother won't recognize you!)*

From the opposite tunnel, sauntering out with an unnerving amount of grace, came Dushasana. He wasn't nearly as bulked out as Bhima or his elder brother Duryodhana. In fact, Dushasana was surprisingly lean, clad in shimmering purple silks, and wearing silver anklets that genuinely, musically chimed when he walked. 

"Is he... is he wearing ghungroos?" I blurted out, momentarily distracted from my existential panic. 

"Ah, Dushasana," Vikarna chuckled softly, popping another ancient grape into his mouth. "The pride and joy of the Hastinapur Cultural Committee. What the historians won't tell you, my oddly-dressed friend, is that Dushasana is an un-defeated, four-time consecutive gold medalist in Kathak and advanced Bharatanatyam. He’s got the footwork of a caffeinated peacock."

"Wait, what?" 

"Watch and learn," Vikarna smirked. 

The conch blasted. Bhima didn't waste a single millisecond. He charged like a raging rhinoceros, swinging his weapon in a brutal, decapitating horizontal arc. "Waheguru di khalsa!" Bhima bellowed. 

But Dushasana wasn't there. 

With preternatural elegance, Dushasana spun exactly three hundred and sixty degrees on the ball of his left foot, performing a flawless pirouette. The massive mace whistled through the empty air, merely ruffling Dushasana's heavily conditioned hair. 

"E ka Bhaiya!" Dushasana taunted, his voice slipping into a thick, surprisingly melodious Bihari slang. "Hamar speedwa dekh ke darr gayil ka? Ee bhari macewa se hamar sutail baal na baaka hoyi!" *(What brother! Scared of my speed? Your heavy mace won't harm a single styled hair of mine!)*

Bhima stopped, blinking in profound confusion at empty space, before whipping around to find Dushasana standing five feet away, casually doing a complex mudra with his fingers. 

"Oye khoteya, khada reh ek jagah!" Bhima screamed, his face turning an alarming shade of beetroot. "Eh nach-gana ki laa rakheya aye?! Mardaan waangu lad!" *(You donkey, stand in one place! What is this song-and-dance you're doing?! Fight like a man!)*

"Arey Bhaiya," Dushasana laughed, doing a rapid, rhythmic sequence of foot taps (*Tat-Tat-Ta-Thei*). "Fightwa mein thoda lachak hona mangta hai! Aap toh aise hilat bani jaise bori mein aalu bhara ho!" *(Brother, you need some flexibility in a fight! You move like a sack filled with potatoes!)*

I stared in absolute disbelief. It was the most bizarre, visually jarring combat I had ever witnessed. It was essentially a professional Punjabi wrestler trying to swat a highly elusive, aggressively taunting Bihari ballet dancer. 

Bhima roared again, rushing forward and delivering a series of bone-crushing overhead strikes. *Smash! Smash! Smash!* Each blow shattered the packed dirt of the arena floor, sending mini-craters of sand flying into the air. 

Dushasana didn't even draw a weapon. He merely treated Bhima's murderous rampage as a complex choreographic challenge. He leaped, twirled, and backflipped, his silver anklets chiming in perfect synchronization to Bhima's missed swings. At one point, Dushasana actually used the shaft of Bhima's mace as a makeshift ballet barre to execute a perfect mid-air split. 

"Tensionwa mat li Bhaiya," Dushasana giggled, landing softly in a classic tribhanga posture. "Hamar footworkwa se toh Apsara log bhi jealous hovat hai! Aap toh khali pasina bahaat baani!" *(Don't take tension brother. Even the celestial Apsaras are jealous of my footwork! You're just unnecessarily sweating!)* 

"Teri dhi di...!" Bhima sputtered, too out of breath to even finish the Punjabi expletive. 

The crowd was eating it up. Half the stadium was laughing, clapping along to the rhythm of Dushasana’s anklets, while the other half was screaming for Bhima to just hit him once. 

"Fascinating," Vikarna drawled next to me. "It's like watching a bear try to catch a highly trained, deeply annoying butterfly."

Twenty minutes later, the inevitable reality of the Hastinapur royal diet set in. 

Bhima, panting heavily and sweating profusely, dropped the head of his mace to the ground. He leaned on the shaft, his massive chest heaving. "Bas," he gasped out. "Bas. Mera ghee khatam ho gaya. Tainu main kal dekhanga." *(Enough. Enough. My ghee is finished. I will see you tomorrow.)*

Dushasana gracefully bounded over, entirely unsweaty, and did a final, flourishing bow to the royal box. "Tohka bole rehna Bhaiya, hamar staminiya ka koi zor naahi! Draw maan lein?!" *(I told you brother, my stamina has no match! Should we call it a draw?!)*

Bhima just aggressively flipped him off, a universally timeless gesture. 

High up in the royal stands, the conch shell blew for a draw. 

From the royal balcony, Guru Dronacharya leaned over the railing, wildly waving his goblet. "I love you man!" Dronacharya slurred loudly at Dushasana. "But you're stepping on my imaginary pet iguana! Keep the noise down!"
"What my esteemed father means," Ashwatthama yelled defensively, trying to pry the goblet from the Guru's hands, "is that both combatants demonstrated excellent agility!"

Arjuna, sitting slightly ahead of me, buried his face in his beautifully manicured hands and groaned in deep, existential agony. "I told him," Arjuna hissed to himself, his perfect jaw clenching. "I specifically and explicitly told him: Skip the aloo parathas today. 'Just have a salad, Bhrata Bhima,' I said. 'Carbs make you sluggish,' I said. But no! 'Paratha is emotion, Arjuna!' he said. Now look at us. Our grand house, humiliated by a man wearing competitive dance jewelry."

Vikarna leaned forward, clapping a surprisingly comforting hand on Arjuna's tense shoulder. "Oh, loosen your quiver, O flawless one. Let the big guy have his fun. At least nobody lost a limb. Unlike the next match, which promises to be a bureaucratic nightmare."

The drums beat a slow, methodical, incredibly dull rhythm. 

"Next bout!" the royal herald screamed from below. "Prince Yudhishthira, the embodiment of Dharma, versus Prince Vikarna!"

Vikarna sighed heavily, meticulously placing his pipe down on a silver tray. He brushed an invisible speck of dust off his immaculate red silk dhoti. "Duty calls. Wish me luck, peasant."

"Wait, you're fighting?" I asked, suddenly realizing the lazy rich kid next to me was actually a participant. "Where is your weapon?"

"My weapon is a sheer lack of enthusiasm," Vikarna said, strolling out of the VIP box and down the wooden stairs. 

As Vikarna left, a massive, sweating shadow fell over my cushions. Bhima, having miraculously climbed the stairs after his exhausting dance-off, loudly dropped himself into Vikarna's recently vacated seat right beside Arjuna. 

Bhima turned to me, grinning widely. "Oye! Nice threads, foreign boy!" he boomed, offering a hand the size of a frying pan. "Very brave, fighting without armor. Blue is a good color on you!"

I tentatively reached out. Bhima shook my hand. I instantly heard roughly three knuckles pop out of their sockets. I swallowed a scream of agony, feeling confident my right hand was functionally fractured. "Th-thanks," I managed to whisper, cradling my crippled fingers against my chest.

On the sandy floor below, Vikarna emerged. He didn't carry a sword. He didn't carry a mace. He looked like he was walking to a very casual Sunday brunch. 

Opposite him stood Yudhishthira. The eldest Pandava. The man destined to be the great king of righteousness. 

Yudhishthira did not look like a warrior ready for combat. Instead, he carried what appeared to be an armful of heavy palm-leaf manuscripts. He dropped them onto the sand with a heavy thud, adjusted his simple, austere robes, and immediately pointed an accusatory finger at Vikarna. 

Up in the stands, Bhima snorted and nudged Arjuna with a massive elbow. "Look at him, Arjuna. Look at our eldest brother. Bringing homework to a deathmatch. I bet you ten gold coins he tries to cite a tax code to win."
Arjuna rubbed his temples. "Don't encourage him, Bhima. Last week he tried to put me in 'time-out' for an unethical arrow trajectory."

"Bhrata Vikarna! I declare a formal protest against these proceedings!" Yudhishthira announced loudly from the arena floor. His voice lacked Bhima's thunder, but it possessed the precise, cutting drone of a government tax auditor explaining why your deductions were fundamentally illegal. 

The stadium fell into a confused hush. 

Suddenly, King Dhritarashtra leaned forward on the royal balcony. "Why is there a delay? Oh, I know! Why did Yudhishthira refuse to cross the street?!" 
Absolute, terrifying silence. 
"Because he was waiting for the ethical committee's approval!" Dhritarashtra roared, slapping his knee. 
The guards leveled their spears. "LAUGH!" 
"HA HA HA! SO TRUE, O MAJESTIC KING!" the crowd screamed, weeping in fear.

Vikarna crossed his arms, looking intensely bored. "Is this about my lack of armor, Eldest Brother? Because it chafes my nipples, and I explicitly filed a medical exemption yesterday."

"No, this is about the profound, deeply embedded violations of the *Kuru Code of Tournaments and Ethical Exhibitions, Sub-Clause 14, Paragraph C!*" Yudhishthira countered, unrolling a massively long scroll. 

"Oh gods, he brought the rulebook," an aristocratic woman behind me groaned, burying her face in her hands. 

"The rules clearly state," Yudhishthira droned loudly, reading from the leaf, "that all participants must bow to the east before commencing a bout. You merely nodded towards the southeast! Furthermore, the ethical purity of the fighting ground must be maintained. And yet, I spy multiple violations of decorum simply by looking at the audience!"

Yudhishthira whipped around, his righteous gaze scanning the VIP boxes before locking—with horrifying precision—directly onto me. 

"For instance!" Yudhishthira shouted, jabbing a finger in my direction. "Behold that man in the third tier! He is wearing garments forged from a strange, stiff blue fabric that aggressively outlines the contours of his lower limbs! This is a severe violation of the *Hastinapur Modesty Protocol*! How can I concentrate on my chakras when faced with such profound indecency?!"

My face flushed so hot I thought my skin would melt off. An entire stadium of bronze-age warriors suddenly turned to stare at my slim-fit Levi's. 

"Hey, hold on a second!" Vikarna yelled from the ground, waving a dismissive hand at Yudhishthira. "Don't bring the peasant into this. His incredibly tight, blood-circulation-ruining blue pants are a bold fashion choice. It’s called *foreign avant-garde*, Yudhishthira. Look it up. Just because you dress like a monk who lost a bet doesn't mean you can gatekeep the VIP dress code!"

"I am not gatekeeping, I am enforcing Dharma!" Yudhishthira argued, clutching his pearls—or rather, his rudraksha beads. "Furthermore, Vikarna, your attitude constitutes a microaggression against my ethical sensitivities!"

"Your ethical sensitivities are annoying!" Vikarna retorted. 

To the side of the royal balcony, Guru Dronacharya let out a massive, wet burp that unfortunately made it into the magical stadium acoustic amplifier. 

"Eeeccch," Dronacharya slurred, rubbing his bleary eyes. He was holding a large, golden goblet that smelled distinctly of fermented grape mash. "Sssstop talking. Shush. Too many words. Not enough stabbing. You..." he pointed his wine goblet vaguely at Yudhishthira and Vikarna. "You both lose. No, wait. Draw. Yes, draw. Everyone’s a winner today. Can we move on? I have a headache the size of Mount Kailash."

"But Guru-ji!" Yudhishthira protested, mortified. "We haven't even crossed blades! The Dharma of combat—"

"Dharma is... taking a nap," Dronacharya declared, waving a dismissive hand. "Match over. Shoo. Get off my lawn- er, sand."

The massive drums played a deeply unenthusiastic *badum-tish* beat. The crowd groaned in unanimous disappointment, throwing half-eaten ancient snacks toward the fighting pit. Vikarna merely shrugged, gave Yudhishthira a smug little wave, and headed back up the tunnel. 

I sank lower into my seat, praying for invisibility. Yudhishthira glared at my jeans one final time before gathering his scrolls and storming off in righteous indignation. 

"Well," Vikarna sighed, dropping back onto the cushions next to me a minute later. "That was exhausting. Peasant, pass me those grapes."

Before I could hand him the fruit, the herald screamed the next announcement. 

"The pinnacle of archery! Prince Arjuna versus the esteemed Uncle, Shakuni of Gandhara!"

If Bhima’s entrance was thunderous, Arjuna’s was entirely cinematic. He descended a long, dramatic ramp, bathed perfectly in a shaft of localized sunlight. He carried the massive, legendary Gandiva bow. He looked serious, focused, and utterly lethally competent. 

From the shadows across the arena, Shakuni limped out. He was a thin, rat-like man with a perpetual sly grimace. He leaned heavily on an ornate walking stick and carried a standard-issue wooden bow as if checking a box on a mandatory compliance form. 

"Let the target be revealed!" Dronacharya slurred from the balcony. 

Far, far across the complex, practically outside the stadium itself, a small mechanical wooden bird was hoisted atop a massive pole. It was so far away it looked like a literal speck of dust. 

"The objective," Ashwatthama yelled, translating for his still-wobbly father, "is to pierce the eye of the wooden bird from this distance! A true test of precision!"

Arjuna stepped up to the crease in the sand. He nocked an arrow. The stadium fell dead silent. He drew the string back, his muscles coiling flawlessly. He closed his eyes. The concentration was palpable. 

"Foul!" 

Shakuni's nasal, grating voice shattered the silence like shattered glass. 

Arjuna froze. He slowly lowered his bow, staring incredulously at Shakuni. "What?"

Shakuni hobbled forward, wagging a bony finger. "Foul play, Prince Arjuna! According to the *Kurukshetra Regional Management HR Policy, Section 4, Sub-section A, Clause 12* regarding Work Environment Safety: An archer must maintain a minimum three-foot clearance between his elbow and any adjacent personnel! You are standing far too close to the royal sand-sweeper over there!"

Arjuna blinked. He looked to his left. A terrified servant holding a broom was standing about four feet away. "He's not even close to my elbow!" 

"It's a hostile work environment!" Shakuni declared loudly, ignoring him. "Furthermore, your bowstring is pulled back past your ear. This is an ergonomic hazard! I am filing an immediate grievance with the tournament safety committee!"

"This is an archery tournament, not a guild safety audit!" Arjuna yelled, his famous composure finally cracking. He raised his bow again, visibly fuming. "I am shooting the bird!"

He drew back. 

"Second foul!" Shakuni screamed. 

Arjuna’s arrow misfired, embedding itself harmlessly into the sand a hundred feet away. 

"What now?!" Arjuna bellowed. 

"You did not verbally declare your intentions!" Shakuni smiled a sickly, wicked smile. "Transparency in operational goals is a core pillar of Hastinapur corporate synergies. How do I know you were aiming for the bird and not trying to assassinate me? This creates a toxic culture of mistrust."

"I am going to murder you," Arjuna whispered, his eyes blazing. 

"Ah! Threatening a co-participant! That's an automatic disqualification pending a sensitivity training workshop!" Shakuni declared gleefully. 

Before Arjuna could actually resort to physical violence, Shakuni pulled out his own bow. He didn't look at the target miles away. He aimed towards a wooden post directly to his right—about fifteen feet away. 

*Twang.*

The arrow chunked ungracefully into the wood. 

Shakuni raised his hands in victory. "I declare myself the winner!"

"You didn't even shoot at the bird!" Arjuna was practically vibrating with rage. 

"Ah, but my young Prince," Shakuni countered smoothly, "we must analyze the metrics of 'success'. The bird is merely a metaphorical target. By striking this post, which is structurally integral to the safety of this stadium, I have demonstrated a far higher 'Proximity to Core Objectives' and 'Value-Added Impact' than your failed attempt at a distant, non-revenue-generating wooden avian."

"That doesn't even mean anything!" Arjuna screamed. 

"Look at the data, Arjuna! The data doesn't lie!" Shakuni cackled. 

Arjuna let out a primal scream of pure, undiluted frustration. He spun around, didn't bother planting his feet, didn't bother breathing, and savagely ripped his bowstring back. Without aiming, he fired. 

The arrow broke the sound barrier with a sonic crack. It flew a mile across the stadium, a blur of silver, and effortlessly, perfectly struck the wooden bird directly in the center of its tiny painted eye. 

The stadium erupted. It was a shot of impossible, god-like perfection. 

Arjuna breathed heavily, finally satisfied. He lowered his bow, waiting for Shakuni to concede the argument. 

But suddenly, the massive wooden gates of the arena blew open. 

Riding in on a gleaming golden chariot, flanked by cheering mercenaries, was a man whose entire existence seemed built to upstage Arjuna. He wore dazzling armor that literally fused with his skin. His earrings shone like miniaturized suns. 

Karna. 

Karna didn't even stop his chariot. As the horses charged violently across the sand, Karna casually stood up, drew his immense bow, and fired a single arrow. 

The arrow soared through the air, traveling the literal mile... and then somehow, impossibly, physics-breakingly *split* Arjuna’s arrow perfectly in half, burying itself deeper into the wooden bird's eye. 

The stadium went completely, utterly silent. The sheer impossibility of the shot broke the collective brains of twenty thousand people. 

Karna stopped his chariot in the center of the arena. He tossed his hair in the wind, crossed his arms, and struck a pose so outrageously cool it belonged on the cover of a men's fitness magazine. He stood perfectly still, closing his eyes, literally waiting for the applause to validate his existence. 

Shakuni, ignoring the pose entirely, limped up to Karna’s chariot. "Why are you even here?" Shakuni sneered. "This is a royal tournament, you glaringly shiny upstart."

Karna opened one eye, looking somewhat confused. "I... I actually don't know," he admitted earnestly. "But it has something to do with me being an archer. Whenever someone shoots an impossible target, I feel a cosmic compulsion to ride in and shoot it slightly better."

Arjuna’s renowned composure completely snapped. "Ok! Ok, you won!" Arjuna threw his hands up in furious frustration. "Hurray! Karna is a champion! Everyone say it after me! Are you happy now?!"

Karna just resumed his pose, turning his chin five degrees upward to catch the sunlight better. 

Up in our VIP box, Bhima leaned over to me and whispered loudly. "Look at him. The most egoistic guy on the entire subcontinent. He just wants awards in any tournament, even when he isn't invited! Last month he showed up and won the award for Best Cooking in the royal kitchens. Best Cooking! He is very irritating."

Arjuna groaned, burying his head in his hands again. 

Down below, Shakuni quietly walked over to the officials' desk, picked up the golden winner's trophy, shoved it under his arm, and casually limped out of the stadium. "Easiest KPI I've met all week," he muttered to himself. 

High above, Guru Dronacharya drunkenly fumbled for the balcony railing. "I shee... two birds," he slurred enthusiastically, waving his goblet. "And four arrows! Give the shiny man a participation certificate!"

Ashwatthama quickly pulled his father back. "What my esteemed father means," Ashwatthama yelled, sweating profusely, "is that both archers have demonstrated unparalleled mastery!"

"Is this entire time period just an orchestrated sitcom?" I whispered, cradling my fractured hand and rubbing my temples. 

Vikarna, having strolled back up from his 'draw' against Yudhishthira, casually reclaimed a spare seat next to me. 

"So, Karna," I muttered to Vikarna, trying to keep track of the rapidly growing cast of mythological characters. "Is he always like this?"

"I am *Vikarna*," the prince corrected strictly, glaring at me through the smoke of his pipe. "Never confuse me with Karna. Not even phonetically. Though I will say, my elder brother Duryodhana likes him because he actually gets stuff done. And he knows some... other secrets. But that's above your paygrade, peasant."

Vikarna sighed and packed away his clay pipe. "Now brace yourself. You haven't even seen my father try to do his closing stand-up comedy yet."
