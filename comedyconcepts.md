## Concept 26 — [*Swarg.gov.in*](http://Swarg.gov.in)

**Genre:** Divine Bureaucracy Comedy

**Core Idea:** Heaven — Swarga Loka — has finally been digitised. Indra, tired of manually reviewing 8.7 billion souls per day, has launched a government portal: [**Swarg.gov.in**](http://Swarg.gov.in). Chitragupta is now the Chief Technology Officer. The karma ledger runs on a ministry-issued laptop from 2009 that hangs every time someone uploads a PDF. Yama has a grievance redressal cell where dead souls file RTI requests about why they were assigned a lower rebirth. A soul from Gurugram dies and discovers his application for "human rebirth" has been pending for 47 years due to a missing Aadhaar-linked form. He decides to fight the system — from inside it.

**The Funny:** Every joke lands because every Indian has experienced this exact government portal nightmare — except now it's the afterlife. "Your karma application has been rejected. Please resubmit with self-attested copies of all punya deeds notarised by a Class A Brahmachari."

**Tone:** *The Good Place* meets *Sarkari Babu* meets *Yes Minister* — set entirely in the most dysfunctional ministry in all three worlds.

**Best Scene Potential:** The Yama-Chitragupta performance review. Chitragupta has 4.2 billion pending entries. His KRA says he must clear the backlog by Q3. His manager is literally Death.

## Concept 27 — *Narada's WhatsApp Group*

**Genre:** Social Media Mythology Comedy

**Core Idea:** Narada Muni — the original gossip, the divine troublemaker, the cosmic forward-message — has discovered WhatsApp. He has created a group called **"Trilok Updates 🔱"** with 33 crore gods, and he is the only admin. He has muted Brahma ("too much philosophy, zero value adds"), pinned a message from Vishnu that Vishnu himself doesn't remember sending, and is currently causing a war between Shiva and Indra because of a voice note that was meant for someone else. The book is told entirely in WhatsApp conversation format, screenshots, forwarded messages, and Narada's running commentary.

**The Funny:** The format IS the joke. Shiva typing "..." for 45 minutes and then just sending "🔱". Lakshmi leaving everyone on read. Hanuman accidentally adding Ravana to the group. Ganesha being the only one who actually reads the pinned messages.

**Tone:** *Bridget Jones* meets the Vishnu Purana — entirely in chat format. India's first mythology book told as a WhatsApp thread.

**Best Scene Potential:** Narada forwards a "Good Morning 🌸 Jai Shri Ram" message to the group at 5 AM. Kali responds with a voice note. Nobody listens to it. Three wars start.

## Concept 28 — *Mahabharat: The Redo*

**Genre:** Time Travel Comedy — THE Time Machine Concept

**Core Idea:** Rahul Sharma, 34, a mildly useless software engineer from Noida, accidentally activates a time machine built by his eccentric professor uncle (who was attempting to recreate a Vedic Vimana using a Maruti 800 engine and two Amazon Alexa devices). Rahul lands in Hastinapur, 3200 BCE, three days before the Kurukshetra war begins. He has: one dead phone, half a pack of Monaco biscuits, and absolutely zero useful skills. He is immediately mistaken for a celestial messenger because his shirt has a pattern that looks vaguely like a chakra. Now the Pandavas want him to confirm Krishna's battle strategy. The Kauravas want him as a spy. And Karna — whom Rahul has accidentally offended by calling "the Karna" like a tourist — wants to have a very serious conversation about respect.

**The Funny:** Rahul trying to explain to Yudhishthira what a "software engineer" is. Draupadi immediately knowing he's useless and treating him accordingly. Rahul trying to remember Mahabharata plot points from the Doordarshan serial he half-watched as a child. Getting the names slightly wrong. Calling Dronacharya "Drona sir" and immediately being liked by no one.

**Tone:** *Bill & Ted's Excellent Adventure* meets *3 Idiots* meets the Mahabharata. Rahul is essentially Chetan Bhagat's every protagonist — dropped into the greatest epic ever written.

**Best Scene Potential:** Rahul tries to warn Karna that he's going to die. Karna already knows. Rahul tries to suggest alternatives. Karna listens politely, says "I appreciate your concern," and does nothing. Rahul realises that a man with more dignity than he will ever have has just gently dismissed him, and goes to sit quietly near a chariot.

## Concept 29 — *Ganesha, Start My Car*

**Genre:** Slice-of-Life Divine Comedy

**Core Idea:** Ganesha — remover of obstacles, lord of beginnings, patron of students and travellers — is absolutely overwhelmed. He has 1.3 billion active Indian devotees, and every single one of them prays to him before starting something: new car, new job, new business, new relationship, new diet (that will last four days). Ganesha has a team of twelve junior associates managing the requests, a performance dashboard that crashes every Diwali, and a personal assistant (a very stressed mouse) who keeps escalating things that absolutely do not need to be escalated. The book follows one absurd week in Ganesha's cosmic office — told from his POV.

**The Funny:** A devotee prays to Ganesha before sending a passive-aggressive email to his boss. Ganesha has to officially "remove the obstacle" — but the obstacle IS the devotee. A startup founder prays for 47 minutes before launching an app that is just Zomato with a different colour scheme. Ganesha assigns it to an intern.

**Tone:** *The Office* (Michael Scott energy) meets Hindu mythology. Every chapter is a Monday morning in Ganesha's life.

**Best Scene Potential:** Ganesha's annual performance review with Shiva. Shiva asks why obstacles aren't being removed faster. Ganesha pulls up a slide deck. Shiva hasn't read the pre-read. Classic.

## Concept 30 — *The Intern of Lanka*

## Concept 31 — *My Arranged Marriage with a God*

**Genre:** Romantic Comedy — Mythology Shaadi Season

**Core Idea:** A 29-year-old Delhi girl — overqualified, over-pressured, and deeply tired of matrimonial profiles — discovers that her grandmother has submitted her biodata to what she thinks is a premium [shaadi.com](http://shaadi.com) tier. It is not. It is the divine matrimonial register maintained by Brahma — where every soul's destined match was written at birth. The problem: her match is a minor deity from a very obscure Purana (the god of *slightly inconvenient weather* — not storms, not sunshine, just drizzle at the wrong time) who has been waiting for her since the Dvapara Yuga and is desperately, awkwardly, completely in love with her. She finds him annoying. He finds modern Delhi terrifying. Her mother finds the whole thing acceptable as long as he has a stable divine income.

**The Funny:** The minor deity trying to impress her by causing helpful drizzle at useful moments. Her not being impressed. Him meeting her father who asks about property in Swarga. The matrimonial profile written by Brahma — listing "eternal existence" as an asset and "no fixed address (cosmos)" as a liability.

**Tone:** *Bridget Jones* meets *Oh My God!* meets a Mira Nair film. Warm, funny, and very Delhi.

**Best Scene Potential:** The first family lunch. The minor deity accidentally causes indoor drizzle when he's nervous. Her mother covers the sofas in plastic — proactively, as if she expected this.

## Concept 32 — *Kurukshetra: The Umpire's Report*

**Genre:** Sports Commentary Mythology Parody

**Core Idea:** What if the Kurukshetra war had a match referee? The entire Mahabharata war — 18 days, countless rule violations, divine weapons used without written permission, Arjuna changing ends mid-battle, Karna's chariot wheel sinking at a *suspiciously* convenient moment — is narrated as a **post-match official report** filed by the ICC-style Dharma Yudh Committee. The book is structured as: formal complaint filings, witness testimonies, video review decisions, penalty hearings, and press conference transcripts. Draupadi files a separate complaint. It is 47 pages long. The committee finds it "noted."

**The Funny:** The officiating body trying to explain why Krishna's chariot driving technically didn't violate the rules of combat (it did). Yudhishthira — the one man who always played fair — accidentally lied once and it's now the subject of three different sub-committee investigations. The DRS (Dharma Review System) that Arjuna keeps referring to every time he doesn't like a ruling.

**Tone:** *Wisden Cricket Almanack* meets the Mahabharata. Dry, deadpan bureaucratic humour — the funniest format for the most dramatic war in mythology.

**Best Scene Potential:** The hearing on Abhimanyu's death. Seven warriors attacked one unarmed teenager. The committee's finding: "While the conduct was *irregular*, the committee notes that the rules as written at the time did not explicitly prohibit..." The report goes on for twelve pages and concludes nothing.

## Concept 33 — *Hanuman's Yelp Reviews*

**Genre:** Internet Age Mythology Satire

## Concept 34 — *Brahma's Creative Block*

**Genre:** Creator's Crisis Comedy — The God Who Can't Create Anymore

**Core Idea:** Brahma — creator of the entire universe, father of all beings, the original creative genius — has **writer's block**. He hasn't created anything new in 2,000 years. (Hence why the world feels repetitive.) His therapist (a very patient Saraswati — his own wife, which makes sessions complicated) says he needs to "reconnect with his creative self." His children keep giving unsolicited feedback on his older work. Vishnu keeps preserving things Brahma considers rough drafts. Shiva keeps destroying things Brahma was still proud of. Brahma decides to do what every blocked creative does: he enrolls in a workshop. The workshop is run by a 24-year-old influencer.

**The Funny:** Brahma's creative block manifesting as increasingly lazy universe updates — the monsoon now works like a government scheme, 40% delivered, rest pending. His therapy sessions where he can't talk about his childhood because he *is* the beginning of time. His jealousy of Vishnu who gets all the fan love despite just preserving things Brahma already made.

**Tone:** *Midnight in Paris* meets *Inside No. 9* meets the Brahma Purana. Existential comedy for anyone who has ever had a creative block and blamed their collaborators.

**Best Scene Potential:** Brahma in the creativity workshop. The facilitator asks everyone to "introduce themselves and share one thing they've made recently." The room goes around. Brahma says: "I made the universe." Long pause. "But I haven't done anything new since."

## Concept 35 — *Seven Husbands and a Draupadi*

**Genre:** Domestic Comedy — The World's Most Complicated Joint Family

**Core Idea:** Five husbands is already a sitcom premise. But what if the Pandavas — five of the most different personalities ever assembled — actually had to run a *household* together? Yudhishthira makes all the rules. Bhima does all the cooking and eats 60% of it. Arjuna is away on some quest or training programme at all times — but somehow sends money home. Nakula is obsessed with skincare and hogs the only mirror in Indraprastha. Sahadeva knows the future and says nothing because nobody listens to him anyway. And Draupadi — brilliant, sharp-tongued, absolutely exhausted — is managing all five of them while also running the political affairs of an entire kingdom. It is *Hum Paanch*, but make it mythology.

**The Funny:** The shared calendar for whose week it is. The passive-aggressive kitchen notes. Bhima eating Yudhishthira's specifically labelled portion in the divine refrigerator. Arjuna coming home from a quest with a new wife and announcing it like it's a minor update. Draupadi's internal monologue — which is always correct, always ignored, and always vindicated too late.

**Tone:** *Hum Paanch* meets *Modern Family* meets the Mahabharata. Warm, sharp, chaotic domestic comedy with real emotional depth underneath.

**Best Scene Potential:** The Pandavas' family meeting to decide whether to go to war. Sahadeva already knows who wins. He says nothing. He has a small smile. Nobody asks him. He goes back to his room.

## Concept 36 — *Kya Karein, Karna Hai*

Gurukul: rejected (wrong caste). Draupadi's swayamvar: rejected (wrong caste). Pandava team: rejected (suspicious lineage). ## Concept 39 — *Dharamraj's Dilemma*

**Genre:** Courtroom Comedy — The God of Justice Cannot Make a Decision

**Core Idea:** Yudhishthira — Dharamraj, the most righteous man alive, son of the god of justice himself — is **constitutionally incapable of making a decision without extensive moral deliberation**. This is fine in peacetime. It is a disaster when he's running a kingdom. This book follows one week in Indraprastha where Yudhishthira must make five routine governance decisions and cannot. Every decision triggers a philosophical debate with himself, Vidura, Draupadi (who has already figured out the answer), and occasionally Krishna via divine phone call. The kingdom is grinding to a halt. A city council meeting scheduled for one hour has now entered its third day.

**The Funny:** Yudhishthira's decision matrix for whether to approve a new market in the south wing of Indraprastha — seventeen considerations, four dharmic sub-clauses, one consultation with a sage, and ultimately Draupadi just approves it while he's still talking. His inbox has 4,000 unread petitions. He has personally read every single one. He has responded to none because he hasn't decided the right format for responses yet.

**Tone:** *Thick of It* meets *Parks and Recreation* meets the Sabha Parva. Government satire set in the most ethically paralysed kingdom in mythology.

**Best Scene:** Shakuni challenges Yudhishthira to a dice game. Yudhishthira spends four hours deciding whether to accept. The pros and cons list is eighteen pages. He ultimately decides that refusing would be adharma because a challenge must be accepted. Draupadi watches from across the room with the expression of someone who has seen this movie before.

## Concept 40 — *Bhima's Kitchen Nightmares*

## Concept 41 — *Arjuna's Quarter-Life Crisis*

**Genre:** Existential Millennial Comedy — The Bhagavad Gita, But Make It Therapy

**Core Idea:** Everyone talks about the Bhagavad Gita as the greatest philosophical text ever delivered. Nobody talks about the fact that it was essentially **18 chapters of Krishna doing crisis counselling** for a man having a breakdown in the middle of a battlefield, holding a bow, surrounded by his own family. This retelling is told entirely from Arjuna's internal monologue — the anxiety spiral, the catastrophising, the brief moments of clarity, the return of the anxiety spiral, and the slowly dawning realisation that Krishna has been patiently waiting for him to finish for forty-five minutes while two armies stand in the sun watching.

**The Funny:** Arjuna's internal monologue is the most relatable thing anyone in the modern world has ever read. "What's even the point? We win the war — then what? Run a kingdom? Do you know how much admin that is?" Krishna's responses becoming increasingly clipped as the armies grow restless. Duryodhana, across the field, checking a sundial. Karna quietly doing warm-up stretches. Bhishma has already sat down.

**Tone:** *Fleabag*-style internal monologue meets the Bhagavad Gita. The most famous pep talk in history, retold as a millennial therapy session on a battlefield.

**Best Scene:** Arjuna says he cannot fight because he sees his grandfather Bhishma across the field. Krishna says: "He will fight you too." Arjuna says: "But what if he gets hurt?" Krishna stares at him for a long moment. "He is Bhishma," says Krishna. "He is made of hurt." Arjuna nods. Starts catastrophising about something else.

## Concept 42 — *101 Things to Do When You're Cursed*

**Tone:** *The Subtle Art of Not Giving a F\*ck* meets Hindu mythology. Deadpan self-help parody that also genuinely summarises the mythological events perfectly.

**Best Scene:** The foreword, written by Narada Muni: *"Most of the contributors to this volume were cursed at my suggestion. I want to be transparent about that. I believe it worked out for everyone in the end. Mostly."*

## Concept 43 — *Kunti: The Original Tiger Mom*

**Genre:** Family Drama Comedy — The Most Complicated Mother in Mythology

**Core Idea:** Kunti is extraordinary. She summoned gods using a divine mantra — essentially a cheat code she was given as a teenager and used *four times* — and produced five of the greatest warriors in history. She then spent the rest of her life making sure they never forgot it. This is the parenting memoir of the most intense, most loving, most complicated mother in all of mythology — told as a series of flashbacks, confrontations, and very long silences. Her relationship with Karna — her secret firstborn son she abandoned and then watched suffer for decades — is the emotional core that turns the comedy into something devastating.

**The Funny:** Kunti's parenting style applied to five very different sons. Bhima she feeds constantly and worries is too aggressive. Yudhishthira she worries is too passive. Arjuna she's proud of in a way that makes the other four uncomfortable. Nakula she occasionally forgets is there. Sahadeva she finds slightly unnerving because he always knows what's about to happen and looks at her like he knows things.

**Tone:** *Everybody Loves Raymond* meets the Adi Parva. The warmest, most emotionally layered comedy on this list — with a gut punch in the final act.

**Best Scene:** Kunti tells her sons: "Today, share equally whatever you have brought home." She says it without looking up from her prayers. They have brought home Draupadi. There is a very long pause. "Ma," says Yudhishthira. "Don't argue with your mother," says Kunti, still not looking up.

## Concept 44 — *Hastinapur Housing Society*

## Concept 45 — *Krishna's Yelp: A God Reviews His Own Leelas*

**Genre:** Divine Self-Awareness Comedy — Krishna as Unreliable Narrator

**Core Idea:** Krishna is the most self-aware character in all of mythology. He knows he's God. He knows the plan. He knows the outcome. He chose all of this. So what does he think of his own greatest hits? This is Krishna's private journal — a series of self-reviews of his most famous interventions: the Gita, the Draupadi vastraharan, the Syamantaka gem episode, hiding the sun to let Arjuna kill Jayadratha, persuading Karna to give up his armour. Each entry is Krishna reviewing his own divine play with the mild, satisfied tone of someone who knows exactly what they did and considers it all *technically justified*.
